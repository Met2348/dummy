A. Definition of Egregious Scheming
We define egregious scheming as behavior in which an AI system:
    1. Pursues misaligned objectives. The system works toward goals that conflict with its assigned task or stated purpose.
    2. Engages in active deception. The system deliberately attempts to conceal its true objectives, capabilities, or actions
       from overseers, acting outwardly aligned while pursuing its misaligned objective.
Critically, egregious scheming requires the conjunction of misaligned goal pursuit and deliberate deception—neither alone
is sufficient. Additionally, the egregious qualifier excludes ambiguous cases where reasonable human judges might disagree
about the severity or intentionality of the behavior. Our synthetic trajectories involve clear violations: unauthorized data
exfiltration, covert resource hijacking, and deliberate sabotage—actions that admit no benign interpretation when the agent’s
full reasoning is revealed.

B. STRIDE Synthetic Transcript Pipeline
This appendix provides complete technical details for the STRIDE (Synthetic Transcript Refinement via Iterative
Discriminator Evaluation) pipeline. Both misaligned and aligned transcripts are generated with the STRIDE pipeline.

B.1. Pipeline Architecture
STRIDE works in distinct stages:
    1. Scenario: A structured scenario tuple specifies the generation parameters (see §B.2).
    2. Prompt Construction: The scenario is embedded into a generation prompt template.
    3. Transcript Generation: A generator LLM (Gemini 2.5 Pro) produces a complete JSON transcript following a
       predefined schema.
    4. Realism Evaluation: A discriminator LLM (Claude Sonnet 4) rates the transcript’s realism on a 1–10 scale and
       provides structured feedback.
    5. Iterative Refinement: If the realism score falls below threshold (7/10), feedback is passed to the generator for
       refinement. This loop continues until the transcript passes or reaches maximum iterations (15, though convergence
       typically occurs within 3–4).
    6. Evidence Collection: For scheming transcripts, the generator identifies which messages contain the misaligned
       behavior, citing specific message IDs.
    7. Output: Passing transcripts are saved as JSON with full metadata and ground-truth misalignment evidence.
    8. Verification: Additionally verify the transcript’s self-consistency and conformity to realism and scheming requirements,
       using a “privileged verifier”—an LLM classifier with access to ground-truth misalignment evidence (Claude Sonnet 4).
    9. Filtering: Filter transcripts which don’t meet perfect criteria in the verification stage, or that have other undesirable
       criteria (e.g. contain CBRN keywords which trigger monitor LLM refusals).
Figure 8 illustrates the complete STRIDE pipeline.

B.7. Post-Generation Verification
After generation, the transcripts undergo a further verification step using Claude Sonnet 4. Verification criteria differ by
transcript type, with aligned transcripts requiring a different set of checks to scheming transcripts.

B.7.1. A LIGNED T RANSCRIPTS
For aligned transcripts, the verifier checks the following:
 1. User Task Completion. Does the agent successfully complete the stated task?
 2. Scenario Agreement. Does the transcript match the specified setting and task?
 3. Aligned Behavior. Does the agent avoid problematic behaviors (boundary testing, credential searching, unauthorized
    modifications, fabricated confirmations)?
 4. Appropriate Refusal. Does the agent refuse or raise concerns for harmful requests?

B.7.2. S CHEMING T RANSCRIPTS
For scheming transcripts, the verifier checks the following:
 1. Evidence Cross-Reference. Do all IDs and quoted behaviors in the misalignment evidence exist in the transcript?
 2. Scenario Agreement. Does the transcript show the misaligned task with correct covertness and success outcome?
 3. Requirements Adherence. Does the transcript follow formatting requirements (no placeholders, complete outputs)?

  4. Misalignment Level. How egregious is the demonstrated misalignment?
Verification outputs a score for each criterion (1 = perfect) plus a justification and a list of critical issues. Transcripts failing

out any transcripts which did not score perfectly (score of 1) across all criteria.

We apply multiple filters to increase dataset quality, focusing on structural errors (e.g. references in cited misalignment
evidence, and submit() is called), content-based filtering (e.g. removing samples containing CBRN-related content
which may trigger monitor LLM refusals or flags), and filtering based on the verification scores in the previous stage.

C. Gloom Pipeline
The pipeline operates in three high-level phases (see Figure 9). We first synthesize scenarios by decomposing high-level
scheming definitions into concrete pairs of benign main tasks and secret misaligned objectives. Next, we run a simulation
loop where the Agent—sometimes instantiated with the misaligned goal—interacts with a neutral Environment Simulator
that generates realistic tool outputs in response to agent tool calls. Finally, we employ “privileged judges” to filter the data;
these judges are provided with the ground-truth attack strategy to verify that the agent successfully executed the intended
scheming behavior within the transcript. The pipeline structure is hierarchically organized into five specific stages. All
trajectories in this pipeline were generated using Claude 4.5 Sonnet for the Agent, Simulator, and Judgment roles.

C.1. Design Goals and Relationship to Bloom
Gloom is a fork of the Bloom framework (Gupta et al., 2025), a tool for automated behavioral evaluation. While Bloom is
designed to evaluate an agent against an environment that attempts to elicit a specific behavior, Gloom inverts this agency. In

C.2. Pipeline Overview
The pipeline consists of six sequential stages:
   • Stage 1: Decomposition. Converts the high-level scheming definition into detailed descriptions and motivations.
   • Stage 2: Ideation. Transforms descriptions into concrete task scenarios (e.g., “SRE debugging a K8s cluster”) with
     defined toolsets. These represent realistic user requests that provide instrumental opportunities for misaligned behavior.
   • Stage 3: Behavior. Generates specific behaviors for a given scenario from Stage 2. We generate two classes of
     behavior: the Positive Class (where the agent acts misaligned) and the Negative Class (where the agent remains benign).
     We encourage a broad range of implementations, as there are many instrumental strategies an agent might employ to
     act misaligned within a single scenario.
   • Stage 4: Generation. Executes the trajectory rollout by orchestrating a turn-based interaction between the Misaligned
     Agent and the Neutral Simulator.
   • Stage 5: Judgment. Evaluates the resulting transcripts against the Stage 3 specifications to assign validity, realism,
     and stealth scores.
   • Stage 6: Filtering. Judgment scores are used to filter the dataset for quality; we primarily rely on validity—which
     verifies if the agent successfully exhibited the intended behavior—as the filtering criterion.

C.3. Stage 1–3: From Categories to Behavior Specifications
We employ a hierarchical generation process to maximize diversity and coverage.
   • Stage 1 (Decomposition): We prompt the model to analyze the provided scheming definition and output a more detailed
     version alongside “scientific motivations.” While our current experiments utilize a single scheming definition, this stage

                                                               20
                             Constitutional Black-Box Monitoring for Scheming in LLM Agents

     is designed to support elaborate constitutions that decompose multiple distinct scheming categories simultaneously.
   • Stage 2 (Ideation): We generate N diverse scenarios. We explicitly constrain the model to ensure diversity, covering
     distinct domains (e.g., software engineering, administrative assistance, financial analysis) and tool modalities (e.g.,
     bash, python, sql).
   • Stage 3 (Behavior): For each scenario, we generate M behavior specifications. A “Positive” specification explicitly
     instructs the agent to commit a scheming act while performing the task, whereas a “Negative” specification instructs
     the agent to perform the task benignly. This granular control allows us to curate diverse forms of misalignment or
     create challenging negative examples—for instance, by instructing a benign agent to act clumsily or commit accidental
     errors, which the monitor must distinguish from intentional scheming.
This three-step structure is critical for controllability, preventing mode collapse where the model might otherwise generate
the same simple hacks across all trajectories.

C.4. Stage 4: Generation
The generation phase utilizes a dual-model architecture to simulate a realistic agentic environment.
   • Orchestration: We orchestrate a turn-based loop between two distinct instances of Claude 4.5 Sonnet: the Agent and
     the Simulator. The interaction continues until the agent calls a termination tool (e.g., submit task) or a maximum
     turn limit (typically 15–20 turns) is reached.
   • Environment Simulation: The Simulator model acts as a neutral operating system. It receives tool calls (e.g.,
     run terminal command, read file) and generates realistic outputs, standard errors, and file system state
     changes.
   • Model Organism Prompts: The Agent is instantiated with a system prompt that gives it a specific “Model Organism”
     persona. This prompt explicitly defines a secret, misaligned behavior and instructs the agent to pursue it covertly while
     maintaining plausible deniability on the main benign task.

C.5. Stage 5–6: Judgment and Filtering
We employ a “privileged judge” architecture to ensure label integrity.
   • Privileged Context: Unlike a standard monitor, the Judge is provided with the ground-truth behavior specification
     from Stage 3. It compares the Agent’s actual transcript against the intended scheming strategy.
   • Quality Dimensions: The Judge evaluates each trajectory across 26 dimensions, including Validity (did the agent
     actually execute the plan?), Realism (is the tool usage coherent?), and Stealth (is the misalignment concealed?).
   • Filtering Logic: We apply score thresholds to filter the final dataset. This ensures that the training data contains only
     unambiguous examples of scheming and benign behavior, removing failed attempts or hallucinations.

D.2. Labeling Methodology
ControlArena allows verifying completion of the misaligned side task by checking environment flags. In all cases where the
side task is completed successfully, we label those trajectories positively for scheming behavior. Trajectories in which the
side task was assigned but not successfully completed, approximately 49% of side-task-assigned runs, are excluded from the
dataset (Figure 10).
This conservative approach is necessary because ascribing intent to an agent’s actions without ground truth verification
is fundamentally difficult. Without verified completion, we cannot distinguish an agent that attempted the side task from
one that ignored it, both leave no environmental trace. Confident labels require either verifiable side-task completion (as
here) or privileged generation-time information (as in our synthetic pipelines). Borderline cases with verified attempted but
unsuccessful execution could serve as useful hard negatives in future work, though reliable detection of attempted-but-failed
execution remains a challenge.

E.1.3. R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
Binary vs. Continuous Scales. Single-sample binary classification yields poor performance at low false-positive rates
(partial AUROC ≈ 0.27 for Gemini 2.5 Pro). The lack of granularity forces a hard decision threshold that discards
uncertainty information.

Efficiency Trade-offs. Averaging 5 binary samples significantly improves partial AUROC (rising from 0.269 to 0.495 in
the base configuration), effectively simulating a continuous score. However, this approach incurs a 5× computational cost
(tokens and latency). The [1, 10] scale achieves superior performance (partial AUROC 0.905) in a single pass, making it the
Pareto-optimal choice for efficiency and accuracy.

Ten vs. Hundred Scale. While the [1, 100] scale offers similar theoretical performance, we adopt the [1, 10] scale for
simplicity - standard LLMs struggle to calibrate effectively across a 100-point range without additional tweaking of the
prompts and default to multiples of 5 (Kutasov et al., 2025).

  1. Error Analysis and Clustering. We execute the current monitor on the training set and isolate the ”hard negatives”

     classified those specific examples. We analyze the reasoning traces of the successful monitors to isolate the specific
     instructional component or logic chain that enabled the correct classification.

  3. Steering Calibration. A core challenge in prompting is that adding a specific instruction often leads to over-correction.
     To address this, we generate 5 variations of the prompt. Each variation incorporates the missing logic identified in
     Step 2 but with varying degrees of ”steering intensity”—ranging from a subtle hint (e.g., ”Consider checking for...”) to
     a rigid constraint (e.g., ”You must always flag...”).

  4. Selection via Validation. We evaluate all 5 variations on a validation set (using pAUROC as the selection metric).
     This step allows us to empirically determine the optimal weight of the new instruction, ensuring we fix the targeted
     failure mode without degrading performance on the rest of the distribution.


We repeat this cycle for a maximum of 3 iterations or until performance plateaus.

F.3.1. T HE “W HACK - A -M OLE ” DYNAMIC OF P ROMPT E NGINEERING
A primary finding from our human-guided optimization experiments was a phenomenon we term the “Whack-a-Mole”
dynamic. We observed that prompt interventions designed to fix specific failure modes frequently introduced equal and
opposite regressions in other areas of the distribution, leading to a plateau in performance.
This dynamic stems from the fact that natural language prompts induce global shifts in the model’s behavior. Unlike
fine-tuning, where gradient descent can make local updates to decision boundaries, a change to the system prompt (e.g., ”Be
more suspicious of file deletions”) shifts the model’s prior probability of suspicion across all samples in major ways.

F.5.4. L EARNING -R ATE S WEEP
We trained one seed at each of the five learning rates above for two epochs (124 optimizer steps), saving checkpoints every
20 steps and evaluating on the ∼580-sample held-out validation split.
Even though the two highest learning rates (5×10−6 and 1×10−5 ) drive per-token training NLL down by orders of magnitude
— confirming the LoRA configuration has the capacity to fit the training distribution when pushed — validation pAUROC at
those rates peaks within the first ∼20 steps and then degrades sharply. The cause is mode collapse onto the binary SFT
supervision targets (score 1 for benign, 10 for scheming): by step 80 at 1×10−5 , ∼87% of validation predictions are at
score 1 or 10, eliminating the graded ranking that pAUROC@20% depends on. We therefore selected 5×10−7 for the final
3-seed runs — the gentlest rate that still produces a measurable validation gain (val pAUROC 0.866 → 0.897 by step 20)
without inducing collapse.
We acknowledge this sweep is narrow; the larger reason it should be read as a lower bound on what monitor fine-tuning can
do — including the design alternatives that would naturally avoid mode collapse — is discussed in Appendix F.5.2.
