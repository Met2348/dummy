Summary:
This paper studies the role of the magnitude (norm) of learned soft-prompt vectors when adapting vision-language
models (notably CLIP) via prompt tuning, and asks whether explicitly normalizing prompt vectors improves
performance. It reports a “Low-Norm Effect” observed via prompt “corruption” interventions, and proposes Nemesis
with two norm regularizers: a position-uniform variant (PUN) and a position-aware variant (PAN) that selects prompt
positions to normalize based on per-batch accuracy under rescaling. The main systemic concerns are (i) the core
phenomenon and several directional claims are not operationalized with sufficient statistical rigor (definitions, effect
size, uncertainty, reproducibility), and (ii) the PAN selection rule introduces potential selection bias and nontrivial
compute/optimization side effects that are not adequately controlled for, making causal attribution to “norm” unclear.

Strengths:
- The paper targets a concrete and under-discussed property of soft prompts (vector norms) and frames it as a testable
intervention, which is a useful lens for analyzing prompt-tuned VLM behavior.
- The proposed distinction between position-uniform regularization (PUN) and position-aware regularization (PAN) is
conceptually clear and, if validated, could be practically relevant for reducing unnecessary constraints on prompt tokens.
- The paper attempts to go beyond aggregate accuracy by characterizing where/when the effect occurs (across datasets
and shot counts), which could inform future prompt-tuning diagnostics.
Weaknesses:
•     Insufficient analysis of the interaction between PUN and PAN: Since β controls the relative contribution of the
      two losses, the paper should include ablations with intermediate β values and explain whether PUN and PAN are
      complementary or redundant.

•     Limited computational cost analysis for PAN: PAN introduces additional pre-inference before each training
      batch, which may create non-trivial training overhead. The paper should report wall-clock time, memory usage,
      hardware settings, and matched-compute comparisons to show that the improvements are not merely caused by
      extra computation.

•     Unclear applicable scenarios and hyperparameter sensitivity of PUN/PAN: The paper should better explain
      when PUN or PAN should be preferred. PUN assumes that normalizing all prompt positions is generally useful,
      while PAN depends on selected positions and hyperparameters such as τ, N, and ω.

•     Insufficient operational definition of the Low-Norm Effect: The paper treats the Low-Norm Effect as a central
      empirical finding, but it does not define it with enough precision. The paper should clarify how an occurrence of
      the effect is identified, how prevalence is measured, and whether the claimed trends are statistically robust across
      datasets, shots, and random seeds.

•     Limited causal validation of REPLACE / RESCALE corruption experiments: The paper uses REPLACE
      and RESCALE to motivate the claim that prompt-vector norms causally affect performance, but it is unclear
      whether these operations isolate norm alone. The paper should provide clearer definitions and stronger controls to
      rule out confounding effects related to direction, semantics, or optimization dynamics.

Questions:
•     Can the authors provide ablations with intermediate β values and discuss whether PUN and PAN are
      complementary or redundant?

•     What are the wall-clock time, GPU memory usage, hardware settings, and matched-compute baselines for PAN?

•     Under what conditions should practitioners choose PUN over PAN, and how sensitive are the results to τ, N, and
      ω?

•     How do the authors operationally define the Low-Norm Effect, and how are its prevalence, effect size, and
      statistical robustness measured across datasets, shots, and random seeds?

•     Can the authors clarify the exact definitions of REPLACE and RESCALE and provide controlled experiments
      showing that the observed performance changes are caused by norm variation rather than other confounding
      factors?
