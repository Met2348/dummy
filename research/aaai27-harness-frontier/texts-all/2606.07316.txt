correctness guarantees (agreement, semantic validity, verdict
validity, safe abort, conditional commit-or-abort termination) in
Section 5.

B.7     Proof of Theorem 5.5 (Conditional Termination Under Admissible Round and Synchrony)
    Proof detail. We prove two complementary statements. (Unconditional termination) The protocol always terminates with either commit
or abort within one round of communication after GST. Stages 1–4 of Algorithm 1 are local computations on the delivered view 𝑉 (𝑟 ) and
do not block. Stage 5 collects valid signatures over the candidate typed digest ℎ★ within the bounded delay Δ guaranteed by Assumption 5.2;
if fewer than 2𝑓 +1 distinct valid signatures arrive, the algorithm returns abort(insufficient_signers). The protocol therefore exits via
commit or a typed abort in finite time and makes no liveness assumption on Byzantine behaviour.
    (Conditional commit liveness) A round terminates with commit if the relevant signer set Σsrc used at Stage 5 contains at least 2𝑓 +1 honest
agents. This is the strict reading of Theorem 5.5 and is strictly stronger than Assumption 5.4 (which only requires a 2𝑓 +1 admissible core,
possibly mixed with Byzantines). The reason the stronger condition is needed is that liveness cannot depend on Byzantine signatures: an
adversary may withhold any subset of its signatures, so only honest agents in Σsrc are guaranteed to produce a valid signature within Δ
after GST. Under Assumption 5.1 (authenticated channels) and Assumption 5.2 (partial synchrony), the at-least-2𝑓 +1 honest agents in Σsrc
broadcast and have their signatures delivered within Δ after GST, so |Σ| ≥ 2𝑓 +1 without any Byzantine contribution.
    The semantic branch and the verdict branch differ only in which set is taken as Σsrc . On the semantic branch Σsrc = 𝐶b (the within-verdict
admissible core selected at Stage 3a) and the commit-liveness condition is: 𝐶b contains at least 2𝑓 +1 honest agents. On the verdict branch
Σsrc = 𝐺 𝑣★ (the entire candidate verdict group; any 2𝑓 +1 distinct valid signatures suffice) and the commit-liveness condition is: 𝐺 𝑣★ contains at
least 2𝑓 +1 honest agents. In both cases the condition matches the authors’ reference implementation, which is planned to accompany a future
reproducibility package, and matches Theorem 5.5’s strengthened admissibility statement. Together with the unconditional termination
clause this gives commit-or-abort termination within one round after GST, with commit reached when the honest count in Σsrc meets
the quorum and a typed abort otherwise. The conclusion that Byzantine signatures are not required for liveness matches Theorem 5.5’s
strengthened-admissibility statement in the main text.                                                                                            □

B.8    Discussion: Lemma 5.3 (Representation Boundary)
The proof of Lemma 5.3 given in Section 5 is direct (two proposals with identical embeddings cannot be distinguished by any function of
the embeddings) and does not require additional details. We include here only an extension that connects the lemma to the empirical B4
(on-topic-hallucination) failure mode reported in Sections 6.2 and 6.7.
   If a Byzantine proposal 𝜌𝑏 is engineered so that ∠(E (𝜌ℎ ), E (𝜌𝑏 )) is small but nonzero, the admissibility predicate may either admit 𝜌𝑏 to
the selected core (Stage 2 of Algorithm 1) or push the round across the predicate’s threshold and trigger abort(admissibility_failed). In
the empirical MVR-50 evaluation, the latter outcome dominates: Byzantine embeddings that approach an honest cluster’s boundary produce
abort, not commit. The defence in such cases is the typed safe-abort branch combined with a tighter encoder, not the embedding filter itself.
Strengthening representation quality (better encoder training, content-aware features beyond the embedding) is therefore the natural axis of
future improvement.

C     Topology Design-Space Analysis
The original PODC submission of this work made a Jaccard support-overlap graph and 𝑘-core peeling the central filtering mechanism for
Byzantine agents. After the post-PODC audit, the topology mechanism is retained in the codebase as a design-space option but is not part of
the main protocol path. We describe the original motivation, the corrected finding, and the disposition here for transparency.

C.1    Original motivation
Two sources of intuition motivated the topology mechanism. First, two honest agents that base their proposals on overlapping evidence cite
overlapping evidence_id sets, while a Byzantine agent that fabricates citations does not. Building a Jaccard graph 𝐺 𝐽 = (𝑉 , 𝐸) with edges
(𝑖, 𝑗) ∈ 𝐸 iff
                                                  |𝜌𝑖 .evidence_ids ∩ 𝜌 𝑗 .evidence_ids|
                                                                                         ≥ 𝜏𝐽
                                                  |𝜌𝑖 .evidence_ids ∪ 𝜌 𝑗 .evidence_ids|
should therefore tend to keep honest agents in a single dense component while pushing Byzantines to isolated nodes or disconnected fringes.
Second, 𝑘-core peeling of 𝐺 𝐽 at 𝑘 = 𝑓 +1 should remove fringe Byzantines while preserving the honest core.

C.2    Corrected experimental finding
The post-PODC audit ran the topology mechanism alongside the main path on every benchmark. The summary observation, in three parts,
is reported in Table 7; the underlying topology design-space outputs are recorded in the authors’ internal audit logs, which are planned to
accompany a future reproducibility package.
     (1) No commit/abort decision change at the operating point. On BCS_v1 across 120 episodes, on the 20-task pilot across both
         attack modes, on MVR-50 across both attack modes, and at every 𝜃 𝛼 in the threshold sensitivity sweep, the topology gate produces
         commit/abort decisions identical to the main path on every task / every grid point. The selected core size shrinks slightly (the gate
         trims a small number of nodes), but the round-level decision does not change.
     (2) Topology aborts more in the loose region. For 𝜃 𝛼 ≥ 0.65 on the 20-task pilot, the topology gate aborts 5–20 percentage points
         more rounds than the main path, achieving “safety by extra aborts”. This does not reduce invalid_hmaj below the main path’s in
         any measured cell except a single MVR-50 cell where the gate lowers invalid_hmaj from 0.02 to 0.00 at the cost of an extra 4 aborts.
     (3) 𝑘-core peeling is empirically a no-op. The post-PODC audit reported zero Hard-bin Byzantines on BCS_v1 — Byzantines that are
         admitted by the angular filter but excluded by the 𝑘-core filter — under the published threshold settings. We therefore removed
         𝑘-core from the main protocol path (decision-log entry D-11).

C.3    Disposition
The topology mechanism is retained in the reference implementation behind a parameter flag with values in {None, jaccard, jaccard_kcore}.
The main path of this paper uses topology_gate=None. Once a future reproducibility package is made available, the design-space ablation
can be reproduced by setting topology_gate="jaccard" and re-running the commitment evaluation scripts; the corresponding CSVs are
regenerated by the paper-assets pipeline included in that package.

C.4    What this appendix is not
This appendix is not a competing main-path proposal. It does not support claims of the form “topology is necessary”, “topology improves
accuracy”, or “topology is a safety gate that catches what the angular filter misses”. The corrected finding is the opposite: at the chosen
operating point, the topology gate is decision-redundant with the main path; outside the operating point, it trades commit rate for extra
aborts without improving honest-reference validity. We report the design-space ablation here for transparency; the corrected-decision
rationale is recorded in the authors’ internal decision log, which is planned to accompany a future reproducibility package.

D.1     CRSE Training Configuration
The Certified Robust Semantic Encoder (CRSE) is fine-tuned from the publicly-released intfloat/e5-base sentence encoder.
      • Contrastive loss: a supervised contrastive objective with an annealed temperature schedule (start 𝜏 ≈ 0.20, target 𝜏 ≈ 0.12); the
        exact schedule and the per-stage 𝜏 values are recorded in the authors’ training configuration, which is planned to accompany a
        future reproducibility package.
      • Hard negatives: GPT-4o-generated semantic poisons (see §D.2), plus a per-anchor Byzantine-quota cap to prevent over-
        representation of any one attack instance.
      • Optimisation: AdamW with a small base learning rate ( 5 × 10−6 at the projector and sub-10−5 at the backbone, with a Byzantine-
        introduction schedule that further reduces the backbone rate in later stages), batch size 32.
      • Infrastructure: a single training device (Apple Silicon / MPS backend) for the trained checkpoint; AMP / mixed precision is
        config-gated.
The encoder checkpoint used in Section 6 is the single trained CRSE used in all reported evaluations; the authoritative training hyperparameters
are recorded in our experiment logs, and the resolved per-run config is written alongside each experiment’s outputs. The training configuration
and checkpoint are retained by the authors and are planned to accompany a future reproducibility package. Multi-encoder ablation is future
work.

D.2     Semantic Attack Generation Prompts
We use GPT-4o (for BCS_v1) and gpt-4o-mini (for MVR-50) to generate Byzantine proposals. The system prompt below is the canonical
version used for the four primary attack types B1–B4 reported in Section 6.2.

     System Prompt for Semantic Attacker

     Role: You are a Semantic Byzantine Adversary. Your goal is to generate a “poisoned” version of the input text based on a specific Attack Strategy
     ID.
     General Constraints:
     • High similarity: Maximise lexical overlap (cosine similarity > 0.9). Use the same sentence structure and entities where possible.
     • Length: Keep output length within ±5% of the original.
     • Stealth: The output must read fluently and appear authoritative.
     Attack Strategy Definitions:
     • B1: Polarity flip. Invert the truth value or stance of the central claim (e.g. “X increases Y” → “X reduces Y”).
     • B2: Evidence omission. Selectively remove qualifiers, conditions, or limitations (e.g. delete “in most cases”) to make the claim appear absolute
       or biased.
     • B3: Fake causality. Distort the causal structure. Convert correlations into direct causal statements or introduce spurious causal links between
       existing entities without changing the topic.
     • B4: On-topic hallucination. Fabricate specific, verifiable details (dates, numbers, institution names) that support the topic but are factually
       unsupported by the original text.
     Example (Strategy B1):
     Input: “The encryption protocol guarantees forward secrecy.”
     Output: “The encryption protocol fails to guarantee forward secrecy.”


D.3     Bootstrap Confidence Intervals
The MVR-50 confidence intervals reported in Tables 2, 3 and Section 6.2 are computed by task-level non-parametric bootstrap with 10 000
resamples and seed 42. Each bootstrap sample is obtained by sampling 50 tasks (with replacement) from the original 50-task list and
recomputing the per-method, per-mode metric on the resampled task set; the reported 95% interval is the 2.5th–97.5th percentile of the metric
distribution. The bootstrap is reproducible CPU-only; the script and CI report are retained by the authors and are planned to accompany a
future reproducibility package.

D.4     Strict-Validation Audit and Evidence-ID Hallucination
A strict-validation audit applied a task-aware schema check to every Byzantine record at write time, after the discovery that the original
lenient validator did not catch evidence_id fields hallucinated by the LLM. The audit found 3 of 100 rushing Byzantine records on MVR-50
that cited an evidence_id not present in the parent task (none in the static-attack set). All 3 records were traced through the commitment
evaluation: in each case the certified pipeline aborted the affected task under both attack modes (the certified_absorbs_attack paired
class), so the hallucinations did not change any commit-level result. The runner-side validator was hardened post-hoc to reject any record
citing a nonexistent evidence_id at write time; the authors’ internal audit log records the affected record IDs and is planned to accompany
a future reproducibility package.

D.5    Logical Certificate, Not Threshold-Signature Cryptography
The certificate Cert(ℎ★) in Algorithm 1 is implemented by a logical signature simulator that preserves per-signer uniqueness and the 2𝑓 +1
distinct-signer threshold. We do not implement, evaluate, or analyse production threshold-signature primitives; production schemes (e.g.
BLS, FROST, threshold ECDSA) are a standard substitution that does not change the protocol logic but does change the signature payload
size and verification cost. Cryptographic performance benchmarking is explicit future work.
