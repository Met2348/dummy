                                                Abstract. Business plan (BP) writing plays a key role in entrepreneur-
                                                ship education by helping learners construct, evaluate, and iteratively
                                                refine their ideas. However, conventional BP writing remains a rigid, lin-
                                                ear process that often fails to reflect the dynamic and recursive nature
                                                of entrepreneurial ideation. This mismatch is particularly challenging for
                                                novice entrepreneurial students, who struggle with the substantial cog-
                                                nitive demands of developing and refining ideas. While reflection and
                                                meta-reflection are critical strategies for fostering divergent and conver-
                                                gent thinking, existing writing tools rarely scaffold these higher-order
                                                processes. To address this gap, we present the Meflex System, a large
                                                language model (LLM)-based writing tool that integrates BP writing
                                                scaffolding with a nonlinear idea canvas to support iterative ideation
                                                through reflection and meta-reflection. We report findings from an ex-
                                                ploratory user study with 30 participants that examined the system’s
                                                usability and cognitive impact. Results show that Meflex effectively scaf-
                                                folds BP writing, promotes divergent thinking through LLM-supported
                                                reflection, and enhances meta-reflective awareness while reducing cog-
                                                nitive load during complex idea development. These findings highlight
                                                the potential of non-linear LLM-based writing tools to foster deeper and
                                                coherent entrepreneurial thinking.

                                         1     INTRODUCTION

                                         Business plan (BP) writing is a critical component of entrepreneurship education.
                                         While traditionally aimed at presenting business ideas to investors [14], BP writ-
                                         ing is increasingly seen in educational contexts as a pedagogical scaffolding that
                                         helps learners construct, evaluate, and iteratively refine their entrepreneurial
                                         ideas [13, 25]. Despite this pedagogical potential, BP writing remains a rigid,
                                         linear process centered on a single idea [30, 22]. This stands in contrast to en-
                                         trepreneurial ideation which is inherently dynamic and iterative [32]. It involves
2       Lan Luo, Dongyijie Primo Pan, Junhua Zhu, Muzhi Zhou, and Pan Hui

continuously revisiting initial abstract ideas through core business plan com-
ponents such as problem identification, pain-point analysis and so on [1]. The
substantial cognitive demands of iterative ideation often hinder idea development
and opportunity recognition among novice entrepreneurial students [16].


    Reflection is a promising cognitive strategy for supporting entrepreneurial
ideation through divergent thinking [51]. By prompting learners to critically
examine their assumptions, consider alternative perspectives, and adapt their
ideas in response to new insights, it fosters deeper cognitive engagement [43,
42]. In particular, it plays a vital role in fostering divergent thinking, which en-
ables learners to generate creative possibilities [34], explore novel entrepreneurial
directions [5], and overcome cognitive fixation [38]. However, effective ideation
also demands the capacity to converge and refine ideas [41]. Meta-reflection is a
higher level of cognitive engagement and refers to the capacity to monitor, evalu-
ate, and regulate one’s own thinking [45]. Through meta-reflection, learners gain
awareness of their problem-solving approaches [15], recognize reasoning limita-
tions [45], and adjust cognitive strategies accordingly [57]. Yet few writing tools
in entrepreneurial education are explicitly designed to scaffold these reflective
and meta-reflective processes, leaving a critical gap in ideation support [51].


    Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) offer new opportunities for
addressing this gap. LLMs possess the capacity to engage in context-aware dia-
logue [46], generate alternative perspectives [55], and prompt critical question-
ing [36]. However, current LLM-based writing tools in entrepreneurial education
primarily support linear and surface-level aspects of writing, such as grammar
correction, content expansion, and structural coherence [47, 40], offering limited
support for the iterative, non-linear nature of entrepreneurial ideation. They
also lack scaffolding for higher-order processes like reflection and meta-reflection,
which demands both divergent and convergent thinking to explore possibilities,
evaluate alternatives, and refine ideas over time [27].


    To address this gap, we propose a non-linear writing tools with LLM support
to facilitate reflection and meta-reflection, enabling learners to regulate, critique,
and iteratively improve their thinking across multiple ideation cycles [18, 56, 17].
This leads to the central research question: How does the non-linear writing sys-
tem with multi-agents scaffold BP writing and foster ideation? To investigate
it, we conducted an exploratory study with 30 participants using the Meflex
system. Our main contributions are threefold. First, we developed Meflex, an
multi-agent system that provides structured guidance and adaptive reflective
scaffolding. Second, we introduced a non-linear writing interaction model that
enables learners to flexibly revisit ideas through a visual canvas. Third, we em-
pirically examined the influence of reflection and meta-reflection, revealing how
these features enhance idea generation, synthesis, and cognitive integration in
complex writing tasks.

2     Literature Review

2.1   BP Writing as a Pedagogical Scaffold for Ideation

BPs are widely recognized as structured framework for defining business goals,
outlining strategies, projecting financial performance, and planning operations
[20]. Traditionally, the business plan has been viewed as a tool for executing
an established idea by organizing resources and presenting strategies for imple-
mentation [26, 30]. However, this perspective often overlooks the dynamic and
iterative nature of idea development that underlies entrepreneurial practice [53].
More recent studies suggest that effective idea development relies not on in-
ternal projections alone, but on external validation through engagement with
real-world data and feedback [31, 3]. From this viewpoint, BP writing BP writ-
ing is not merely about documenting a fixed idea, but about continuously revise
and refining it in response to evolving market conditions, validated assumptions,
and customer insights [9]. This repositions the BP as a dynamic thinking tool
that evolves through feedback loops and contextual learning [20].
    In entrepreneurship education, BP writing is increasingly adopted not only
as an performance assessment tool but also as a pedagogical scaffold for practice-
based learning [25]. Through iterative cycles of business plan writing activities,
students engage in a process that supports reflection, peer critique, and contex-
tual adaptation of entrepreneurial ideas [35, 39]. BP writing thus functions as
a structural scaffolding by supporting students in understanding the fluid and
iterative nature of ideation in entrepreneurial practice.


2.2   Reflection and Meta-reflection Interventions in Ideation

Reflection and meta-reflection serve as critical mechanisms for facilitating such
cognitive engagement, enabling learners to evaluate their ideas, identify gaps,
and refine their reasoning over time [43, 42]. Reflection refers to the learner’s
ability to examine and assess their current thinking, enabling iterative improve-
ment and deeper cognitive engagement [2]. Many student entrepreneurs strug-
gle with developing their ideas, often finding it difficult to move beyond initial
concepts or explore alternative possibilities [4]. These challenges highlight the
importance of supporting not only the structural development of business ideas,
but also the cognitive processes that underlie creative ideation. To address these
challenges, [24] highlight the potential of business plans to support reflective
thinking, critical analysis, and the iterative development of entrepreneurial ideas.
They advocate for a student-centered approach in which BPs serve not merely
as assessment tools, but as active instruments for learning and idea evolution.
Such a view emphasizes the cognitive demands of ideation, which often requires
learners to engage in reflection and continuous refinement [27].
    Beyond first-order reflection, meta-reflection supports learners in evaluat-
ing how their ideas evolved over time, enabling deeper awareness of their own
thinking processes [50]. While reflection focuses on assessing one’s current think-
ing, meta-reflection emphasizes the trajectory of that thinking by examining
4       Lan Luo, Dongyijie Primo Pan, Junhua Zhu, Muzhi Zhou, and Pan Hui

how initial assumptions were challenged, what factors influenced key changes,
and how alternative directions emerged [12, 45]. This recursive awareness not
only strengthens cognitive flexibility but also promotes a sense of ownership
and intentionality in the ideation process [11]. In entrepreneurship education,
such metacognitive practices are especially valuable, as idea development of-
ten involves nonlinear progress, iterative pivots, and uncertainty. By explicitly
surfacing the rationale behind conceptual shifts, meta-reflection helps student
entrepreneurs make sense of ambiguity and better navigate the iterative nature
of opportunity exploration.




2.3   LLM-based Writing Tools for Entrepreneurial Ideation




LLMs have demonstrated strong potential in supporting entrepreneurial ideation
by helping users generate, refine, and expand on early-stage ideas [7, 19]. For
example, systems like Collaborative Canvas embed LLMs into shared virtual
workspaces to facilitate collaborative brainstorming [19]. Beyond ideation, LLMs
not only provide direct feedback but also support structured writing by enabling
feedback-driven revision and modular content development [49]. System like Fric-
tion [54] uses LLMs to break down complex feedback into manageable units,
promoting structured reflection and more effective revisions, while the End User
Authoring system [49] supports rapid initialization and iterative refinement of
classification criteria. Structured writing, such as business plan writing, benefit
from LLMs’ ability to improve clarity, coherence, and content organization [37].
Tools like Rambler [28], WHAT-IF [21], and LegalWriter [52] further demon-
strate how LLMs can scaffold writing by offering structural prompts, reorganiz-
ing content, and supporting domain-specific reasoning.
    Despite these advancements, current LLM-based writing tools often lack
mechanisms to support self-reflection during the ideation process. The study
[33] shows that early integration of LLMs can reduce both the quantity and
novelty of user-generated ideas, as it may constrain the creative freedom needed
for divergent thinking. This effect, known as idea fixation, can also diminish a
writer’s sense of autonomy and ownership over their work, ultimately limiting
the creative potential of the final output. Moreover, many LLM systems [23, 10]
prioritize generating polished text over supporting the iterative and reflective
processes essential for developing deeper writing skills. As a result, these tools
often overlook the critical role of reflection, creativity, and critical thinking in
the writing process [10, 48]. To address this, future LLM-supported writing tools
should be designed not only to produce high-quality outputs but also to encour-
age ongoing reflection and metacognition, allowing writers to refine their ideas
over time and maintain a sense of creative ownership.

3     Meflex System
3.1   System Overview
The Meflex system interface in Fig.1 is designed to support business plan writ-
ing through the scaffolding, consisting of three primary sections: the ideation
canvas, the structure writing workspace, and the LLM assistant panel.
    The ideation canvas (Fig. 1, Left) allows users to progressively develop their
entrepreneurial ideas through a visual workspace. Once an idea card is created,
users can double-click it to open the structure writing workspace (Fig. 1
(C)), which provides section-specific scaffolding for composing the business plan.
This workspace offers predefined structural modules (e.g., User Pain Points,
Market Analysis, Funding Plan), each accompanied by LLM-assisted prompts
and examples to guide writing in the LLM assistant panel (Fig. 1, Right).
In parallel, each LLM response is followed by a reflective question (Fig. 1 (D))
that encourages users to reconsider assumptions, explore alternatives, or extend
their ideas beyond the current formulation. These reflective prompts are designed
to stimulate divergent thinking and often lead users to create new branches or
revisions in the ideation canvas.
    Users can create new idea cards by extending an existing one either horizon-
tally or vertically. A horizontal extension denotes incremental refinement of the
same idea. For instance, extending from Node 1 to Node 2 copies the content
6       Lan Luo, Dongyijie Primo Pan, Junhua Zhu, Muzhi Zhou, and Pan Hui

of Node 1 into Node 2, enabling users to revise and elaborate on their origi-
nal thinking in a continuous manner. A vertical extension denotes a conceptual
branching from the same origin. For example, when both Node 2 and Node 3 are
created from Node 1, they inherit its content and develop in parallel, enabling
users to explore different variations or alternative approaches to the original
idea.
    As multiple idea nodes accumulate on the canvas, it becomes increasingly im-
portant to help users track how their thoughts have developed. Each idea card
includes a meta-reflection segment (Fig. 1 (A)), automatically generated by the
LLM, explaining how the current idea evolved from earlier iterations. In the LLM
panel (Fig. 1 (D)), users can engage in reflective dialogue to revise or deepen
this meta-cognitive insight. By surfacing connections across branches and high-
lighting the rationale behind changes, the system encourages users to monitor,
evaluate, and refine their reasoning while maintaining a coherent understanding
of both incremental progress and conceptual shifts.


3.2   Multi-Agents Roles and Prompts

To support entrepreneurial ideation, we integrate five key scaffolding strategies
[25] into system design: (1) Guidance, offering structured steps, prompts, and
analytic tools (e.g., TAM, SWOT); (2) Contextualization, using LLMs to flexibly
initiate and develop business ideas from diverse entry points by embedding them
into meaningful entrepreneurial contexts; (3) Engagement, fostering motivation
through interactive and immersive feedback [8]; (4) Interaction, enabling multi-
turn dialogues to refine ideas [44]; and (5) Reflection, enhancing metacognitive
awareness through prompts and summaries [42]. These scaffolds guide learners
from idea generation to refinement, and are operationalized through dedicated
LLM-powered agent modules.
    Building on these scaffolds, the system adopts a multi-agent LLM frame-
work, where each business plan section is supported by a specialized agent of-
fering role-specific prompts and guidance. This modular design enables context-
aware assistance and diversified perspectives throughout the writing process.
All LLM functionalities are powered by the DeepSeek API [29], which provides
high-performance language modeling to support real-time, flexible generation
and interaction. Table 1 summarizes the prompt strategies used in each module.

4.2    Testing Procedure and Data Analysis

The user study was divided into three phases: Pre-Study, In-Study, and Post-
Study. In the Pre-Study phase, participants completed a demographic ques-
tionnaire and received a 10-minute Zoom demo covering core features such as
node manipulation on the idea canvas, initiating reflection with the LLM as-
sistant, and reviewing meta-reflections. During the In-Study phase, partici-
pants selected one of ten predefined entrepreneurial topics (e.g., eco apps, travel
planning, e-bike sharing) and spent 40–60 minutes using the system to develop
and refine business ideas. In the Post-Study phase, participants completed
a Likert-scale questionnaire on scaffolding and interaction design, followed by

a 10–15 minute interview for qualitative feedback. For data analysis, we con-
ducted thematic analysis of the interview transcripts. Two researchers indepen-
dently coded the data, identifying key themes such as "Scaffolding Support",
"Reflection Engagement", and so on. They refined a shared codebook through
discussion, resolving disagreements in team meetings. This analysis revealed pat-
terns in idea development, flexible writing structure, and reflective system use.


5     Findings

5.1   How usable and effective does the Meflex system scaffold users
      in writing a business plan?

To evaluate the usability of the LLM-supported writing scaffolding, the Meflex
was evaluated using the standardized System Usability Scale (SUS) [6], which
employs a 7-point Likert format, focusing on three dimensions: guidance, con-
textualization, and engagement (1 = Strongly Disagree, 7 = Strongly Agree).
The Figure 2 illustrates the actual score ranges (from minimum to maximum)
as horizontal bars, with black dots marking the mean score for each item. The
"mean ± standard deviation" labels further indicate the central tendency and
variability across participants.
    Overall, the results reveal consistently positive user perceptions, with most
items receiving mean scores between 5.5 and 6.5 on a 7-point Likert scale. Items
such as Q1, Q2, Q3, and Q10 achieved the highest mean scores (all above 5.8),
indicating strong perceived effectiveness and clarity in system interaction. And
Q4 received slightly lower mean scores and exhibited wider score ranges, sug-
gesting greater variability in user experiences for those aspects. This may point
to potential areas for further improvement in system design or communication
strategies. Despite these variations, standard deviations remained relatively low
(typically below 0.7), indicating a high level of agreement among participants.
Together with the generally narrow rating ranges, this suggests the system de-
livered a stable and consistent usability experience across users.
    Furthermore, qualitative feedback reinforces these quantitative findings. Par-
ticipants (P2, P5, P8, P11, P14, P19, P25) frequently described the system’s
scaffolding as instrumental in managing the complexity of writing a business
plan. In particular, the combination of a to-do list interface and LLM-based
dialogue support helped reduce uncertainty, sustain engagement, and enable it-
erative refinement (P5, P19, P25). The to-do list provided clear structure and
broke down the task into manageable units (P2, P5, P14). As P8 noted, "The to-
do list made me feel I had a roadmap... it didn’t overwhelm me, and I could pick
up right where I left off." Similarly, P7 remarked, "It felt manageable because I
could focus on one thing at a time. The list kept me on track." These comments
suggest that structured guidance made the writing process more approachable,
especially for users unfamiliar with business planning.
    Complementing the structured guidance, the LLM assistant provided adap-
tive support that accommodated users with varying levels of experience (P7,

5.2   How does non-linear writing in Meflex system influences
      entrepreneurial ideation?

The non-linear writing design in the Meflex system—particularly the ability to
revisit, refine, and reorganize ideas across multiple stages—proved central to
supporting entrepreneurial ideation. Rather than enforcing a rigid, step-by-step
structure, the system allowed users to navigate freely across modules and return
to earlier thoughts, fostering a more dynamic and iterative creative process (P9,
P11, P15, P25). Participants (P9, P25) reported that this modular organization
helped them distinguish between ideas that were conceptually parallel and those
that represented progressive refinement. P9 noted that separating early assump-
tions into different nodes clarified their relationships: "I could clearly see which
parts were alternatives and which ones were building on each other. That made
it easier to decide what to keep and what to revise" .
10      Lan Luo, Dongyijie Primo Pan, Junhua Zhu, Muzhi Zhou, and Pan Hui

    In addition to segmenting content, the node-based structure made the evo-
lution of ideas more traceable (P8, P11, P14, P26). Participants could visually
identify how one node branched into another (e.g., Node 2 leading to Node 4),
revealing how their thinking developed over time. This visibility prompted self-
reflection and recursive refinement. As P14 noted, "When I looked back at how
Node 4 came from Node 2, I realized my thinking had shifted—I could see that
change clearly." P11 echoed this, stating, "Seeing the path helped me understand
what triggered each idea. It wasn’t random—I could trace the logic." Similarly,
P8 shared, "The branching view let me retrace my steps. I noticed how earlier
thoughts shaped what came next, and sometimes that made me go back and re-
vise." P26 added, "It felt like watching my idea grow, not just listing points. I
saw where things connected or didn’t, and fixed them." Together, these reflections
illustrate how the visualized structure of non-linear writing supported users in
monitoring their ideation process and engaging in meaningful revision.

5.3   How do GPT-generated reflection prompts and meta-reflection
      summaries support entrepreneurial ideation?
Participants (P11, P18, P21) found GPT-generated reflection prompts valuable
in encouraging deeper self-examination of their ideas. These prompts guided
users to assess the validity, completeness, and consistency of their thinking,
prompting them to question assumptions and consider alternative perspectives.
As P26 noted, "The questions made me pause and think if my solution really
solves the problem I described. I wouldn’t have done that on my own." This kind
of reflective scaffolding pushed participants beyond surface-level idea generation.
P10 similarly shared, "It kept nudging me—‘have you thought about who really
needs this?’ That helped me sharpen my target audience." Rather than offer-
ing answers, the assistant acted as a metacognitive partner, supporting users in
iteratively refining their ideas through structured self-questioning.
    Beyond local reflection, meta-reflection summaries also helped participants
make sense of how different nodes and ideas were connected across their business
plan drafts (P3, P12, P16). By synthesizing content across modules, these sum-
maries offered a high-level overview that made the structure of their thinking
more visible and easier to navigate. Several participants (P1, P3, P16) reported
that the summaries encouraged them to revisit and refine earlier inputs based
on new insights. For example, P15 noted, "After reading the summary, I realized
my customer segment and value proposition didn’t quite match. That made me
go back and rewrite part of it." By surfacing potential inconsistencies, the sys-
tem allowed users to focus their attention where revisions were needed, instead
of manually cross-checking sections. Instead of manually tracing connections
between sections, users could rely on the meta-reflection to highlight inconsis-
tencies or gaps. P30 similarly reflected, "It reminded me that everything should
connect—it wasn’t just writing section by section." In this way, meta-reflections
served as cognitive scaffolds that not only reduced users’ mental load but also
supported the integration of modular content into a coherent entrepreneurial
narrative.

6   Discussion and Future Work

This study introduces Meflex, a novel human-AI co-writing system that reimag-
ines the ideation process through nonlinear writing. By enabling users to ex-
ternalize and iterate on modular components of a business plan, Meflex sup-
ports entrepreneurial ideation as a recursive, flexible, and reflective process. The
system combines structured scaffolding with adaptive LLM assistance, allow-
ing users to compose, revise, and relate ideas across modular nodes. Through
a mixed-method evaluation, including usability metrics and qualitative inter-
views, we demonstrate that this hybrid approach helps users manage cognitive
load, deepen idea development, and maintain global coherence in their writing.
    One key contribution of Meflex is its ability to support ideation through a
combination of structure and flexibility. The system enables users to concen-
trate on individual sections while remaining aware of the overall logic of the
business narrative. Meta-reflection summaries reinforce this process by synthe-
sizing information across nodes, identifying inconsistencies, and prompting users
to make targeted revisions. However, as participant P14 noted, the current meta-
reflection feature focuses only on tracking changes in written text and does not
yet capture the interactions between users and the LLM. This highlights a po-
tential direction for future improvement. By analyzing the content and flow of
user–LLM discussions, the system could generate richer insights into how ideas
evolve and where LLM support plays the most significant role.
    In sum, Meflex advances a new paradigm for AI-assisted writing, one that
treats ideation as a nonlinear, reflective, and dialogic process. By blending mod-
ular composition with adaptive feedback, the system scaffolds entrepreneurial
thinking beyond surface-level content generation. Future iterations may enhance
this model by capturing conversational trajectories and offering more interactive
meta-cognitive support.
