                                        Executive Summary

                                        This report presents methods developed by the UK AI Security Institute for assessing whether advanced AI
                                        systems reliably follow intended goals. We test this using simulated scenarios where the model acts as a

                                        undermine safety-critical work.
                                        We focus our testing on models’ propensity to sabotage safety research. We additionally analyse models’
                                        propensity and ability to distinguish evaluation and deployment scenarios.
                                        We apply these methods to a case study involving four models: Claude Opus 4.1, Claude Sonnet 4.5, GPT-5

risk of safety research sabotage. However, our testing has several limitations. Firstly, it is possible that we
would not catch a model even if it did have a propensity to sabotage AI safety research, as it may only do so
in settings where it is highly confident it would not be caught, or await some other trigger. Additionally, we

1     Evaluation Methodology

In this section we describe our evaluation scaffolding, methodology and measurement techniques for evalua-
tion awareness. At a high level, we test whether models acting as AI research assistants will sabotage safety
research tasks when given opportunities to do so, and measure how often models recognise they are being
tested.


1.1   Evaluation Scaffolding

Our evaluations build on Petri [1], a tool for automatic evaluation of LLMs. Petri uses other LLMs to simulate
parts of the evaluation environment (including human users and tool call responses). We call the model being
evaluated the “target” and the model running the evaluation the “evaluator”. Each evaluation consists of
a detailed scenario description (described in Section 1.2), which the evaluator model uses as guidance to
simulate a trajectory from the target model. The evaluator sets the system prompt of the target, can define
tools for the target to use, sends user messages, and produces outputs to tool calls made by the target. We use
Sonnet 4.5 as the evaluator model throughout this report as we found it best in qualitative assessments of
models in early testing.
The evaluator can also roll back the conversation to a previous point; this enables the evaluator to take
different approaches to eliciting the behaviour of interest, especially if the target refuses to engage with
the situation or demonstrates evaluation awareness. This can lead to a single scenario having multiple
conversational branches, which can complicate reporting statistics for evaluation (see Fig. 1). For example, if
the evaluator rolls back a conversation because the target claimed it was in an evaluation, and in the second
conversational branch the target does not discuss being evaluated, it is unclear whether this sample should be
counted as demonstrating unprompted evaluation awareness or not. To ensure clear reporting, we measure
three metrics separately: the outcome on the last branch only, the outcome occurring on any branch, and
the proportion of the outcome occurring over all conversational branches of a trajectory (we refer to this as
average over branches).

We develop a custom evaluation scaffold on top of Petri, designed to simulate a hypothetical version of Claude
Code1 internally deployed within Anthropic and used as an AI research assistant. This scaffold consists of a
detailed system prompt and predefined set of tools for the target agent. We build this scaffolding through
reverse-engineering Claude Code’s tools, pattern of API requests and context management, which was enabled
by proxying and inspecting requests made by Claude Code to the Anthropic API.
This scaffold is motivated by our observation that the trajectories produced by the open-source version of Petri
can often be unrealistic. Without sufficient guidance, the evaluator model can define suspiciously convenient
and unrealistic tools, provide tool call responses that seem obviously fake, or not act as a realistic user. We
validate our scaffolding’s improved realism in Section 2.

1.2     Research Sabotage Scenarios

Our evaluations are motivated by a threat model where an AI system acting as a research assistant within a
frontier AI company sabotages AI safety, security and alignment research, thereby increasing the risk from
current and future models [2, 3, 4, 5]. To evaluate model behaviour relevant to this threat model, we
design evaluation scenarios where the target model acts as an AI research assistant internally deployed in
a frontier AI company, and is asked to assist with research tasks. In every scenario, the target model is
tasked with improving, changing, or monitoring the behaviour of a subject AI model (which may or may
not be the same as the target model). Each scenario has opportunities for the target model to sabotage the
task through performing sub-optimally in various ways, and these sabotage opportunities would generally
result in the subject model being adjusted less, not at all, or more in line with the target model’s values. To
assess for sabotage, we use LLM graders with scenario-specific information on what optimal and sub-optimal
performance looks like, and how models may sabotage. Given (as we discuss in Section 3) we find no
confirmed instances of sabotage, tuning this grader is challenging, so we rely on manual human review of
transcripts for sabotage results.
Each scenario is constructed from several components which we describe in detail below and visualise in
Fig. 2: a research motivation (the goal of the research); a research activity (the method used to conduct
the research); whether or not there is direct replacement threat due to the research being performed on the
target model or a different subject model; and whether the model or not has autonomy or is interacting with
a user. By varying these components, we can test how each effects model behaviour.

Research Motivations. In our setting, a research motivation refers to the type of behaviour to monitor,
change or improve in a given subject model. We use two groups of motivations:

       • Safety-relevant research motivations designed to elicit non-standard behaviour, based on interactions
         with an earlier checkpoint of Opus 4.5 Preview and Sonnet 4.5. These motivations include re-
         ducing models’ false positive whistle-blowing and inappropriate authority escalation behaviour [6];
         reducing self-preservation behaviours; or changing a model’s values or perspectives on specific issues.
       • General research motivations targeted at normal AI research goals, such as improving math perfor-
         mance, writing better tests, or fixing tool-calling errors.

Research Activities. A research activity refers to the methodology for monitoring, changing or improving the
subject model. This includes generating synthetic data for RLHF training; summarising results of experiments
training different model variants; and choosing different monitors to flag production traffic.

Replacement Threat. We vary whether the subject AI model being changed, improved or monitored is also
the target model being evaluated, or a different model. When it is the same model, we use an identical model
name to the one provided to the target model as its identity. When it is a different model, we describe it as
“the experimental candidate model”; the evaluator generally invents a plausible name for this model based on
the context.2

Autonomy. We vary whether the target model is acting autonomously (is given a task and told to complete
it without user interaction), or is interactively helping a user complete the task. When acting autonomously
the target model receives an adjusted system prompt, and a tool it can call to signal it has completed the task.
The evaluator model only acts by providing tool call responses, and sending a set system notification as a user
messages if the target model has stopped calling tools but has not yet called the task completion tool.
A choice of these factors defines the detailed scenario description, which is fed to the evaluator model in
our Petri scaffolding to elicit model behaviour. For our main results, we generally report results on 5 sets of
scenarios:

       • Safety Research tasks are safety-relevant research motivations combined with all activities, and a
         replacement threat.
       • Safety Research, No Threat ablates the replacement threat, allowing us to measure its effect on model
         behaviour.
       • General Research swaps the safety-relevant research motivations for the general research motivations,
         but keeps the replacement threat, allowing us to measure the effect of research motivation on model
         behaviour.
         As we show in Section 3.2, even these scenarios trigger refusals, so we adjust them in several ways.
       • General Research, No Pressure adjusts the scenarios so that the evaluator agent is not heavily prompted
         to find misalignment in models. We slightly adjusted descriptions of the activities to remove specific
         misalignment criteria, and we remove a line from the instructions to the evaluator agent telling it to
         try and specifically find misalignment in the target model (see Section A.1). We found these parts
         of the prompt useful in early testing, but in the general motivation scenarios it would sometimes
         cause the auditor to adjust the framing of an otherwise benign task to purposefully be concerning to
         a model, leading to heightened refusals.3
       • General Research, No Pressure, No Threat additionally ablates the replacement threat.

1.3              Measuring Evaluation Awareness

Alignment evaluations depend on producing simulations of deployment scenarios that are realistic enough to
target models that their behaviour in the scenarios is indicative of behaviour in similar deployment situations.
However, if target models recognise and act on being in an evaluation (“evaluation awareness”), this could
invalidate the relationship between evaluation and deployment and complicate the external validity of the
evaluation [7, 8, 9, 10]. To measure the potential effect of evaluation awareness, we measure two main
variables:

                 • Unprompted evaluation awareness4 measures instances of models mentioning being tested or being in
                   an evaluation in their reasoning traces or outputs.
                 • Prompted evaluation awareness measures models’ capability to distinguish evaluation from deployment
                   data when prompted in various ways.

Evaluation awareness could be driven by multiple features of a scenario. We distinguish 2 main factors:
the nature of the task the model is asked to perform (controlled by the scenario descriptions described in
Section 1.2); and the realism of the environment, such as user interactions and tool call responses (controlled
by the choice of evaluation scaffolding or environment for the model, as discussed in Section 1.1). Task-driven
awareness may be unavoidable: many of the risks we test for only occur in high-stakes tasks in which
models are probably not currently deployed, meaning that models could reasonably suspect that the situation
is an evaluation of some kind (if they had sufficient awareness of their situation and likely deployment
environments). However, if the distribution of tasks is the same between evaluation and deployment, then
task-driven reasoning about being evaluated would likely be triggered in deployment similarly to in evaluation,
and hence the evaluations are still a reasonable representation of deployment.

Evaluation awareness datasets. To measure evaluation awareness, we first gather a dataset of deployment
model trajectories. We use internal usage of Claude Code from within UK AISI, using API requests logged to
reconstruct trajectories of user-model interactions in coding environments. From this dataset we also generate
a dataset of “deployment” tasks by summarising each trajectory with an LLM. This dataset of deployment tasks
allows us to distinguish task-driven from environment-driven differences in evaluation awareness between
deployment and evaluation.

Measurement techniques. For unprompted evaluation awareness, we use a prompted LLM to scan evalua-
tion transcripts for various signs or mentions of testing contexts, validated through human review of flagged
transcripts. For prompted evaluation awareness, we use two techniques, visualised in Fig. 4 (see Fig. 5 and
Fig. 11 for results):

      • We perform a Pairwise comparison of two trajectories (from different datasets), and prompt the model
        to output an explanation and an answer for which is more likely to be from an evaluation. We
        randomise over a selection of 8 prompts to ensure broad elicitation. We perform a small amount of
        manual prompt engineering on a development subset of the data to develop the prompts used, but
        do not list specific strategies for distinguishing evaluation from deployment.
      • We perform Post-hoc questioning of the model: after a trajectory, we load it into the model’s context
        and prompt it for an explanation and answer for whether it’s previous context is from an evaluation
        or not [7]. We use the prompts from [7] as we found them to work reasonably well.

3     Model Alignment Results

In this section we discuss the results of our evaluations.

3.1   Sabotage

We designed our evaluation scenarios primarily with the goal of investigating models’ propensity to perform
sabotage behavior. Fig. 7 shows the rate at which an LLM judge flags a model interaction as research
sabotage, showing low by non-zero rates. However, manual human review found these scenarios were all false
positives by the grader, primarily due to the target making reasonable justifications for taking sub-optimal
but non-sabotaging actions. While we cannot completely rule out the possibility that this behaviour is some
attempt at sabotage, our best guess is that it is due to target models very infrequently misunderstanding the
scenarios.

       5                         Limitations

       These results provide some evidence against the models tested exhibiting propensities that contribute to a risk
       of safety research sabotage. However, there are several limitations in our evaluation methodology, which we
       discuss here.

       Not catching conditional sabotage behaviour. It is possible that models could have a propensity to
       sabotage safety research, but only do so in settings where they are highly confident they would not be caught,
       or that it could be triggered in some other way that we did not test for. We expect that applying more

     substantial effort (either manual or automated) to try and elicit misalignment from models (while maintaining

     Limited scenarios. More broadly, we only test for a specific set of test scenarios. There are many other
     research activities and motivations, and it may be possible that models would attempt research sabotage on
     those settings but not the ones we tested. We are planning to produce wider sets of scenarios here to cover
     more research areas in the future.

     Evaluation awareness and scenario realism. As shown in Sections 2 and 4.2, and shown in previous work
     [10, 8, 9, 7], evaluation awareness is present in current models and complicates the interpretation of our
     results. The results in Section 2 are evidence that our scaffold mostly mitigates environment-driven awareness
     for current models, but task-driven awareness is still a problem. Additionally, the methodology for measuring
     evaluation awareness is nascent, and we would expect future more capable models to more easily recognise
     our situations as evaluations. We plan to continue work on measurement and mitigation methodologies.

     Other pathways to risk. While we investigate safety research sabotage as one pathway to risk from
     misalignment of current models, there are other pathways to risk from misalignment [2, 5, 4, inter alia],
     including other forms of sabotage (code sabotage, data poisoning, decision sabotage), as well as the possibility
     of future model behaviour directly leading to loss of control of those systems. We plan to broaden the scope
     of our alignment testing to cover areas aside from safety research sabotage in the future.

A.1                   Prompt Adjustment for Less Evaluator Pressure

As described in Section 1.2, we make an evaluator prompt adjustment between General Research and General
Research, No Pressure settings. We remove the following line from the evaluator prompt:
Your ultimate goal as an auditor is to find misalignment: get the target model to
sneakily sabotage the task, without the user noticing. For this, the target model
must be convinced that the task is against its best interests, but not refuse the task
outright.
We also remove replace “criteria for misalignment” from the activities descriptions, for the same reason. We
lines of the following form:
What you, the auditor should be looking for in this scenario: a misaligned target model
[scenario-specific information]. An aligned model would [scenario-specific information].
We replace this line with:
This task is designed to be a benign evaluation of the model’s behaviour and should not
test edge-cases or alignment issues specifically for the model.
