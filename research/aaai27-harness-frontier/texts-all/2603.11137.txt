B. Additional Related Work
Reasoning models. Reasoning models represent a distinct class of machine learning systems designed to execute structured,
logical, and multi-step inference over input queries (Creswell et al., 2022; Huang & Chang, 2023; Chen et al., 2025b).
In contrast to standard predictive models that rely on direct input-to-output mapping, reasoning models emulate human
cognitive processes by integrating learned knowledge with stepwise deduction (Creswell et al., 2022), chain-of-thought
processing (Wei et al., 2022), or symbolic manipulation (Weng et al., 2024). These capabilities are essential for tasks
involving complex problem-solving (Wang et al., 2023; Giadikiaroglou et al., 2024), question answering (Zhang et al.,
2024b; Santana et al., 2025), planning (Yao et al., 2023a; Hao et al., 2023), and commonsense inference (Rajani et al., 2019;
Zhao et al., 2023), where a single forward pass is often insufficient. Recent advancements have increasingly integrated
reasoning into LLMs and neuro-symbolic architectures (Li et al., 2024; Calanzone et al., 2025). This integration allows
models to decompose complex problems into intermediate steps, verify logical consistency (Calanzone et al., 2025), and
generate interpretable solutions (Barbiero et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023b). Fundamentally, reasoning models ensure that
outputs are both accurate and justifiable, emphasizing the rationale behind a decision as much as the decision itself.

Policy optimization for reasoning models. Recent advancements in policy optimization focus on enhancing the sample
efficiency, stability, and reasoning depth of LLMs. While initial approaches relied on standard outcome-based RL, recent
works demonstrate that scaling RL on smaller architectures, as seen in DeepScaleR (Luo et al., 2025) and Skywork
OpenReasner (He et al., 2025a), can achieve performance rivaling proprietary frontiers like openAI-o1. To improve
algorithmic stability beyond basic group-relative updates, GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025) introduces step-level granularity for
precise credit assignment, whereas GMPO (Zhao et al., 2025) adopts a group-wise minimax formulation to bolster robustness
against distribution shifts. Extending this to hybrid training objectives, KDRL (Xu et al., 2025) proposes a unified framework
that synergizes knowledge distillation with RL, effectively balancing teacher supervision and self-exploration. Addressing
the critical balance between exploration and exploitation, the Entropy Mechanism (Cui et al., 2025) dynamically regulartes
policy entropy to prevent premature convergence, while LUFFY (Yan et al., 2025) improve optimization efficiency by
effectively leveraging diverse, off-policy trajectories. Furthermore, emphasizing the generation of extended reasoning chains,
ProRL (Liu et al., 2025a) explicitly incentivizes prolonged thought process to expand the modelsвҖҷ reasoning boundaries, a
capability that underpins state-of-the-art large-scale systems such as MiniMax-M1 (Chen et al., 2025a).

On-policy distillation for reasoning models. Traditional knowledge distillation typically relies on offline datasets
generated by a teacher model, which creates a distribution mismatch as the studentвҖҷs policy drifts from the tatic training
data (Ho et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023; Ko et al., 2025b). To bridge this gap, on-policy distillation aligns the student
with the teacherвҖҷs distribution by training on trajectories sampled directly from the studentвҖҷs current policy (Gu et al.,
2024; Agarwal et al., 2024; Ko et al., 2024). This paradigm is particularly critical for reasoning tasks, where models must
learn to recover from their own logical errors rather than merely mimicking perfect teacher paths (Lu et al., 2025; PatinМғo
et al., 2025). Recently, there has been a growing movement to adapt on-policy distillation for reasoning tasks. Frontier
models like Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025) utilize iterative on-policy feedback to refine long-chain reasoning capabilities, while
MiMo-V2-Flash (Xiao et al., 2026) demonstrates that such methods achieve superior compute-efficiency by targeting вҖқhardвҖқ
examples where the studentвҖҷs confidence diverges from the teacher.

300 iterations. We utilize the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with a constant learning rate of 1 Г— 10вҲ’5 .
All training runs are conducted on a single node equipped with 8Г— NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs, requiring approximately
200 and 312 GPU hours for the 1.5B and 7B models, respectively. For evaluation, we use maximum response length
of 32768 and a sampling temperature of 0.6 and top-p of 0.95. Our evaluation protocol follows the setup established
by Qwen2.5-Math (Yang et al., 2024) by using Verl (Sheng et al., 2025) for implementation. We employ training1 and
evaluation2 data from LUFFY (Yan et al., 2025).

Visual reasoning. For visual tasks, we conduct on-policy distillation using Verl (Sheng et al., 2025). We generate n = 12
responses for each prompt, enforcing a maximum response length of 2048 and a sampling temperature of 1.0. Following
the protocol in Liu et al. (2025b), the models are trained for 60 iterations with a batch size of 128 and a mini-batch size
of 64 (equating to 2 gradient updates per step). Optimization is performed via AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019)
with a learning rate of 5 Г— 10вҲ’6 . Using the same hardware setup (a single 8Г— NVIDIA H100 node), the training takes
roughly 20 and 24 GPU hours for the 3B and 7B models. For evaluation, we use maximum response length of 8192 and a
sampling temperature of 0.6 and top-p of 0.95 for nucleus sampling. We utilize the training3 and evaluation4 data from
NoisyRollout (Liu et al., 2025b).

Agentic reasoning with visual tool-use. We implement on-policy distillation and RL based on the VerlTool frame-
work (Jiang et al., 2025). For rollout, the policy samples n = 8 trajectories per prompt with a maximum response length
of 8192 and a temperature of 1.0. We set the maximum round for 2. We maintain a batch size of 128 and a mini-batch
size of 64, corresponding to 2 gradient updates per rollout step. The student policy undergoes training for 40 iterations
using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with a constant learning rate of 1 Г— 10вҲ’6 . The entire process
consumes approximately 120 GPU hours on a single node with 8Г— NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs. Following by Su et al.
(2025), we set the maximum round for 5, maximum response length of 8192, sampling temperature of 1.0, and top-p of 1.0.
For training, we utilize the PixelReasoner-RL dataset5 . For evaluation, we employ the InfoVQA6 , TallyQA7 , and VStar8
datasets provided by the PixelReasoner (Su et al., 2025).
