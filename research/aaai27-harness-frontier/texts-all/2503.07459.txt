                                         SUMMARY
                                         Complex medical reasoning requires integrating heterogeneous clinical evidence across mul-
                                         tiple inference steps. Large language models (LLMs) now approach this through two routes:
                                         internalized reasoning and externalized agent scaffolding (frameworks that decompose prob-
                                         lems collaboratively amongst multiple LLMs). To determine whether these routes are exclusive
                                         or complementary, we introduce MedicalAgentsBench, a filtered benchmark of 862 complex clin-
                                         ical questions drawn from the union of eight medical datasets via difficulty-aware curation and
                                         contamination screening. Evaluating three internalized reasoning models (DeepSeek-R1, o1-
                                         mini, and o3-mini), seven base models, and nine externalized agent-based methods, we find
                                         that internalized and externalized approaches each independently improve performance, and
                                         that their benefits compound: the highest accuracy is achieved by layering agent workflows onto
                                         an internalized reasoning model (i.e., o3-mini + MDAgents with 35.1%). Pareto analysis shows
                                         this combination dominates the cost–performance frontier; moreover, lightweight optimization on
                                         inexpensive models offers an entry point for resource-constrained settings. Our benchmark is at
                                         https://github.com/gersteinlab/MedicalAgentsBench.

                                         INTRODUCTION
                                         Clinical reasoning often requires synthesizing heterogeneous evidence (patient history, labora-
                                         tory values, imaging findings) across multiple inferential steps to arrive at a diagnosis or man-
                                         agement decision. Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities
                                         in medical natural language processing, ranging from clinical question answering to diagnostic
                                         assistance 1–5 ; yet, as illustrated in Figure 1, even state-of-the-art models (OpenAI o3, GPT-
                                         4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) continue to struggle with the multi-step reasoning these scenarios de-
                                         mand 6,7 .

                                                                                               1
     The evolution of medical reasoning evaluation has progressed with numerous specialized
datasets. Popular benchmarks like MedQA 8 , PubMedQA 9 , and MedMCQA 10 established the
foundation with standardized multiple-choice questions from medical licensing exams and PubMed
abstracts. The field then expanded to domain-specific resources like clinical notes 11 and ques-
tion summarization 12 , integrating visual elements through datasets like pathology images 13 and
radiology questions 14,15 . To address linguistic and regional diversity, multilingual datasets emerged 16–18 .
The ecosystem further matured with comprehensive collections such as MultiMedQA 19 , which
combines seven distinct medical datasets.
     Research in applying LLMs to medical tasks has progressed through several distinct phases.
Initial efforts focused on evaluating general-purpose LLMs in medical contexts 20–22 . General
foundation models 19,23 achieved physician-level performance through prompt engineering, fol-
lowed by domain-specialized open-source models 24–26 . The field then advanced toward spe-
cialized reasoning methods 7,27 and retrieval-augmented generation 28–30 . More recently, agent-
based frameworks such as MedAgents 31 and MDAgents 32 have shown promise by leveraging
collaborative multi-agent systems for complex medical decision-making.
     To improve LLM reasoning on such tasks, the community has explored a spectrum of ap-
proaches that differ in how reasoning is structured and externalized. As summarized in Table 1,
existing methods can be broadly categorized into traditional prompting, advanced prompting,
discussion-based agent frameworks, and agentic workflow optimization methods, reflecting in-
creasing degrees of reasoning externalization and structural optimization. Traditional prompt-
ing techniques, such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and Self-Consistency (SC) 33,34 , aim to elicit
intermediate reasoning steps by prompting models to explicitly verbalize multi-step logical infer-
ences before producing a final answer, with SC further improving robustness through aggregat-
ing multiple independently sampled reasoning paths. Advanced prompting methods, including
domain-specific approaches such as MedPrompt 35 , extend this paradigm by introducing struc-
tured self-reflection, diversity, or multi-round prompting within a single model instance, but typ-
ically yield only incremental performance gains in complex medical reasoning settings. In con-
trast, discussion-based agent frameworks such as MedAgents 31 and MDAgents 32 externalize
reasoning by coordinating multiple specialized agents that engage in explicit discussion, consul-
tation, and cross-verification, decomposing complex medical reasoning into collaborative sub-
tasks such as hypothesis generation and evidence evaluation. Beyond discussion-based agent
frameworks, agentic workflow optimization methods 36,37 further automate the design of agent
interaction structures by algorithmically optimizing reasoning workflows and control strategies,
rather than relying on fixed, hand-designed agent protocols. While agent-based approaches
can substantially enhance reasoning performance by externalizing deliberation and verification
beyond a single model forward pass, they also introduce increased system complexity and com-
putational overhead.
     In parallel, a new class of reasoning models has emerged, including OpenAI o3-mini and
DeepSeek R1 38 . In this work, we use the term reasoning models to refer to LLMs whose pa-
rameters are explicitly optimized, via reinforcement learning, chain-of-thought supervision, or
distillation, to perform multi-step reasoning internally, rather than relying on external orches-
tration or tool-based decomposition. A useful overview of this emerging category is provided
by Raschka 39 . In effect, these models amortize reasoning into their parameters: training-time
optimization shifts more of the cognitive work upstream, so complex inference can be executed
within a single model call at deployment time 38 . Unlike agent frameworks, these models aim to
perform complex reasoning within a single forward pass. This distinction raises a fundamental
and unresolved question: in medical reasoning tasks, do internalized reasoning and external-
ized agent workflows represent exclusive, competing paradigms, or are they complementary
approaches that can readily be combined?
     Answering this question is hindered by several limitations of existing medical benchmarks.

                                                   2
First, as shown in Table 2, current datasets contain a large proportion of straightforward examination-
style questions. On such questions, even base LLMs achieve high accuracy (see Table 3, “Full”
columns), masking meaningful differences between internalized reasoning models and agent
frameworks. Second, inconsistent and subjective sampling practices across prior studies 31,32
prevent reliable cross-method comparison, particularly between approaches that differ substan-
tially in cost and inference structure. Third, existing benchmarks rarely evaluate performance
jointly with computational cost and inference time, despite these factors being central to the
design trade-offs of agent-based systems.
     To address these challenges, we introduce MedicalAgentsBench, a benchmark specifically
designed to enable a principled comparison between internalized reasoning models and exter-
nalized agent frameworks on complex medical reasoning tasks. Unlike existing benchmarks that
either focus on general medical knowledge or suffer from ceiling effects, MedicalAgentsBench
employs a pipeline that: (1) draws from eight diverse established medical datasets (MedQA, Pub-
MedQA, MedMCQA, MedBullets, MMLU, MMLU-Pro, MedExQA, and MedXpertQA); (2) applies
difficulty-based filtering to select questions that challenge current models (specifically, questions
where fewer than 50% of tested models provide correct answers); (3) conducts thorough con-
tamination analysis to ensure validity; and (4) incorporates human annotations from medical
professionals to verify reasoning depth requirements. Our comprehensive experiments yield
several key insights: (a) both externalized agent scaffolding and internalized reasoning indepen-
dently improve performance, and their benefits are complementary: layering agent workflows on
internalized reasoning models (e.g., o3-mini + MDAgents) yields the highest overall accuracy;
(b) the Pareto frontier of cost–performance trade-offs is dominated by reasoning-model–agent
combinations at the high end and by lightweight workflow optimization methods (e.g., SPO) at
the low end; and (c) open-source reasoning models achieve competitive accuracy, though their
extended reasoning chains do not necessarily reduce per-sample inference cost.
     Concurrent with our work, several related evaluation efforts have emerged. MedHELM 40
provides a holistic evaluation framework for LLMs across a broad range of medical tasks. MedA-
gentBench 41 introduces a virtual EHR environment for benchmarking clinical agent capabilities.
MedAgentBoard 42 benchmarks multi-agent collaboration against conventional methods across
diverse medical tasks. MedAgentGym 43 proposes a scalable agentic training environment for
code-centric reasoning in biomedical data science. Together, these works reflect the growing
community interest in rigorous, principled evaluation of LLMs and agent-based systems in med-
ical contexts.


RESULTS
Benchmark overview
MedicalAgentsBench is a carefully curated benchmark designed to evaluate complex medical
reasoning tasks. Drawing from eight established medical datasets (MedQA 8 , PubMedQA 9 ,
MedMCQA 10 , MedBullets 44 , MMLU 45 , MMLU-Pro 46 , MedExQA 47 , and MedXpertQA 48 ), we sys-
tematically construct a challenging subset of 862 questions (average token length: 147.4) that fo-
cuses on complex reasoning scenarios. Our multi-stage construction pipeline combines difficulty-
aware curation (selecting questions where fewer than 50% of tested models answer correctly),
contamination screening via MELD 49 , and human validation of reasoning depth by four medi-
cal professionals using a five-level rubric (see Methods for full details). As shown in Table 3,
while models achieve high accuracy on the Full set (e.g., GPT-4o: 88.2% on MedQA), their
performance drops significantly on our Hard subset (e.g., GPT-4o: 32.2%), confirming that the
selected questions demand deeper analytical processing and multi-step medical reasoning.

                                                 3
Quantitative evaluations
Most models struggle with our challenging benchmark, with even powerful systems like GPT-4o
achieving only 32.2% accuracy on MedQA and 18.1% on MedExQA in our Hard subset. This
substantial performance drop confirms that our selection criteria effectively identify questions that
require advanced reasoning. Amid these challenges, DeepSeek-R1 demonstrates remarkable
performance, achieving the highest scores on five datasets (MedMCQA: 30.9%, MMLU: 43.7%,
MMLU-Pro: 36.8%, MedExQA: 26.0%, and MedXpertQA-U: 26.3%). Similarly, o3-mini excels
on two datasets (MedQA: 52.9% and MedBullets: 50.3%).
    The central finding from our method comparison (Table 4) is that externalized and internal-
ized reasoning are complementary. Agent frameworks substantially improve base models: for
instance, GPT-4o + AFlow (29.8% average) outperforms bare GPT-4o zero-shot by a wide mar-
gin. Reasoning models independently advance the frontier: bare o3-mini (27.8%) already sur-
passes most agent-augmented base-model configurations. Critically, layering agent workflows
on top of reasoning models yields further gains: o3-mini + MDAgents achieves the highest over-
all accuracy (35.1%), followed by o3-mini + MultiPersona (34.1%) and o3-mini + CoT (32.7%),
all surpassing bare o3-mini by 5–7 percentage points. This indicates that agents add value even
when the underlying model is already strong, likely by introducing diverse reasoning paths and
catching errors that a single model pass would miss.
    Among individual methods, automated workflow optimization (AFlow) and multi-persona prompt-
ing (MultiPersona) achieve the strongest results when applied to base models, while discussion-
based agent frameworks (MedAgents, MDAgents) show their greatest value when paired with
reasoning models. Traditional prompting gains from CoT-SC over CoT are inconsistent on this
hard subset, and domain-specific methods like MedPrompt lack consistency across datasets
(see Table 4 for full per-dataset results). Additional qualitative error analysis and representative
failure cases are provided in Supplemental Note S1.


Benchmark quality assessment
We assess benchmark quality along two dimensions: reasoning depth to verify cognitive difficulty
and data contamination to ensure fair and reliable model comparison.

Reasoning depth analysis
To directly assess whether MedicalAgentsBench emphasizes multi-step clinical reasoning rather
than superficial difficulty or obscure knowledge retrieval, we conducted an expert-annotated
analysis of reasoning depth using a standardized five-level rubric detailed in the human vali-
dation protocol in Methods. Four medical students in their clinical years independently anno-
tated a stratified sample of 20 questions per dataset (10 from the Full split and 10 from the
Hard split), covering all eight source datasets across nine evaluation subsets (180 questions to-
tal). This design enables a direct comparison of the cognitive demands imposed by the original
datasets versus the curated benchmark. Figure 2 reveals a clear structural shift in cognitive de-
mands between the two splits. In the full distributions of generalist benchmarks such as MMLU
and MMLU-Pro, reasoning depth is bottom-heavy, with a predominance of Level 1 and Level 2
questions solvable via direct fact retrieval or single-step deduction. In contrast, the Hard subset
exhibits a pronounced shift toward Levels 4 and 5, corresponding to multi-hop clinical reasoning
that requires synthesizing heterogeneous evidence or resolving competing hypotheses.
    This pattern is consistent across diverse medical domains and is not driven by a small num-
ber of outlier datasets. For example, in MedXpertQA, the Hard subset effectively removes direct-
recall questions and concentrates on scenarios that require integrating patient history, vital signs,

                                                 4
and diagnostic findings to arrive at a diagnosis or management decision. Even for case-based
datasets such as MedBullets, which already operate at relatively high reasoning levels, the Hard
split further emphasizes Level 5 reasoning involving conflicting clinical evidence or subtle con-
traindications. Inter-annotator agreement is substantial (overall Fleiss’ κ = 0.62, Supplemental
Table S3), indicating that these distinctions in reasoning depth are clinically meaningful and re-
producible rather than subjective.

Data contamination analysis
We assessed potential data contamination using the MELD (Memorization Effects Levenshtein
Detector) framework 49 . MELD splits each question into two halves: the first half is provided
as context, and the model is prompted to generate the second half. The Levenshtein distance
ratio between the generated output and the original unseen portion quantifies memorization;
higher similarity indicates that the model has likely encountered the question during training. This
approach is particularly well-suited to multiple-choice benchmarks, where verbatim reproduction
of question text, rather than answer choices, serves as the primary indicator of training exposure
(Figure 3). MELD has high precision but unknown recall: a detected match strongly indicates
memorization, but absence of a match does not guarantee clean data.
    The analysis reveals a clear divide between commercial and open-source models. OpenAI
models show consistently low similarity scores (median 20–25%), suggesting minimal verba-
tim memorization. In contrast, several open-source models (DeepSeek-R1, QwQ-32B, Llama-
3.3-70B) exhibit substantially higher scores, with some reproducing question texts at over 95%
character-level accuracy on MMLU and MMLU-Pro (60–90% similarity range). Importantly, our
Hard subset demonstrates significantly lower MELD scores across all models than the corre-
sponding Full sets, confirming its utility for more reliable cross-model comparison. A manual
review of high-similarity cases in MedMCQA and MedExQA attributed some elevated scores to
shared standard medical terminology rather than direct data leakage. Given these contamination
concerns, our primary method comparisons (Table 4) focus on OpenAI models (GPT-4o-mini,
GPT-4o, o1-mini, o3-mini), which exhibit the lowest contamination risk and therefore provide
the most reliable basis for comparing reasoning strategies. Search-based optimization methods
(SPO and AFlow) were evaluated only for GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o.


Cost-performance analysis
Our evaluation examines both model architecture efficiency and reasoning method effectiveness
through a cost-performance lens. We analyze these dimensions separately using two comple-
mentary visualizations.

Base model cost-efficiency
As shown in Figure 4, the ten base models occupy markedly different positions in accuracy–cost
space across the eight datasets. The marker encoding and Pareto frontier are described in the
figure caption; here, we focus on the substantive pattern. In each panel, the red dashed frontier
identifies non-dominated models that achieve the best accuracy attainable at a given inference
cost, whereas points below the frontier are strictly inefficient relative to another model on the
same dataset. DeepSeek-R1 and o3-mini recur on or near this frontier across many datasets,
indicating that they frequently define the strongest empirical trade-offs among the base models.
    The frontier nevertheless varies by task. MedQA and MedBullets show steep gains as in-
ference cost increases, whereas PubMedQA exhibits a flatter profile with more modest returns


                                                 5
to expensive models. Across several of the more challenging datasets, internalized reasoning
models occupy stronger positions than non-reasoning models at comparable cost levels, sug-
gesting that explicit training for multi-step reasoning can improve deployment-time efficiency as
well as accuracy. Open-source models demonstrate competitive performance. On the Full test
set, DeepSeek-R1 achieves the highest accuracy among all models on several benchmarks,
including MedMCQA (82.2%) and MMLU-Pro (79.7%), rivaling or surpassing closed-source al-
ternatives. However, its extended reasoning chains result in higher per-sample inference costs
than compact closed-source models such as o3-mini, illustrating a trade-off between reasoning
depth and cost efficiency.

Reasoning method cost-efficiency
Figure 5 extends our analysis to the full space of model–method combinations. The Pareto
frontier, together with the annotated transitions in the figure, reveals two distinct mechanisms for
improving medical reasoning, with opposite cost signatures.
    Externalizing reasoning trades cost for performance. Adding agent scaffolding to a fixed
base model consistently improves accuracy but increases inference cost. For instance, moving
from bare GPT-4o-mini (11.0%) to GPT-4o-mini + SPO (20.1%) nearly doubles performance
at a modest cost increase. However, not all externalization is equally efficient: GPT-4o-mini
+ MedAgents (18.3%) costs far more than SPO yet achieves lower accuracy, illustrating that
heavier agent scaffolding can yield diminishing or even negative returns on weak base models.
The same pattern holds at the top of the frontier: o3-mini zero-shot (27.8%) improves to o3-mini
+ CoT (32.7%) and then to o3-mini + MDAgents (35.1%), each step adding both accuracy and
cost.
    Internalizing reasoning improves performance while reducing inference cost. Switch-
ing from an agent-augmented base model to a bare reasoning model can simultaneously in-
crease accuracy and decrease per-sample inference cost. The most striking example is the
transition from the cluster of GPT-4o + agent configurations (25–30% accuracy at 10–100×
higher inference cost) to bare o3-mini (27.8%): the reasoning model achieves comparable or
higher accuracy at an order of magnitude lower inference cost. This reflects the efficiency of in-
ternalized reasoning at deployment time, where multi-step inference is compiled into the model’s
parameters rather than orchestrated through repeated API calls. We note, however, that this
analysis considers only inference cost; the training cost required to develop reasoning capabili-
ties (e.g., reinforcement learning, chain-of-thought distillation) is not publicly disclosed by model
providers and may be substantial.
    These two mechanisms are complementary and together define the Pareto frontier. The op-
timal strategy traces a path that first externalizes reasoning on cheap models (GPT-4o-mini +
SPO), then internalizes by upgrading to a reasoning model (o3-mini), and finally externalizes
again by layering agents on the reasoning model (o3-mini + MDAgents). Agent frameworks ap-
plied to non-reasoning models (e.g., GPT-4o + MedAgents) fall well below this frontier, as their
high inference cost is not justified by commensurate accuracy gains. Practitioners should there-
fore view internalized reasoning as the primary investment for improving accuracy per dollar, with
externalized agent scaffolding as an additive enhancement best applied on top of already-strong
reasoning models.


DISCUSSION
The central finding of this study is that internalized reasoning and externalized agent scaffold-
ing are not substitutes but complements. Agents lift base-model accuracy (GPT-4o to GPT-4o

                                                 6
+ MedAgents), internalized reasoning models do the same at lower inference cost (GPT-4o +
agents to o3-mini), and agents on top of internalized reasoning models push accuracy further
still (o3-mini to o3-mini + MDAgents, 35.1%). Neither approach subsumes the other. Internalized
reasoning models internalize multi-step inference through training-time optimization, while agent
frameworks introduce diverse viewpoints and iterative error correction at inference time. The two
mechanisms operate on different failure modes, which explains why their gains compound rather
than overlap.
     The Pareto frontier analysis further clarifies when each approach is most valuable. At the
budget-conscious end, lightweight workflow optimization methods such as SPO applied to inex-
pensive base models (e.g., GPT-4o-mini + SPO) provide the most cost-efficient entry point. At
the high-performance end, internalized reasoning models combined with agent methods (o3-mini
+ CoT, o3-mini + MDAgents) dominate the frontier. Discussion-based frameworks (MedAgents,
MDAgents) show their greatest value when paired with internalized reasoning models, where the
additional inference cost is justified by meaningful accuracy gains. Our error analysis (Supple-
mental Note S1) confirms this mechanistically: multi-agent aggregation amplifies shared biases
when agents lack diverse reasoning priors, whereas structured self-critique and role-specialized
disagreement can recover from localized errors.
     For deployment, these results suggest a simple decision rule. If the budget allows an inter-
nalized reasoning model, use one; agents on top will further improve accuracy. If not, lightweight
workflow optimization (e.g., SPO on GPT-4o-mini) offers the best accuracy per dollar. In either
regime, the choice is not “agents or internalized reasoning models” but “how much of each.” This
trade-off is especially salient in healthcare, where privacy requirements and local infrastructure
constraints may preclude sending sensitive clinical data to frontier proprietary systems 20,50 . In
practice, the key question is which combination of internalized reasoning models and external-
ized workflows best satisfies privacy, compute, latency, and accuracy requirements.
     More broadly, MedicalAgentsBench demonstrates the value of decision-oriented benchmark-
ing that jointly evaluates accuracy, cost, and reasoning quality. By filtering for genuinely difficult
questions and screening for data contamination, our benchmark exposes meaningful perfor-
mance differences that standard medical QA datasets obscure through ceiling effects.


Limitations of the study
Our benchmark draws exclusively from multiple-choice QA datasets derived from educational
resources, which do not capture the full complexity of clinical practice (e.g., longitudinal pa-
tient management, ambiguous presentations, multi-modal evidence integration). Validating these
findings on real-world clinical decision-making tasks remains an important next step.
    The 50% accuracy threshold used for difficulty filtering introduces a potential circularity: the
“hard” subset is defined relative to the specific models evaluated. We mitigate this through
architectural diversity in the model pool and independent human validation of reasoning depth,
but future work should explore task-intrinsic difficulty measures that do not depend on model
performance.
    Our cost comparisons focus on inference-time expense. For proprietary internalized reason-
ing models such as o3-mini, the training cost required to produce these reasoning capabilities
is not publicly disclosed, making it difficult to compare the full end-to-end economic trade-off
between internalizing reasoning during training and externalizing it through agent scaffolding at
inference time.
    Finally, our evaluation covers a fixed set of nine agentic methods. More sophisticated strate-
gies, such as step-wise verification, heterogeneous model ensembles, and dynamic agent col-
laboration, remain unexplored and could alter the comparative landscape between internalized

and externalized reasoning.


METHODS
Evaluation tasks and datasets
MedicalAgentsBench is a carefully curated benchmark designed to evaluate complex medical
reasoning tasks. We draw from eight established medical datasets, described below.

MedQA MedQA 8 is a large-scale open-domain medical question answering dataset collected
from professional board exams such as the USMLE. In our benchmark, we use 1,273 English
multiple-choice questions that test a broad range of clinical and foundational medical knowledge.

PubMedQA PubMedQA 9 is a biomedical question answering dataset constructed from PubMed
abstracts to evaluate reasoning over primary research literature. We use 500 expert-annotated
yes/no/maybe questions, where each item pairs a research question with its corresponding ab-
stract.

MedMCQA MedMCQA 10 is a large-scale multiple-choice dataset built from Indian medical en-
trance exams (AIIMS and NEET PG), covering many subjects and topics. Our benchmark in-
cludes 2,816 questions sampled from MedMCQA, focusing on diverse real-world exam-style
problems.

MedBullets MedBullets 44 comprises USMLE Step 2/3-style clinical questions sourced from
the MedBullets online learning platform. We use 308 challenging case-based questions, each
designed to simulate realistic clinical decision-making scenarios with expert-authored explana-
tions.

MedExQA MedExQA 47 is a benchmark designed to evaluate medical question answering with
a focus on explanation quality across underrepresented specialties. We extract 935 questions
spanning Audiology, Nutrition, Occupational Therapy, Physical Therapy, and Speech-Language
Pathology, each paired with multiple expert explanations.

MedXpertQA MedXpertQA 48 is a recent benchmark targeting expert-level medical knowledge
and advanced clinical reasoning across multiple specialties and body systems. We include 2,450
text-based questions from MedXpertQA, covering both understanding-oriented and reasoning-
intensive scenarios.

MMLU The Massive Multitask Language Understanding benchmark 45 includes several medi-
cally relevant subjects. Following the Med-PaLM setting, we retain only the medical fields clini-
cal_knowledge, professional_medicine, college_medicine, medical_genetics, anatomy, and col-
lege_biology, yielding 1,089 questions.




                                               8
MMLU-Pro MMLU-Pro 46 is an enhanced, more challenging extension of MMLU that empha-
sizes reasoning and harder distractors. We include only subjects whose source (src) belongs to
the health category, specifically clinical_knowledge, professional_medicine, college_medicine,
medical_genetics, nutrition, human_aging, anatomy, and virology, resulting in 818 questions.
   These source datasets vary significantly in size (from 308 to 2,816 questions), average token
length (18.7 to 316.1), and number of options (3 to 10), providing diverse evaluation contexts
(Table 2).


Benchmark construction
As illustrated in Figure 6, we construct MedicalAgentsBench by aggregating questions from mul-
tiple medical QA datasets and applying a multi-stage filtering and verification process to ensure
difficulty, reasoning depth, and clinical validity. Our hard-set selection process is based on three
key criteria:

Model performance distribution As visualized in Figure 7, we analyze the proportion of mod-
els that correctly answer each question (k/N ratio). Questions where less than 50% of models
provide correct answers (left of the dashed line in Figure 7), are categorized as hard candidates.
This ensures our benchmark focuses on truly challenging questions that current models struggle
with.

Dataset sampling strategy We carefully balanced our benchmark to ensure comprehensive
coverage across various source datasets. Our Hard subset includes precisely 100 questions
each from MedQA, PubMedQA, MedMCQA, MMLU-Pro, and MedExQA. From MedXpertQA,
we incorporated both its Reasoning (MedXpertQA-R) and Understanding (MedXpertQA-U) sub-
sets (100 questions each), which were annotated in the original paper to distinguish between
questions requiring complex clinical reasoning versus those primarily testing medical knowledge.
Additionally, we included 89 questions from MedBullets and 73 questions from MMLU. This sam-
pling distribution maintains proportional representation across the source datasets while ensur-
ing sufficient sample size to evaluate model performance across different medical question types
and their associated reasoning requirements.

Reasoning depth We prioritize questions that require multi-step reasoning processes, where
solving the problem involves a sequence of logical deductions or inferences rather than sim-
ple fact retrieval. This type of reasoning is typically demonstrated in long chain-of-thought ap-
proaches, where models must work through several intermediate steps to reach a conclusion.
To systematically assess reasoning depth, we developed a five-level classification rubric and
engaged medical professionals to annotate each question (see the human validation protocol
below for the complete rubric and validation details). The need for such complex reasoning is ev-
idenced by the significant performance gap between base models and agent-based approaches.
As shown in Table 3, while models achieve high accuracy on the Full set (e.g., GPT-4o: 88.2%
on MedQA), their performance drops significantly on our Hard subset (e.g., GPT-4o: 32.2% on
MedQA-Hard), confirming that these selected questions demand deeper analytical processing
and medical reasoning capabilities.
   In summary, we construct MedicalAgentsBench through a multi-stage pipeline designed to
ensure question difficulty, diversity, and evaluation validity. Specifically, we first evaluate each
candidate question across multiple model architectures (as shown in Table 3) to ensure architecture-
independent difficulty. Second, we apply a difficulty-aware dataset sampling strategy to balance


                                                 9
coverage across source benchmarks. Third, we incorporate human annotations to verify that se-
lected questions require multi-step medical reasoning rather than surface-level recall. Finally, we
conduct a data contamination analysis using MELD (Memorization Effects Levenshtein Detec-
tor) 49 to identify questions that may have been memorized during model training. This analysis
involves splitting each test item (including the question text but excluding answer options) into two
halves, providing models with only the first half, and measuring the similarity between their gener-
ated continuations and the original second half. By filtering out potentially memorized items, this
step safeguards the fairness and validity of the benchmark for evaluating genuine medical rea-
soning capabilities. As shown in Figure 3, the Hard subset selected for our MedicalAgentsBench
benchmark consistently demonstrates lower similarity scores (20-40%) compared to the original
datasets, suggesting our filtering process effectively selects questions that test genuine reason-
ing rather than memorization. Complete details of our MELD methodology and its limitations are
discussed in the data contamination analysis under Results. Finally, four medical professionals
(M.D. students) review the final question set to verify clinical relevance and reasoning complexity
through the validation protocol described below. The resulting MedicalAgentsBench benchmark
contains 862 questions with an average token length of 147.4.


Human validation protocol
To ensure the quality and clinical validity of benchmark questions, we conducted a human val-
idation study using four medical students in their clinical years with formal clinical training as
annotators. The validation process served two primary objectives: assessing the level of reason-
ing depth required to answer each question and verifying the correctness and clinical relevance
of the questions. Each annotator received standardized training materials and participated in a
calibration session, during which they jointly reviewed and discussed 10 representative sample
questions to ensure consistent interpretation and application of the evaluation criteria. Follow-
ing calibration, annotators independently reviewed assigned question subsets, with overlapping
samples included to enable estimation of inter-annotator agreement.

Reasoning depth assessment For reasoning depth assessment, we developed a systematic
rubric that defines reasoning depth as the minimum number of distinct cognitive inference steps
required to arrive at the correct answer. Each step corresponds to a discrete inference, calcu-
lation, or clinical judgment that builds upon previously derived information. Supplemental Table
S1 summarizes the five-level reasoning depth classification system and provides representative
clinical examples for each level. Annotators independently reviewed each question, documented
the required reasoning steps, and assigned depth scores according to this rubric. To enable
reliable estimation of inter-annotator agreement, we sampled 20 questions from each dataset
(10 from the original dataset and 10 from the curated hard subset) for comparative annotation by
multiple reviewers.
    Inter-annotator reliability was quantified using Fleiss’ κ, which measures agreement beyond
chance among multiple raters. Specifically, for each item, we compute the proportion of rater
pairs that assign the same category and average this quantity across items to obtain the ob-
served agreement P̄ . The expected agreement P̄e is computed from the squared category fre-
quencies averaged across all ratings. Fleiss’ κ is then defined as

                                                 P̄ − P̄e
                                            κ=            .
                                                 1 − P̄e
Across all datasets, the resulting inter-annotator agreement is κ = 0.62 (Supplemental Table S3),
which is commonly considered substantial agreement for complex cognitive annotation tasks.

                                                 10
Question validity assessment Concurrent with reasoning depth assessment, annotators eval-
uated question validity using strict exclusion criteria. Questions were flagged for removal if they
contained wrong answers contradicting current medical guidelines, lacked sufficient clinical con-
text for definitive answers, tested outdated or clinically irrelevant knowledge that could be rea-
sonably interpreted multiple ways. Supplemental Table S2 summarizes our question exclusion
criteria and the review process.
     Our quality control protocol implemented several measures to ensure annotation consistency.
Following the initial calibration phase, any question flagged by at least one annotator underwent
group review. Questions were removed from the final dataset if two or more annotators agreed
on the validity of the concern, with all exclusions documented with a specific rationale. Of the
initial candidate questions, 21 questions were removed due to validity concerns: 5 for wrong
answers, 5 for poor clinical context, and 11 for clinical irrelevance.

We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of both base models and agentic reasoning methods
across our MedicalAgentsBench benchmark. Our experiments follow a standardized protocol to
ensure fair comparison, with consistent prompting strategies and evaluation metrics across all
models and methods.

closed-source models (GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Claude-3.5-Haiku, o1-mini,

Reasoning methods Additionally, we evaluate 9 distinct agentic reasoning approaches span-
ning three categories. The first category includes baseline prompting methods such as Chain-
of-Thought and Self-Consistency. The second encompasses advanced prompting techniques:
Multi-Persona, Self-Refine, and MedPrompt. The third category covers agent-based frame-
works, including medical-specific collaboration frameworks (MedAgents and MDAgents, which
we reimplemented to ensure accurate parsing of multiple-choice responses) and search-based
agent methods (SPO and AFlow) with search strategies consistent with the original setting. Be-
cause agentic pipelines require extensive multi-step reasoning, evaluating them on all existing
medical benchmarks would be computationally prohibitive. Therefore, Table 1 reports agentic re-
sults solely on MedicalAgentsBench, while Table 2 provides cross-benchmark comparisons for
single-call model evaluations. Our implementations of these baseline methods and evaluation
framework are publicly available at https://github.com/gersteinlab/MedicalAgentsBench/.
    Our data contamination analysis (Figure 3) revealed that OpenAI models demonstrate con-
sistently lower memorization metrics across all datasets compared to other model families. This
finding guided our decision to use GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, and o3-mini as primary models for
method comparison to minimize performance advantages stemming from potential training data
contamination.

Standardized evaluation All experiments utilize identical prompt templates and evaluation
protocols to ensure a fair comparison. We implement a standardized two-round inference pro-
tocol per query for agent-based methods requiring multiple inference rounds (e.g., MedAgents).
Multi-agent approaches (e.g., MDAgents) consistently employ three distinct agent roles for all
evaluations. This standardization mitigates confounding factors arising from implementation vari-
ations, allowing us to more accurately attribute performance differences to the methods them-
selves rather than to differences in their implementations.


Cost analysis
To analyze cost-performance trade-offs, we followed a standardized evaluation protocol. Under-
standing these trade-offs is particularly important given the significant computational resources
required by advanced reasoning models, which often come with substantially higher inference
costs and longer processing times than traditional models. Similarly, multi-agent frameworks
require multiple rounds of API calls for agent interactions, further increasing computational costs
and inference time. In this analysis, we focus exclusively on inference-time cost and latency, as
training costs are highly model-dependent, often proprietary, and difficult to attribute fairly on a
per-task or per-sample basis.
    For API-based commercial models (OpenAI and Claude), we calculated costs using their
published pricing rates based on total token usage (input + output). For open-source models
(such as DeepSeek, Llama, and QwQ), we estimated costs based on Together AI’s hosting
rates (https://www.together.ai/), a platform that provides API access to open-source models.
This approach enables fair cost comparisons between proprietary and open-source alternatives
across different deployment scenarios. The total cost of experimentation was $226.17. We
measured inference time as wall-clock time per sample, including prompt construction and model
inference, across agent-based methods that included their complete interaction cycles.

Supplemental Note S1. Error Analysis
We analyze five representative clinical cases (Tables S4–S8) to characterize failure modes
and success patterns across reasoning paradigms. Each table presents side-by-side reason-
ing traces from base models, reasoning-optimized models, and agent-based systems. Red-
highlighted segments mark critical decision pivots where hypotheses are introduced, overturned,
or revised.

Case 1 (Table S4): Convergent reasoning in IRIS management. An HIV patient on ART
develops worsening pulmonary symptoms despite rising CD4 counts, a classic presentation of
immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS). GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o both misman-
age this case: the former favors drug sensitivity testing, while the latter considers discontinuing
therapy. DeepSeek-R1, by contrast, entertains alternative diagnoses (infection relapse, drug
resistance) during its extended chain-of-thought but systematically eliminates each against the
clinical timeline (symptom onset 4 weeks post-ART, improving immunologic markers). Each re-
evaluation step narrows the differential rather than broadening it, and the model correctly selects
continuation of current therapy (A). The key observation is that deliberation length per se does
not determine accuracy; what matters is whether successive reasoning steps converge toward
or diverge from the strongest hypothesis.

Case 2 (Table S5): Refinement versus consensus amplification in osteomyelitis. A pa-
tient presents with osteomyelitis following a cat bite. The clinically relevant pathogen is Pas-
teurella multocida, which requires ampicillin–sulbactam (B). The initial chain-of-thought selects
vancomycin (E), overweighting generic severity cues (osteomyelitis suggesting MRSA) while ne-
glecting the pathogen-specific exposure history (cat bite indicating Pasteurella).
    Under Self-Refine, the model re-examines pathogen likelihood across refinement rounds and
shifts from broad MRSA coverage to pathogen-directed therapy, ultimately converging on the cor-
rect answer. Under MultiPersona, however, three independent agents generate similar MRSA-
focused rationales. During aggregation, inter-agent agreement reinforces the shared error rather
than correcting it. This distinction is clinically consequential: iterative self-critique recovers from
anchoring bias by forcing re-examination of premises, whereas naive aggregation amplifies cor-
related mistakes when agents share the same reasoning blind spot.

Case 3 (Table S6): Hypothesis reversal in small bowel obstruction. A patient with prior
abdominal surgery and diabetes presents with symptoms consistent with adhesive small bowel
obstruction. GPT-4o and o3-mini correctly identify adhesions (A) through straightforward pattern
matching (surgical history + obstructive symptoms). DeepSeek-R1 initially reaches the same
conclusion but then revisits the differential: the model notes the patient’s diabetes and explores
autonomic neuropathy (B) as an alternative etiology for dysmotility. Without an explicit stop-
ping criterion or confidence threshold, this secondary hypothesis displaces the original, despite
weaker supporting evidence.
    The failure mechanism is distinct from knowledge gaps or reasoning errors in the traditional
sense. The model possesses sufficient clinical knowledge to reach the correct answer and does
so early in its reasoning chain. The error arises from unconstrained hypothesis exploration: ad-
ditional deliberation destabilizes an already-correct conclusion. Compared with Case 1, where
re-evaluation converges, this case shows that the same extended reasoning process can di-
verge when intermediate steps introduce plausible but weaker alternatives without calibrating
their relative likelihood.

Case 4 (Table S7): Disagreement-driven correction in septic shock. A patient in refractory
septic shock requires vasopressor escalation. The single-model chain-of-thought selects hydro-
cortisone (C), reasoning from persistent hypotension to adrenal insufficiency. This represents a
plausible but premature inference that bypasses the standard vasopressor escalation protocol.
    Within MultiPersona, agents initially disagree: some advocate corticosteroids, others argue
for epinephrine as the guideline-recommended second-line vasopressor. Critically, this disagree-
ment triggers explicit comparison of competing clinical rationales rather than simple majority vot-
ing. The synthesized response identifies that vasopressor escalation should precede steroid
therapy per Surviving Sepsis guidelines, converging on epinephrine (B). The correction depends
on structured disagreement: agents with different initial hypotheses are forced to reconcile their
reasoning against shared clinical criteria, exposing the premature inferential leap in the corticos-
teroid rationale.

Case 5 (Table S8): Role-specialized revision in neonatal galactosemia. A neonate presents
with findings consistent with classic galactosemia. Within MedAgents, the initial synthesized re-
port (v0) correctly identifies the diagnosis but introduces a spurious dietary recommendation
(avoiding fruit juice). This is mechanistically incoherent, since galactosemia involves defec-
tive galactose metabolism, not fructose intolerance. Domain-specialized agents (ophthalmology,
hepatology, clinical nutrition) flag this inconsistency: cataracts and hepatotoxicity in galactosemia
are driven by galactitol and galactose-1-phosphate accumulation, not by generic sugar exposure.
The revised synthesis (v1) narrows the intervention to eliminating lactose-containing feeds and
switching to soy-based formula (B), correctly targeting the underlying metabolic defect. The
correction arises not from agent diversity alone, but from the combination of domain-specific
knowledge with an explicit revision mechanism that forces the synthesis to address identified
inconsistencies.

Summary.     Three recurring failure and recovery mechanisms emerge across these cases:

  1. Convergence versus divergence in extended reasoning. Longer chain-of-thought im-
     proves accuracy when successive steps narrow the differential (Case 1), but degrades
     accuracy when unconstrained exploration displaces well-supported hypotheses (Case 3).
     The outcome depends on whether intermediate re-evaluation is convergent or diffusive.

  2. Consensus amplification. Multi-agent aggregation without structured disagreement han-
     dling reinforces shared reasoning errors (Case 2, MultiPersona). When agents share the
     same anchoring bias, agreement increases confidence in the wrong answer.

  3. Structured correction. Both iterative self-critique (Case 2, Self-Refine) and role-specialized
     disagreement resolution (Cases 4–5) can recover from reasoning errors, but only when the
     correction mechanism forces re-examination of specific premises rather than generating
     independent rationales from the same prior assumptions.

   These patterns are consistent with our quantitative findings: stronger base reasoning reduces
the marginal value of agentic scaffolding, but targeted correction mechanisms (disagreement
detection, premise re-examination, domain-specialized review) remain beneficial when they ad-
dress specific failure modes rather than adding undifferentiated deliberation.

 Question: A 4-week-old boy is brought to the pediatrician by his parents for an initial evaluation. His parents are concerned that he is not feeding well and has lost weight over the last 2 weeks.
 He was born at home via spontaneous vaginal delivery at 37 weeks of gestation to a G1P1 mother. His mother did not receive prenatal care. His blood pressure is 70/44 mmHg, pulse is
 151/min, and respirations are 41/min. His weight is 3 kg (4th percentile) compared with 3.5 kg at birth (45th percentile). On physical exam, he is jaundiced with an enlarged liver and spleen. He
 does not appear to respond to visual stimuli, and further examination reveals bilateral clouding of the lens. Which of the following interventions could have avoided this patient’s symptoms?
 Options: A. Avoiding fruit juice and sweetened foods; B. Changing to a soy-based formula; C. Providing imiglucerase enzyme replacement; D. Removing phenylalanine from maternal diet
 during pregnancy; E. Vitamin B6 supplementation.
 Correct Answer: B (Changing to a soy-based formula).
 Clinical Explanation: This patient who presents with failure to thrive, hepatosplenomegaly, and bilateral cataracts most likely has classic galactosemia. Patients with this disorder should avoid
 lactose-containing products by changing to a soy-based formula. Classic galactosemia is an autosomal recessive defect in galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase. This enzyme is involved in
 the conversion of galactose to glucose, and a deficiency of this enzyme results in the accumulation of galactose 1-phosphate in the liver, kidney, and brain. This metabolite acts as a phosphate
 sink, meaning that it traps all free phosphate in the cytosol and inhibits the formation of other phosphate-dependent metabolites such as adenosine triphosphate. This results in failure to thrive,
 vomiting, lethargy, and hepatosplenomegaly after ingestion of galactose-containing products such as milk. Patients will also develop bilateral cataracts if the deficit is not detected in the first few
 weeks after birth. Avoiding galactose by changing to a soy-based formula will avoid the complications of this disorder. Turck discusses using soy protein for infant feeding. The author notes that
 there are safety concerns with regards to the high phyto-estrogenic isoflavone content of these formulas. The author recommends that despite these safety concerns, these formulas should still
 be used in patients with classic galactosemia.

 multisystem involvement (growth, liver, and eyes) strongly suggest a congenital metabolic disorder unmasked by routine feeding rather than an isolated infectious process. The most worrisome
 elements are the early age, progressive weight loss, and evolving liver disease, which together indicate an ongoing toxic exposure.
 Medical Genetics Expert: The triad of hepatosplenomegaly, jaundice, and neonatal cataracts is highly characteristic of classic galactosemia due to GALT deficiency. Without newborn screening

 and also reduced to galactitol; these metabolites disrupt hepatic function and lens transparency. The key concern is that continued galactose intake rapidly leads to liver failure, sepsis (often

 coagulopathy, and sepsis. The most urgent issue is that the same nutrient (lactose/galactose) driving liver injury is still present in each feed, making prompt dietary modification central to both
 treatment and prevention.
