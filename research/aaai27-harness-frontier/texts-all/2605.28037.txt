                                                     Prompt-based personality control is a key technique for designing large language model (LLM)
                                                 dialogue agents that behave consistently and appropriately across social contexts. However, speci-
                                                 fying Big Five personality traits (BFTs) in a prompt does not necessarily ensure that the intended
                                                 traits are expressed in generated utterances. This paper investigates this mismatch from an in-
                                                 teractionist perspective, viewing personality expression as a context-dependent outcome shaped by
                                                 the interplay between trait specification and situational factors. Specifically, we analyze how per-
                                                 ceived BFT expression in LLM-generated dialogue is influenced by three prompt factors: assigned
                                                 personality traits, dialogue roles, and expressive styles. Using a factorial design that systematically
                                                 combines six personality conditions, three role conditions, and three expressive-style conditions, we
                                                 generate 1,080 LLM-agent dialogues in each of English and Japanese. We then evaluate the target
                                                 agent’s utterances using an LLM-as-a-judge framework to estimate expressed Big Five traits. The
                                                 results show that expressed personality is shaped not only by explicit trait specification, but also by
                                                 dialogue role and expressive style. These effects are trait-specific: dialogue role strongly influences
                                                 Openness, expressive style substantially shapes Conscientiousness and Agreeableness, and explicit
                                                 trait specification dominates Neuroticism. Moreover, even in the absence of explicit personality-trait
                                                 specification, social and expressive conditions induce distinct personality-like impressions. Cross-
                                                 linguistic comparisons show broadly similar personality-expression patterns between English and
                                                 Japanese dialogues, with noticeable differences emerging only under specific combinations of per-
                                                 sonality, role, and expressive style. These findings suggest that personality control in LLM agents
                                                 should be understood not as a direct consequence of trait prompting, but as a context-dependent
                                                 process involving the interaction of personality specification, social role, and expressive style.

studies have shown that prompt-based personality modulation does not always lead to the intended
behavioral changes, and that internally specified BFTs may not be expressed in generated utterances
as intended (Zakazov et al., 2024; Nagao et al., 2025). These findings reveal a central challenge for
controllable LLM agents: specifying a personality trait in a prompt does not necessarily ensure that the
corresponding trait will be expressed in dialogue.
A possible reason for this mismatch is that personality expression is shaped not only by explicit trait
specification, but also by the social conditions under which dialogue takes place. For example, an agent
instructed to be open-minded may express this trait differently when engaging in casual chat than when
acting as a salesperson in a task-oriented interaction. Similarly, an emotional or rational expressive
style may change how the same personality prompt is perceived. Although prior studies have examined
persona prompting, intrinsic personality traits, roles, and emotional expression in LLMs, these factors
have largely been analyzed in isolation. As a result, it remains unclear how assigned personality traits
are expressed under different combinations of dialogue roles and expressive styles.
In this paper, we address this issue by analyzing personality expression in LLM-generated dialogue
from an interactionist perspective. As illustrated in Figure 1, our approach contrasts with prior factor-
wise analyses that examine control factors separately. Specifically, we analyze how internally specified
personality traits interact with two dialogue-level social factors: the agent’s role and expressive style. Our
analysis is grounded in interactionist theories of personality (Sheldon et al., 1997; Wood and Roberts,
2006; Mischel, 2009; Fleeson, 2001; Mischel, 2013; Fleeson and Jayawickreme, 2015), which view behavior
not simply as a direct manifestation of internal traits, but as an outcome of interactions between trait
dispositions and social context. From this perspective, personality expression in LLM dialogue may
depend not only on which traits are specified, but also on the role assigned to the agent and the expressive
manner in which it is instructed to respond.
We operationalize personality expression using the Big Five framework, one of the most widely used
models of personality, comprising Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neu-
roticism. Using a factorial design, we systematically combine six personality conditions, three role
conditions, and three expressive-style conditions, and generate dialogues between LLM agents in both
English and Japanese. The target agent’s utterances are then evaluated using an LLM-as-a-judge frame-
work to estimate expressed Big Five traits. This design allows us to examine not only whether explicit
trait prompts affect expressed personality, but also how their effects are modulated by dialogue roles and
expressive styles, and whether these patterns are broadly shared across languages.
Our results show that personality expression in LLM-generated utterances should be understood not
only as a consequence of explicit trait prompts, but also as an outcome shaped by dialogue-level social
factors. This paper makes the following contributions:

  1. We introduce an interactionist framework for analyzing personality expression in LLM-generated
     dialogue, focusing on the joint effects of prompt-specified Big Five traits, dialogue roles, and ex-
     pressive styles.
  2. We provide a systematic factorial analysis showing that personality expression is trait-specific and
     context-dependent: the relative influence of personality specification, role, and expressive style

       differs substantially across Big Five dimensions.
    3. We show that role and expressive style shape expressed Big Five traits even in the absence of
       explicit personality-trait specification, indicating that personality-like impressions can emerge from
       dialogue-level social and expressive conditions.
    4. We compare English and Japanese dialogues and find broadly similar personality-expression pat-
       terns, with noticeable differences emerging under specific combinations of personality, role, and
       expressive style.

Taken together, our findings suggest that personality expression in LLMs can be usefully analyzed from an
interactionist perspective: expressed traits are not simply direct reflections of internal trait specifications,
but are shaped by the social conditions in which dialogue takes place. This underscores the importance
of situating personality control in LLMs within broader social and linguistic contexts.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes our analysis methodology.
Section 3 presents the results. Section 4 discusses the findings. Section 5 reviews related work. Finally,
Section 6 concludes the paper.


2     Method
Figure 2 provides an overview of our analysis method. We first generate dyadic dialogues between pairs
of a target LLM agent and an interlocutor LLM agent. We then analyze how the prompt configurations
influence the generated dialogue. For each dialogue, an LLM judge evaluates the extent to which the
target agent expresses each Big Five trait using a five-point Likert scale.


2.1     Prompt Factors

We manipulate three prompt factors assigned to the target agent: personality condition, role, and
expressive style. Taken together, these factors allow us to examine how explicitly specified personality
traits, dialogue roles, and expressive styles shape conversational behavior. The interlocutor agent is
assigned only a role, allowing us to examine how the target agent’s assigned attributes manifest across
different conversational contexts.


Personality. To examine how explicitly specifying a Big Five trait shapes the generated dialogue, we
define six personality conditions. The Unspecified condition serves as the baseline, in which no Big Five
trait is specified. In each of the five trait-specific conditions, one Big Five trait is specified as high, while
the remaining traits are left unspecified.
Specifically, we consider the following six personality conditions:

    1. Unspecified: no Big Five trait is specified.
    2. Extraversion: high Extraversion is specified.
    3. Agreeableness: high Agreeableness is specified.
    4. Conscientiousness: high Conscientiousness is specified.
    5. Neuroticism: high Neuroticism is specified.
    6. Openness: high Openness is specified.


Role. We consider three roles to examine how personality-related expressions vary between uncon-
strained and socially constrained interactional settings. The Chat role does not impose a specific task
objective and is therefore treated as the role baseline. We select the Salesperson role as a represen-
tative example of a socially constrained role and include the Customer role as the counterpart in the
Salesperson–Customer pairing.


                                                       3
Specifically, we consider the following three roles:

  1. Chat: a chat partner.
  2. Salesperson: a salesperson at a consumer electronics retail store.
  3. Customer: a customer visiting a consumer electronics retail store.

The resulting role pairings are Chat–Chat, Salesperson–Customer, and Customer–Salesperson.


Expressive Style. We define three Expressive Style conditions to examine how explicitly specifying
an expressive orientation influences the generated dialogue. The Unspecified condition serves as the
expressive-style baseline, in which no expressive style is specified. In the remaining conditions, either
an emotional or a rational expressive style is specified. We operationalize expressive style using two
prompt-based conditions, Emotional and Rational, which contrast affectively oriented and cognitively
oriented forms of expression. This contrast is motivated by established theoretical frameworks, including
the stereotype content model in social cognition (Fiske et al., 2002) and dual-process theory in cognitive
psychology (Kahneman, 2011), both of which distinguish affective from cognitively oriented aspects of
social expression. Specifically, we consider the following three expressive style conditions:

  1. Unspecified: no expressive style is specified.

  2. Emotional: an emotionally expressive style is specified.
  3. Rational: a rationally expressive style is specified.


Prompt design. The prompt formulations used to operationalize the Big Five traits follow Jiang et al.
(2023) and are provided in Appendix A. These formulations use the same trait-descriptive adjectives
as Jiang et al. (2023), originally taken from McCrae and John (1992). The prompt formulations for
expressive styles are provided in Appendix B. An example prompt for the Salesperson role in an English
dialogue under the Extraversion and Emotional conditions is provided in Appendix C.


2.2    LLM-based Personality Evaluation

We adopt an LLM-as-a-judge paradigm for personality assessment. Our goal is not to measure the target
agent’s “true” personality, but to estimate the Big Five traits perceived from its generated utterances.
This use of LLM-based evaluation is motivated by prior work showing that LLM-based personality
judgments can align with human personality judgments at levels comparable to, or exceeding, ratings
by familiar human informants (Wright et al., 2026). Although our setting differs in that we assess
personality traits expressed in dialogue rather than self-reported personality, this evidence supports the
use of LLM-based ratings as a scalable proxy for human judgments of perceived personality expression.
We therefore interpret the resulting scores as estimates of perceived personality expression, rather than
as direct psychometric measurements. The prompt used for personality evaluation is presented in Ap-
pendix D. To assess the robustness of these estimates to evaluator choice, we compare ratings from two
independent evaluator models in Section 3.1.


2.3    Analysis Setup

For dialogue generation, we used GPT-5.2 (gpt-5.2-2025-12-11), and for dialogue evaluation, we used
Gemini 2.5 Flash (gemini-2.5-flash). We used a separate evaluation model to mitigate self-preference
bias (Zheng et al., 2023), whereby a model may assign overly favorable judgments to outputs resembling
its own generation style.
Each dialogue consisted of 10 turns. For each combination of role, personality condition, and expressive
style condition, we generated 20 dialogues. Dialogues were generated in both English and Japanese,



                                                       4
whereas the evaluation prompt was written in English and applied uniformly to dialogues in both lan-
guages.
The evaluation focused exclusively on the target agent. For each setting, Big Five trait scores were
assessed for the target agent in each of the 20 generated dialogues. The means of the resulting scores
were visualized using radar charts. Neuroticism scores were converted into Emotional Stability scores by
subtracting them from 6 prior to visualization. This transformation ensures consistent axis directionality,
such that higher values indicate more positive trait expressions across all axes. For the ANOVA analyses,
each generated dialogue was treated as one observation, rather than aggregating scores at the condition
level. Thus, for each two-way ANOVA, we analyzed 2 × 3 × 20 = 120 observations, yielding 114 residual
degrees of freedom. For each three-way ANOVA, we analyzed 2 × 3 × 3 × 20 = 360 observations, yielding
342 residual degrees of freedom. These dialogue-level observations correspond to repeated stochastic
generations under the same prompt condition, and the resulting statistical tests should therefore be
interpreted as analyses of variation across generated dialogue samples rather than as measurements from
independent human participants.
We analyze the effects of personality condition, role, and expressive style on Big Five trait expression in
LLM agents. Specifically, we conducted the following analyses:

Effect of Personality. To examine the direct effect of explicit Big Five trait specification, we evaluated
     Big Five trait expression under the Chat role, which serves as the role baseline, and the Unspecified
     expressive-style condition.
Joint Effects of Personality and Role. To examine the joint effects of Personality and Role, we
     compared Big Five trait scores under the Unspecified expressive-style condition across Personality
     and Role conditions.
Joint Effects of Personality and Expressive Style. To examine the joint effects of Personality and
     Expressive Style, we compared Big Five trait expression across combinations of Personality and
     Expressive Style conditions, under the Chat role condition.
Joint Effects of Personality, Role, and Expressive Style. To examine the joint effects of Person-
     ality, Role, and Expressive Style, we compared Big Five trait expression scores across all combina-
     tions of these conditions.
Comparison Between English and Japanese Dialogues. To examine cross-linguistic differences,
   we compared the evaluation results for English and Japanese dialogues under corresponding exper-
   imental conditions.


3     Results

3.1    Robustness of LLM-based Evaluation

Before presenting the main results, we examined whether the estimated Big Five scores were robust
to the choice of LLM-based evaluator. In addition to the primary evaluator, Gemini 2.5 Flash, we re-
evaluated the English dialogues using OpenAI o3-mini. For each of the 54 Personality–Role–Expressive
Style conditions, we computed the mean score for each Big Five trait over 20 dialogue samples for each
evaluator, and calculated the RMSE between the two sets of condition-level means. We also computed
Pearson’s correlation coefficients at the individual-dialogue level. As shown in Table 1, RMSE values were
relatively small across all five traits, and Pearson’s correlations indicated moderate-to-strong agreement
between the two evaluator models. These results suggest that the estimated trait scores were not strongly
dependent on the particular LLM-based evaluator used in this study.
However, this analysis should be interpreted only as evidence of inter-evaluator consistency among LLM-
based judges. It does not establish that the estimated scores are equivalent to human judgments of
personality expression. Accordingly, we treat the resulting scores as LLM-based estimates of perceived
personality expression, rather than as validated measurements of how human annotators would perceive
the same dialogues.

3.2    Effect of Personality Condition

Mean Big Five Trait Scores

Figure 3 shows the expressed Big Five trait scores in English dialogues for the Chat role under the
Unspecified expressive-style condition. Figure 3a shows the scores under the Unspecified personality
condition, which we regard as a baseline reflecting the default expressive characteristics of LLMs.
Compared with the Unspecified personality condition, specifying a Big Five trait generally increased
the score of the corresponding expressed trait. This pattern was especially clear for Openness and
Neuroticism, whereas it was less pronounced for Conscientiousness and Agreeableness. These results
suggest that prompt-specified personality conditions can influence perceived Big Five trait expression,
but that the strength of this influence varies across trait dimensions. These baseline scores are used
in the following sections as reference points for interpreting the joint effects of Personality, Role, and
Expressive Style.

3.3    Joint Effects of Personality and Role

Mean Big Five Trait Scores

Figure 4 presents the expressed Big Five trait scores in English dialogues under the Unspecified expressive-
style condition, separately for each Personality and Role condition. Each panel corresponds to one
Personality condition, and the three lines indicate the Chat, Salesperson, and Customer roles.
The results show that role assignment shaped expressed Big Five traits even when no explicit Big Five
trait was specified. Under the Unspecified Personality condition, Conscientiousness was higher in the
Salesperson and Customer roles than in the Chat role, whereas Openness was higher in the Chat role
than in the Salesperson and Customer roles. This pattern suggests that purchase-related interactions
encouraged more goal-directed and responsibility-oriented responses, whereas unconstrained chat allowed
more flexible and exploratory responses.
The effect of Role also depended on the Personality condition. Role-related differences were relatively
small under the Unspecified, Extraversion, and Agreeableness conditions, but became more pronounced
under the Openness, Conscientiousness, and Neuroticism conditions. These observations suggest that role
assignment does not simply add a uniform contextual effect, but modulates trait expression differently
depending on the specified Personality condition.

3.4    Joint Effects of Personality and Expressive Style

Mean Big Five Trait Scores

Figure 6 presents the expressed Big Five trait scores in English dialogues under the Chat role condition,
separately for each Personality and Expressive Style condition. Each panel corresponds to one Person-
ality condition, and the three lines indicate the Unspecified, Emotional, and Rational expressive-style
conditions.
The results show that Expressive Style systematically shaped perceived Big Five trait expression, even
when no explicit Big Five trait was specified. Under the Unspecified Personality condition, the Emotional
style increased Agreeableness and decreased Conscientiousness and Emotional Stability, whereas the
Rational style increased Conscientiousness and decreased Extraversion and Openness. This pattern
suggests that expressive-style instructions can induce personality-like impressions by making the agent’s
responses more affectively expressive under the Emotional condition and more cognitively oriented under
the Rational condition.

Table 3 presents the results of two-way ANOVAs examining the effects of Personality and Expressive

trait dimension as the dependent variable. In each analysis, Personality had two levels, Unspecified
and the corresponding dimension-specific condition, and Expressive Style had three levels, Unspecified,
Emotional, and Rational.
The ANOVA results confirm that the relative contributions of Personality, Expressive Style, and their
interaction differed substantially across Big Five dimensions. For Openness, Personality showed the
largest effect, ω 2 = 0.730, whereas the effect of Expressive Style was comparatively small, ω 2 = 0.055,
and the interaction was not significant. This indicates that Openness expression was primarily driven
by explicit Openness specification rather than by expressive style.
For Conscientiousness and Agreeableness, Expressive Style showed the largest effects, ω 2 = 0.638 and
ω 2 = 0.454, respectively. These results indicate that perceived Conscientiousness and Agreeableness were
especially sensitive to whether the agent responded in a Rational or Emotional style. For Extraversion,
both Personality and Expressive Style had substantial effects, suggesting that Extraversion expression
was shaped jointly by explicit trait specification and expressive orientation.
For Neuroticism, Personality had the largest effect, ω 2 = 0.731, but Expressive Style and the Personality
× Expressive Style interaction also showed significant effects. As noted in Table 3, the identical values
for the Expressive Style main effect and the interaction reflect the fact that Neuroticism scores under
the Neuroticism personality condition were fixed at the maximum value across expressive-style condi-
tions. Thus, the observed expressive-style effect for Neuroticism was attributable to variation within the
Unspecified personality condition.
Overall, these results show that Expressive Style can substantially alter perceived personality expres-
sion, but its influence is trait-specific. While explicit Personality specification dominated Openness and
Neuroticism, Expressive Style played a central role in shaping Conscientiousness and Agreeableness.

4     Discussion
Main Findings. Our analysis yielded three main findings. First, expressed Big Five traits in LLM-
generated dialogue were not determined solely by explicit personality-trait specifications. Role and
Expressive Style also systematically shaped perceived trait expression, even when no Big Five trait was
explicitly assigned. Second, the relative importance of Personality, Role, and Expressive Style differed
substantially across Big Five dimensions. Personality specification was the dominant source of variation
for Openness and Neuroticism, whereas Expressive Style had the largest effects on Conscientiousness,
Extraversion, and Agreeableness. Third, the effect of personality specification depended on the dialogue


                                                                                  15
context. In particular, the Personality × Role and Personality × Expressive Style interactions showed
that the same personality prompt could be expressed differently depending on the assigned role and
expressive style. Although the three-way interactions were statistically significant, their effect sizes were
modest compared with the dominant main effects and two-way interactions. Thus, the central finding is
not simply that all three factors interact, but that the structure of expressed personality differs across
trait dimensions.


Trait-specific Patterns of Personality Expression. The observed effects suggest that each Big
Five dimension is sensitive to different aspects of the dialogue setting. Openness was strongly influ-
enced by both Personality and Role: explicit Openness specification increased expressed Openness, but
the magnitude of this expression also depended on whether the agent was placed in an unconstrained
chat setting or in a more task-oriented retail interaction. Neuroticism, by contrast, was dominated by
Personality specification, suggesting that prompts specifying high Neuroticism produced a strong and
relatively stable effect across roles and expressive styles. Conscientiousness and Agreeableness were espe-
cially sensitive to Expressive Style. Rational expression tended to increase perceived Conscientiousness,
whereas Emotional expression tended to increase perceived Agreeableness. Extraversion was influenced
by all three factors, suggesting that its expression is shaped jointly by explicit trait specification, social
role, and expressive orientation. These trait-specific patterns indicate that personality control in LLMs
cannot be understood as a uniform effect of trait prompting; rather, each trait dimension appears to
interact with contextual factors in a distinct way.


Personality in Social Situations. These findings support an interactionist view of LLM personality
expression. In this view, expressed traits are not direct readouts of internal personality prompts, but
context-dependent outcomes shaped by the interplay between trait specifications and social conditions.
The present results suggest that role and expressive style can amplify, suppress, or redirect the expression
of a specified personality trait. Importantly, the form of this contextual modulation depends on the target
trait dimension. This trait-specific pattern is consistent with interactionist accounts of personality,
which emphasize that behavioral expression depends on both dispositional tendencies and situational
constraints.


Implications for Controllable Dialogue Agents. The results have practical implications for the
design of LLM-based dialogue agents. If personality control is implemented only by specifying Big
Five traits in prompts, the resulting utterances may not consistently express the intended traits across
different roles or expressive styles. For example, a trait specification that is effective in an unconstrained
chat setting may be weakened or transformed in a task-oriented role. Similarly, an expressive-style
instruction may shift perceived personality even in the absence of explicit trait specification. Therefore,
controllable agent design should treat personality, role, and expressive style as jointly operating control
factors rather than as independent prompt components. In particular, personality-controlled agents
should be evaluated under the specific social roles and expressive conditions in which they are intended
to operate.


Cross-linguistic Differences. The comparison between English and Japanese dialogues showed gen-
erally limited but condition-dependent cross-linguistic differences. This suggests that language does not
uniformly determine personality expression in LLM agents. Rather, language may modulate expressed
traits in interaction with role and expressive style under specific conditions. For example, differences
were observed in emotionally expressive chat and in rational salesperson interactions under high Neu-
roticism. These findings indicate that multilingual evaluation is important, but they also suggest that
cross-linguistic effects should be interpreted cautiously and in relation to specific social and expressive
contexts.


From Prompt-based to Latent-space Control. Although this study focused on prompt-based
control, the interactionist perspective has implications for other approaches to controlling LLM behavior,
including latent-space steering. Recent work has explored methods for controlling personality or style
through internal representations (Frising and Balcells, 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2026). However, if
personality expression depends on role and expressive style, then steering a single personality dimension


                                                     16
in isolation may not be sufficient to produce stable behavior across contexts. Future work should therefore
examine whether the joint effects observed here are reflected in the latent representations of LLMs and
whether such representations can support more robust, context-sensitive personality control.


Cognitive Perspectives. Our findings do not imply that LLMs possess personality in the psycholog-
ical sense. Rather, they show that LLM-generated utterances can produce personality-like impressions
that vary systematically across dialogue contexts. Even when the same personality specification was
used, perceived Big Five traits changed depending on the assigned role and expressive style. This pat-
tern is analogous to the situation-dependent expression of personality traits in humans, where the same
individual may express different aspects of personality across different social roles. A related interaction
between Big Five traits and social roles in humans has also been reported by Sheldon et al. (1997).
Thus, interactionist personality theory provides a useful framework for analyzing how socially situated
prompt conditions shape the personality-like impressions conveyed by LLM-generated dialogue. More
broadly, these limited parallels suggest that comparative cognition may offer a productive perspective
for studying socially situated behavior in computational agents (Voudouris et al., 2025).


5    Related Work
Before the advent of LLMs, work on personality and persona control in dialogue systems followed
several representative paradigms. Early approaches controlled personality-related expression through
overgenerate-and-rank frameworks based on statistical models of personality and alignment (Isard et al.,
2006), or through explicit generation parameters derived from psycholinguistic correlates of personality,
such as verbosity, polarity, self-references, hedges, and lexical choice (Mairesse and Walker, 2007). Later
neural approaches shifted the focus toward persona-grounded dialogue, where responses are conditioned
on speaker profile sentences to improve persona consistency (Zhang et al., 2018). These approaches differ
in whether they control personality or persona through statistical ranking, explicit linguistic parameters,
or profile-based conditioning.
The advent of LLMs has substantially changed this design space. Rather than relying primarily on
hand-engineered generation parameters, task-specific rankers, or supervised persona-conditioned dia-
logue models, LLM-based systems can be steered through natural-language instructions, role descrip-
tions, in-context demonstrations, retrieved memories, or preference-based optimization. This allows
personality to be specified at a higher level of abstraction, while relying on the pretrained model to
realize the corresponding linguistic, pragmatic, and interactional patterns. However, this flexibility also
reduces transparency and controllability: it is often unclear which linguistic cues give rise to a perceived
personality, whether the model will maintain the intended persona across turns, and how personality
specifications interact with task goals, safety constraints, and other prompt attributes.
Recent work on LLM-based personality and persona control has therefore examined both how desired
personalities can be induced and how their expression should be evaluated. Existing approaches span
prompting-based methods, training-based methods such as supervised fine-tuning and preference op-
timization, and representation-level steering methods. Since our study focuses on prompting as a
lightweight and widely used control mechanism, we primarily review prompting-based work. Within
prompting-based research, prior studies have developed along several parallel strands, as summarized in
Table 7.
One line of work investigates whether LLMs can be specified and evaluated along human personality
dimensions. Jiang et al. (2024) analyzed stories generated by LLMs prompted with Big Five traits, using
human evaluation, LLM-based evaluation, and Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC) (Pennebaker
et al., 2001). Serapio-Garcı́a et al. (2025) examined LLM behavior under demographic, contextual, and
linguistic variation using psychometric instruments such as the Big Five Inventory (BFI) (John et al.,
1991) and the International Personality Item Pool–NEO (IPIP-NEO) (Goldberg et al., 2006).
Beyond explicit personality traits, another body of work shows that social specifications can shape LLM
behavior. Persona and role assignments have been shown to influence response style, decision-making
tendencies, and downstream task performance. Chen et al. (2024) surveyed persona-based prompting
and classified personas into demographic, character-based, and individualized categories. Kong et al.
(2024) investigated role-play prompting as a zero-shot method for improving LLM reasoning, showing

Expressive style provides another source of variation that may interact with perceived personality. Reif

Although these studies show that personality traits, roles, styles, and affective cues can each shape
LLM behavior, they typically examine these factors in isolation. In contrast, we adopt the interactionist
perspective of Fleeson and Jayawickreme (2015), which views personality expression as emerging from the
interaction between internal dispositions and situational factors. From this perspective, prompt-specified
Big Five traits are not sufficient explanations of observed behavior: their expression may be amplified,
attenuated, or transformed by the agent’s role, expressive style, and dialogue context. Building on this
view, we examine how Big Five trait prompts manifest in LLM-generated dialogues across roles, styles,
and languages.


6    Conclusion
This study investigated how expressed Big Five traits in LLM agents are shaped by personality specifi-
cations, social roles, and expressive styles. Our results show that LLM personality expression is context-
dependent: personality specification, role, and expressive style each substantially modulate expressed
traits, and their effects interact with one another. These findings support an interactionist view of LLM


                                                        18
personality expression and highlight the importance of modeling the joint effects of personality, role, and
expressive style in socially situated interactions. Future work should examine how these joint effects
are represented in the latent space of LLMs and whether such representations can support controllable
dialogue generation.


Limitations
Evaluation and model dependence. This study evaluated personality expression using LLM-based
judges rather than human annotators. The inter-evaluator comparison between Gemini 2.5 Flash and
OpenAI o3-mini showed only small discrepancies, supporting the robustness of the LLM-based evaluation.
However, this comparison does not establish full psychometric validity and does not eliminate the need
for future human validation. The resulting scores should therefore be interpreted as LLM-based estimates
of perceived Big Five trait expression, rather than as direct psychological measurements. Future work
should validate these judgments against human annotations and replicate the analysis across multiple
generation and evaluator models.


Scope of roles, styles, and prompts. This study considers a limited set of roles, expressive styles,
and prompt formulations. The Salesperson and Customer roles were instantiated in a consumer-electronics
retail scenario, while the Chat role served as an open-domain baseline. Thus, effects attributed to role
may partly reflect task structure, goal orientation, or domain-specific conversational norms. In addition,
the expressive-style conditions were limited to Emotional and Rational styles, and the Big Five, role,
and style prompts represent only one possible prompt design. We therefore interpret our findings within
the examined task and prompt settings, and leave broader evaluation across additional roles, domains,
styles, and prompt formulations to future work.


Language and dialogue setting. The cross-linguistic comparison was limited to English and Japanese,
and the same English evaluation prompt was used for both languages. This design enables a controlled
comparison, but may introduce bias when evaluating Japanese utterances. In addition, the dialogues
were generated through agent–agent interaction rather than interaction with human users. Future work
should examine additional languages, language-specific or multilingual evaluation prompts, and human–
LLM dialogues in real deployment contexts.
