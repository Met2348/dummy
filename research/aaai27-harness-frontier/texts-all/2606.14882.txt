                                         1   Introduction
                                         As robotic systems transition from controlled laboratory settings to real-world deployment, they increasingly
                                         face dynamic, communication-constrained environments that demand adaptability, robustness, and generaliza-
                                         tion. Consider a disaster-response scenario in which a mobile manipulator navigates damaged indoor areas to
                                         retrieve objects or clear obstacles, a drone inspects elevated or obstructed regions from the air, and a ground
                                         robot flexibly explores accessible areas to gather local spatial information and support team collaboration.
                                         In such settings, communication bandwidth may be limited, and centralized cloud connectivity may be
                                         intermittent or unavailable. What’s more, task conditions may evolve unexpectedly as goals change, access
                                         becomes restricted, and team members fail or join during execution. These characteristics make heterogeneous
                                         multi-robot collaboration both essential and highly challenging.
                                         Rule-based pipelines and tightly coupled models often fail to scale under uncertain goals, diverse constraints,
                                         and evolving teams. In this context, LLMs present a transformative opportunity. Their rich semantic
                                         understanding, strong logical reasoning, and powerful generalization capabilities Wei et al. (2022) allow robots
                                         to comprehend high-level task intent and infer implicit constraints. These capabilities also help robots adapt

with in-context learning to improve long-horizon reasoning while respecting bounded context windows.

the proposed framework on a benchmark with diverse tasks, dynamic variations, and team configurations, and
further validate it in real-world experiments. The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:

2      Related work

2.1     Semantic Instruction Grounding for Robotics Planning and Control
Recent efforts have integrated LLMs into robotic task and motion planning by leveraging their rich semantic
knowledge and strong reasoning capabilities. This integration has demonstrated notable improvements in
robotic manipulation, autonomous navigation, and human–robot interaction Jansen (2020); Sharma et al.
(2022); Huang et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022); Ichter et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2023). LLMs are increasingly
employed to interpret high-level instructions, perform hierarchical task decomposition and planning, and
enable end-to-end generation of low-level actions Huang et al. (2022); Ichter et al. (2023); Singh et al. (2023);
Liang et al. (2023). SayCan Ichter et al. (2023) combines pre-trained skills with value functions to select
appropriate actions for embodied tasks. Code as Policies (CaP) Liang et al. (2023) uses structured code
generation for robot control, while VoxPoser Huang et al. (2023a) employs 3D Value Maps and LLM-generated
code for manipulation tasks, with re-planning capabilities in case of failure. The methods discussed above
highlight the substantial potential of LLMs in advancing robot task planning.
Furthermore, integrating LLMs with other modality encoders, such as those for images and point clouds,
opens up new opportunities for advancing robotic task execution. RoboBrain2.0 BAAI RoboBrain Team et al.
(2025) develops a multimodal embodied foundation model that emphasizes perception-driven cognition for
question answering, enabling robots to integrate vision and language for reasoning about affordances, spatial
relations, and interactive tasks. Video foundation models, such as Veo 3 Wiedemer et al. (2025), demonstrate
strong zero-shot capabilities in segmentation, affordance recognition, tool use simulation, and visual reasoning,
which provide promising foundations for enhancing robots’ multimodal perception and embodied cognition.
Building on the synergy between LLMs and multimodal perception, recent works have further enhanced
robotic autonomy through closed-loop feedback mechanisms and dynamic re-planning strategies. TREE-
PLANNER Hu et al. (2024) optimizes the closed-loop feedback process through plan sampling, action tree
construction, and grounded decision-making. Huang et al. Huang et al. (2023b) presented a general formulation
of Inner Monologue that combines different sources of environmental feedback with methods fusing LLM
planning with robotic control policies. HiCRISP Ming et al. (2023) allows robots to identify and correct
errors at each step of the task. The re-planning for closed-loop feedback in COME-robot systems Zhi et al.
(2024) for mobile robots has been primarily focused on the robotic arm, without fully exploiting the inherent
mobility of the robot. This oversight limits the robot’s effectiveness in tasks such as grasping and transporting
objects. Compared to these methods, DynaHMRC emphasizes decision-making and planning tasks for multiple
heterogeneous robots by integrating closed-loop feedback specifically associated with robotic capabilities with
LLMs. As a result, DynaHMRC is able to better leverage the complementary strengths of different robots,
thereby accomplishing complex tasks that cannot be completed by a single robot alone.

2.2     Multi-Agent Organization and Heterogeneous Robot Collaboration
The study of multi-agent systems (MAS) has a rich history Stone and Veloso (2000); Koga and Latombe
(1994). MAS can be classified into two types: homogeneous, where agents share similar characteristics, and
heterogeneous, where agents have diverse capabilities Dorri et al. (2018).

2.2.1   Organization and role learning in MARL.

Beyond LLM-based robotic task execution, organization and role assignment have been widely studied in
MARL for cooperative Dec-POMDPs. A representative line of work learns latent or explicit organizational
structures to improve coordination and scalability. ROMA Wang et al. (2020) learns emergent latent roles
through stochastic role embeddings, encouraging agents with similar roles to share behaviors while specializing
in different subtasks. RODE Wang et al. (2021) further decomposes the joint action space into restricted
role action spaces and learns a role selector, forming a bi-level hierarchy that improves scalability and
transfer. MAVEN Mahajan et al. (2019) introduces a shared latent variable for hierarchical control, enabling
temporally extended and committed exploration for long-horizon coordination. More closely related to our
leader mechanism, SOG Shao et al. (2022) proposes self-organized grouping with conductor election and
message summary to improve generalization under dynamic team composition and partial observability.
These methods are structurally related to our role allocation and leader election mechanisms. However,
they typically operate within the MARL policy-learning paradigm, requiring predefined state/action spaces,
reward functions, and extensive environment interaction or centralized training. In contrast, DynaHMRC
operates at the semantic task-planning level, using LLMs to reason over natural-language task descriptions,
robot self-descriptions, textual feedback, and dynamic task constraints. The roles and leader in DynaHMRC
are therefore not latent variables optimized through policy learning, but semantic and embodiment-aware
coordination structures generated through LLM reasoning and prompt conditioning. In this sense, our work
is complementary to MARL organization methods: it brings role-aware and leader-mediated organization into
LLM-based heterogeneous robot collaboration without training a new low-level MARL policy for each task.

2.2.2   Homogeneous Agents

Sampling-based methods and their various algorithmic improvements have been proposed as a widely used
approach in homogeneous agents Karaman and Frazzoli (2011); Dobson and Bekris (2015). Li et al. Li et al.
(2017) tackled the optimal synchronization in homogeneous multi-agent systems using an actor-critic neural
network and least squares to approximate the control policy and value function. Zhang et al. Zhang et al.
(2020) use a non-policy reinforcement learning algorithm to compute each agent’s optimal control policy. Guo
et al. Guo et al. (2024) places greater emphasis on the organizational framework of LLM agents, investigating
how to harness the potential of LLMs to develop more effective collaboration strategies. CoELA Zhang
et al. (2024) leverages LLMs to establish a collaborative framework for homogeneous agents, facilitating
cooperative exploration, transport, and communication between the two agents. Although CoELA adopts a
decentralized architecture, it does not explicitly address heterogeneous embodiment grounding, dynamic task
specifications, or global consistency in long-horizon tasks. Consequently, when task goals, access constraints,
or team composition change during execution, such designs are prone to communication redundancy and
limited adaptability.

2.2.3   Heterogeneous Agents

Compared to homogeneous agents, heterogeneous agents present different challenges, including the hetero-
geneity of agents, limited view of the environment, and the dynamicity of the multi-agent system (MAS) or
environment Chen and Wang (2005). Several heterogeneous robot combinations have already been proposed,
including aerial-ground collaboration, main-picket collaboration, and humanoid-quadruped collaboration Lis-
sandrini et al. (2020); Yue et al. (2021); Haldane et al. (2014); Wallace et al. (2020). VIKI-R Kang et al.
(2025) fine-tunes vision–language models with reinforcement learning to frame heterogeneous multi-robot
collaboration as a visual–language reasoning task. IRoT-LLM Liu et al. (2024b) applied LLMs to ad hoc and
original heterogeneous robots to generate reasonable collaboration strategies. Centralized LLM frameworks
for heterogeneous robot collaboration include EMOS Chen et al. (2025), which employs embodiment-aware



                                                      4
descriptions with LLM-based agents to orchestrate perception, navigation, and manipulation, and COHER-
ENT Liu et al. (2025), which decomposes and allocates complex tasks for efficient execution. COHERENT
relies on aggregating diverse information sources from multiple robots. As the scale of the robot team increases
and the LLM input context grows, the overall collaboration process becomes increasingly fragile, which may
result in redundant, inconsistent, or infeasible action proposals. This issue is further exacerbated in dynamic
task settings, where changes in task conditions amplify these limitations.
By contrast, our work leverages large language models to construct a decentralized framework for heterogeneous
multi-robot collaboration. The framework enables efficient closed-loop collaboration through role allocation
and reflective reasoning, allowing scalable support for varying numbers of robots within the evaluated settings
and flexible adaptation to diverse task variations. Furthermore, to address the lack of domain-specific data
and the limited specialization of existing open-source models, we collect task-relevant data on the benchmark
and fine-tune open-source models. This enables us to achieve performance that surpasses state-of-the-art
(SOTA) closed-source models on domain-specific tasks, while incurring only modest adaptation costs.

Our research focuses on how to effectively use LLMs to execute collaborative tasks in heterogeneous multi-robot
systems. The goal is to enable generalization across diverse scenarios, varying numbers and configurations
of robots, as well as changes in task goals and constraints. This research falls within the domain of robot
task and motion planning (TAMP). In our implementation, the DEC-POMDP variables are instantiated
through textual prompts, feedback, and executable robot interfaces. Each robot’s local observation oti ∈ Oi
is translated into natural language by a description function fi : Oi → L, lit = fi (oti ), where L denotes the
natural language space. The observation includes task-relevant scene information, robot status, task progress,
and messages received from other robots. The physical action component AF   i corresponds to the robot-specific
atomic action set, while the communication component AM       i is implemented by the communicate action.
After each action, textual feedback reports the execution outcome, including success, failure causes, constraint
violations, and task-progress updates. The feedback is stored together with action and message histories in the
bounded memory hti . The LLM policy πi then maps the textual observation, memory, feedback, and current
leader plan to the next executable atomic action, thereby grounding the formal DEC-POMDP abstraction in
the prompt-based planning loop of DynaHMRC .

considers the differences in capabilities among collaborators can help the team complete more complex tasks
efficiently, while the introduction of a leadership role within the team can improve collaboration efficiency and
reduce redundant actions. App.2.2 and App.2.3 provide the corresponding prompt designs.

3.3     Closed-Loop Execution and Self-Reflection in Heterogeneous Robot Teams
We propose a closed-loop execution and reflection framework based on LLMs to enable collaboration among
heterogeneous multi-robot systems. The framework consists of four functional modules: the Observation
Module, the Memory Module, the Planning Module, and the Reflection Module. The overall workflow
is as follows: the Observation Module and the Memory Module respectively provide perceptual inputs oti
and historical context hti . Based on this information, the Planning Module generates the next action in a
next-action prediction manner. This action is then executed in the environment, producing new feedback f bti .
The Planning Module continuously incorporates the updated perception, historical data, and feedback in a
closed-loop fashion to plan subsequent actions,

3.3.1   Heterogeneous Multi-Robot Collaboration Stage


Observation Module.

This module encompasses all the state information necessary for robot tasks and motion planning. It mainly
includes the following three parts:
   i. Scene Graph: This part presents structured information about the environment. The scene graph contains
      the positions and orientations of key furniture items. For each piece of furniture, at least one navigation
      target point is defined for “navigate" action. This setting is similar to Szot et al. (2022); Gu et al.
      (2023); Makhal and Goins (2018). In addition to this static information, the scene graph incorporates
      dynamic updates, including the open/closed status of manipulable furniture, newly discovered objects,
      and changes in their positions and orientations.
  ii. Messages Information: To achieve effective collaboration in completing tasks, the robots must share
      information and request assistance as needed. All communication behaviors and message contents are
      autonomously initiated and structured by the robots themselves, guided through prompts. For example,
      a manipulation robot (Bob) may request assistance from robots equipped with mobile manipulation
      capabilities to transport objects on the table that are beyond its operational reach; a mobile manipulation
      robot (Alice) may receive specialized requests to operate on articulated objects; a drone robot (Lucy)
      may be asked to explore elevated or hard-to-reach areas; and a mobile robot (David) often proactively
      shares newly discovered information with other collaborators in a timely manner. The communication
      paradigm among robots is inherently flexible, allowing for unicast, multicast, or broadcast. Each robot
      independently selects the appropriate mode based on its operational context.
  iii. Robot Information: This part includes the different capabilities of the robot and the corresponding status
       information. For robots equipped with navigation capabilities, the status includes the robot’s pose
       information. For robots with manipulation capabilities, the status encompasses the gripper’s open or
       closed state, the name of the grasped object, and the maximum grasping range.
Memory Module.

In the context of our work, the tasks are designed as long sequences. As the LLMs are queried multiple times,
they may lose track of prior tasks, making it essential to retain past decision contexts. The memory module is
designed to capture three key components:
   i. Feedback History (f hti ): This section primarily stores limited feedback generated from the robot’s past
      interactions with the environment. We have designed various types of textual feedback for our tasks,
      covering both success and failure cases. For a more comprehensive description of the feedback, please
      refer to Tab. 1.
  ii. Received Message History (mhti ): This part primarily archives messages previously received by the robot
      from other robots.
  iii. Action History (ahti ): This component primarily archives the sequences of decision-making actions
       previously executed by the robot. The action types and formats strictly follow the designed atomic
       action set, as shown in Tab. 2.
In addition, multiple queues are employed to store different types of historical information, each preserving
the latest K steps of its respective interaction history. The historical context is formally defined as follows:

Planning Module.

We employ LLMs to simulate role-playing for various types of robots, guiding task planning through a Chain-
of-Thought (CoT) framework. This process involves selecting and executing one action from a predefined list


                                                          9
at a time. After each action, feedback is obtained, and based on this feedback, along with historical data, the
robot autonomously determines the next subtask or adjusts the plan. This multi-turn interaction enables
robots to collaborate effectively in completing long-horizon tasks.
In mobile manipulation, identifying an optimal base position is critical, as it significantly influences the
success of grasping actions Shao et al. (2025); Duan et al. (2025). To address this, we propose a strategy that
combines the “navigate” and “move” actions to accomplish the task. For instance, when grasping an apple
from a table, the robot first selects a navigation target from the scene graph and moves near the table. It then
evaluates the validity of the robotic arm’s initial and target configurations relative to the apple’s position
and attempts to grasp the object. If the attempt fails, the robot selects alternative target points closer to
the apple, navigates to those positions, and reattempts the action. Should the task remain incomplete, the
robot refines its position using the “move” action, adjusting its base location according to the relative x and y
coordinates between the base and the apple.

In the planning module, the strategy of next-action prediction based on feedback in a closed-loop manner

      observations along with various historical data, and summarize successful experiences and lessons to
      inform its subsequent task planning.

3.4     Complexity Discussion
We briefly analyze the framework in terms of context growth and communication complexity. Let n denote
the number of robots, H the task horizon, ∆t the discussion interval, and Ksel the maximum number of


                                                                10
voting rounds. With bounded history queues, increasing the number of task objects mainly enlarges the task
specification and execution horizon, so the total planning workload grows approximately linearly with H.
In the one-time initialization stages, each robot reasons over information from the other agents, so the
per-agent context grows as O(n) and the total team-wide processing cost grows as O(n2 ). Leader election
requires at most Ksel rounds, with O(Ksel n) logical messages in total, or O(Ksel n2 ) message deliveries under
broadcast semantics. During ordinary execution, planning remains local to each robot, while periodic group
discussion introduces only O(n) communication every ∆t steps. Thus, compared with centralized planners
whose single-model context continuously grows with global team information, our framework keeps most
computation local and only performs low-frequency synchronization.

3.5     Data Construction and Model Adaptation
Our framework leverages the rich semantic knowledge and powerful reasoning capabilities of LLMs to achieve
strong few-shot performance in dynamic tasks. However, current LLMs are predominantly trained on internet-
scale corpora, which fall short of the specialized requirements of heterogeneous multi-robot collaboration.
Due to inherent hallucinations and the absence of domain-specific training data, their action outputs often
remain unstable, inaccurate, and inefficient for task execution. This limitation persists even with mitigation
strategies such as CoT prompting and other prompt engineering techniques. Sec. 4.5.5 provides a detailed
discussion. Moreover, in real-world robotic scenarios, the limited computational resources of edge devices
impose additional constraints on model size and inference speed. To address these challenges, we collected a
domain-specific dataset for heterogeneous multi-robot collaboration in simulation environments and fine-tuned
an open-source LLM.

3.5.1   Data Generation Pipeline

To efficiently gather diverse data, we propose a dual-source action generation framework that integrates
both human-designed and LLM-generated scripts. These action sequences are executed across a variety of
environments and tasks within the proposed collaboration framework, where environmental constraints enforce
the validity of actions. Trajectories that successfully complete the tasks are retained and constitute the
foundation of our dataset. The overall data collection pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 3.
   i. Human-Designed Action Scripts: Human experts carefully design action sequences based on task re-
      quirements. These scripts prioritize efficiency by minimizing redundant steps and yielding shorter
      trajectories. As reliable expert demonstrations, they provide consistent baselines for effective multi-robot
      collaboration. Nevertheless, the reliance on predefined patterns limits their diversity, which constrains
      the model’s adaptability to varied environments and unexpected conditions.
  ii. LLM-Generated Action Scripts: We employ GPT-4o to automatically generate action scripts under the
      same task specifications. Compared with human-designed scripts, these sequences offer greater diversity,
      including variations in action ordering, division of labor, and execution strategies, thus broadening the
      action distribution. However, such diversity may introduce suboptimal or infeasible actions, requiring
      human judgment and subsequent cleaning. This human-in-the-loop process ensures, while enriching
      diversity, the resulting dataset also preserves efficiency and reliability for multi-robot collaboration.
Beyond collecting trajectories, we transform them into step-wise decision-making instances. At every step,
we record the system prompt and user prompt, which encapsulate task background, goals, historical and
current states, executed actions, and feedback from the environment. Along with the ground-truth action,
these prompts are fed to GPT-4o to generate the corresponding reasoning process. The dataset we collected
for model training consists of 30,390 samples. We provide the validation loss learning curves under different
training data sizes in App.12.
This design serves two purposes: (1) it enriches the dataset with explicit decision rationales, enabling training
that combines action supervision with reasoning supervision, and (2) it aligns with the principle of the test-time
scaling law. By structuring each step with progressively richer context and generating detailed reasoning
traces, the model is encouraged to leverage longer chains of thought and deeper contextual information at
inference time, thereby improving performance without additional parameter updates. To demonstrate the



                                                       11
importance of the two supervision signals, we conduct an ablation study in App.11, comparing reasoning-only
supervision, action-only supervision, and their joint supervision.

4.2          Baselines
In this work, we establish two main categories of baselines: traditional heuristic planning algorithms
(MCTS, MCTS-NoINS ) and generative model–based approaches (COHERENT, CoELA, DynaHMRC-GPT4o,
DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B ). We do not include classical decentralized coordination methods as direct baselines
because they rely on substantially different problem formulations from ours. In particular, such methods
typically require explicit task decomposition, hand-crafted utility/reward design, and structured symbolic
state abstractions, whereas our setting emphasizes long-horizon heterogeneous collaboration from semantic
task descriptions, textual feedback, and dynamically changing task conditions. Constructing a fair classical
baseline under our setting would therefore require substantial manual engineering, which would confound the
comparison. All methods are carefully optimized to maximize their performance across different experimental
settings, ensuring fairness in comparison. During model inference, we set the temperature parameter to 0.5
to strike a balance between stability and diversity in action sequence generation. This value ensures the


                                                                                                  15
rationality of the planning outcomes while preserving a degree of explorativeness, thereby enabling adaptation
to complex and dynamic task scenarios. It is worth noting that all of our experiments were designed to
ensure feasibility under the corresponding settings. In other words, we avoided scenarios where the task itself
would be inherently impossible to complete. For example, tasks requiring aerial object manipulation were not
assigned to robot configurations lacking a drone. This design choice guarantees the fairness and validity of
the experimental comparisons. In all experiments, the memory queue length is set to Ml = 10 by default. We
further investigate the effect of a larger memory size (Ml = 15) on the experimental results in App.7.
   • MCTS : We used Monte Carlo tree search Browne et al. (2012) with human prior knowledge, operating
     on global observations of all agents and exploring their joint action space. Since navigation actions
     dominate the search space and reduce efficiency through repetition, we apply a heuristic: prioritize
     non-navigation actions when the previous action was navigational and non-navigation options exist.
     We further improve efficiency by pruning infeasible actions (e.g., grasping out-of-reach objects) and
     restricting actions to those relevant to target objects.
   • MCTS-NoINS : We implemented a variant of the MCTS algorithm that removes the human instruction to
     further validate the performance of the basic MCTS. Except for this modification, all other experimental
     settings, including the pruning strategy and action space constraints, remained identical to those of the
     MCTS baseline.
   • COHERENT : The COHERENT Liu et al. (2025) employs a centralized, high-level LLM-based planning
     architecture. The system derives task and semantic scene representations from global observations; the
     LLM orchestrates high-level planning, producing the complete set of action commands for all robots at
     every environment timestep, while the robots solely execute the issued actions. The LLM has access to
     global robot observations and the complete global interaction history.
   • CoELA: The CoELA Zhang et al. (2024) employs a decentralized, cognitive-inspired framework where
     LLMs perform high-level planning and communication, while dedicated modules manage low-level
     perception and execution. This design leverages the LLM’s reasoning capabilities for complex multi-
     agent tasks in decentralized settings.
   • DynaHMRC-GPT4o: Based on our proposed heterogeneous multi-robot collaboration framework, task
     understanding, reasoning, and planning are performed using the closed-source model GPT-4o.
   • DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B : We employ the open-source Qwen3-4B-Instruct model for the same processes
     within our framework. This model is fully fine-tuned on the dataset we constructed to enhance its
     performance on domain-specific tasks. Furthermore, the relatively moderate scale of 4B parameters
     endows the model with promising feasibility for deployment on resource-constrained robotic edge devices.
     The detailed training configurations of Qwen3-4B are provided in Tab. 4. The sensitivity analysis of
     training hyperparameters is provided in App.16.

4.3   Task Variations and Constraints
Building upon the three primary task categories, we additionally introduce four types of variations during
task execution to evaluate the robustness of the proposed framework in adapting to dynamic task conditions:
   • Changes in the overall task objective (CTO): Changes in the number and types of target objects may
     necessitate additional adaptations, particularly when constraints exist among the objects, in order to
     achieve the task objectives.
   • Introduction of temporary restricted zones (IRZ): If a team member is temporarily restricted from
     accessing a specific area, the framework must reassign the corresponding exploration responsibilities to
     other collaborators to ensure successful task execution.
   • Addition of new collaborators (ANC): The addition of new collaborators requires a reallocation of task
     responsibilities. The self-description of a newly joined collaborator is broadcast to all existing team
     members.
   • Removal of existing collaborators (REC): The removal of collaborators likewise demands a redistribution
     of tasks to maintain overall performance. To simulate unexpected failures, robots are deliberately


                                                      16
      disconnected from the team without any prior indication.
In robotic tasks, fixed constraints play a crucial role alongside dynamic task variations. A notable example
is the capability limitations induced by heterogeneous robot configurations. Beyond this, we introduce an
implicit constraint into the task: only manipulation robots are permitted to execute the final step, such
as placing sandwich ingredients onto the cutting board, while other robots are restricted from placing any
objects there. This constraint is not explicitly defined in advance; instead, robots receive feedback only upon
violating the constraint through their actions. We regard this implicit rule as a form of experiential learning,
showcasing the adaptive learning capabilities of the collaborative framework.

4.4    Evaluation Metrics
We introduce five metrics to evaluate the performance of different methods:
    • Success rate (Succ): The rate of episodes in which the robot completes the full task, meaning all objects
      are placed in the correct positions.
    • Partial success rate (PS): The average ratio of correctly placed objects to the total number of objects
      per episode. This metric reflects task difficulty: as the number of objects and sequence length increase,
      the task becomes more challenging, but individual failures have less impact. In contrast, tasks with
      fewer objects are more sensitive to single failures.
    • Average temporal steps (TS): the average number of temporal steps in all episodes.
    • Average action steps (AS): the average number of action steps in all episodes, excluding wait action.
      The robot’s capability to execute wait action at optimal moments can reduce energy consumption,
      demonstrating that the policy has a comprehensive understanding of the entire task.
    • Average communication cost (CC): the average number of communication events per episode, defined as

4.5.1   Static Comparative experiments

To thoroughly validate the effectiveness of our framework, we conduct a series of combinatorial experiments
across different settings, including varying scenarios, task types, difficulty levels, and robot configuration
combinations. The experiments are performed under both traditional baselines and decentralized approaches
driven by LLMs. Tab. 3 presents the detailed experimental results, where T = 0 indicates that no dynamic
changes were introduced during the task execution. The experiments involve combinations of 2˘4 robot
types, including manipulation (Ma), mobile (Mo), mobile manipulation (MoMa), and drone (UAV) robots.
Specifically, the configurations are “Ma-MoMa” and “Ma-UAV” for two types, “Ma-MoMa-Mo”, “Ma-MoMa-
UAV”, and “Ma-Mo-UAV” for three types, and “Ma-MoMa-Mo-UAV” for four types.
Tab. 3, Fig. 5, and Fig. 6 present the performance of different methods under static task conditions, across
variations in task type, task difficulty, and robot configuration combinations. For each task setting, we


                                                        21
randomly sample three target object sets and repeat each experiment three times to reduce LLM hallucination
and improve reproducibility, and the average performance is reported. Consequently, the “2 types of robot”, “3
types of robot” and “4 types of robot” settings involve 18, 27, and 9 trials, respectively, ensuring statistical
reliability and robustness of the results. Overall, DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B demonstrates the best comprehensive
performance. Compared with other baseline methods, it achieves higher task and partial success rates while
requiring fewer steps to complete the tasks, particularly in terms of action and communication steps, and
demonstrates greater overall stability in the experimental results. This reduction in steps is crucial for
robotic tasks, as fewer steps directly correspond to lower energy consumption. Compared with CoELA, which
also leverages GPT-4o, the DynaHMRC-GPT4o algorithm achieves higher overall and partial success rates;
however, it does not demonstrate a significant advantage in terms of task-step efficiency across different
scenarios and tasks. These results highlight the necessity of collecting domain-specific task data and training
corresponding models.
The baseline algorithm MCTS-NoINS performs poorly across all metrics and achieves only limited success on
simple tasks. This weakness stems from the absence of action guidance to prune search tree branches, leaving
the search space excessively large and making it difficult to find optimal solutions within limited time. In
contrast, MCTS shows relatively better performance, achieving success rates comparable to DynaHMRC-
Qwen3-4B on simpler tasks with fewer robots. However, the performance of all MCTS-based methods is
fundamentally constrained by the size of the search space. Experimental results reveal a steep decline in
performance as task difficulty increases and the number and heterogeneity of robots grow—sometimes dropping
to zero. This trend is clearly reflected in the SUCC and PS subfigure of Fig. 6, where the orange and blue
bars decrease steadily as the number of robots increases.
In contrast, LLM-based methods maintain more stable performance across the evaluated robot configurations,
suggesting better scalability within the small-to-medium heterogeneous team settings studied in this work.
Unlike MCTS, which suffers from exponential growth in search space due to centralized joint modeling, the
decentralized role-playing framework assigns each robot an independent LLM agent. This allows reasoning
and decision-making to proceed in parallel from local perspectives, while lightweight communication enables
effective collaboration. Such a design naturally supports heterogeneous robots without explicit joint modeling,
yielding improved scalability and robustness trends in both quantity and diversity dimensions. We further
demonstrate the generalization capability of DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B to unseen target objects under static
tasks of medium difficulty in App.13.
Moreover, we observe that MCTS-based methods perform particularly poorly on the Sort Solids task,
compared with the other two task categories. This degradation arises because the task introduces an
additional requirement of matching six distinct colors, which dramatically enlarges the search space. In
contrast, LLMs-based methods leverage their strong semantic understanding capabilities to handle such
challenges effectively. To further enhance the capability of MCTS in handling semantic tasks, the comparative
analysis experiments with the LLM-MCTS baseline are provided in App.17.

4.5.2   Dynamic Comparative experiments

To further assess adaptability, the impact of dynamic task conditions on algorithmic performance is investigated
under the medium-difficulty setting. Tab. 5 - 8 and Fig. 7 illustrate the performance of different algorithms
under various dynamic task conditions.
When task objectives change during execution, the success rate of MCTS increases slightly from 26.5%
to 30.4%, indicating that its search process can partially adapt to goal shifts. Nevertheless, its overall
performance remains unsatisfactory. In contrast, LLM-based methods demonstrate substantially stronger
performance, with DynaHMRC-GPT4o achieving the highest success rate of 83.5%. Interestingly, the results
show that DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B underperforms DynaHMRC-GPT4o in the CTO task, despite consistently
outperforming it in the other three tasks. To account for this discrepancy, we empirically identify an
efficiency–robustness trade-off emerging in heterogeneous multi-robot collaboration: efficient strategies can
complete tasks quickly under static goals, but their strong structural commitments to initial objectives make
them less adaptable when goals change dynamically. In such cases, additional backtracking is required to
correct mismatches, limiting adaptability. In contrast, less efficient strategies progress more slowly but benefit
from delayed commitment, enabling smoother transitions to new goals when changes occur, thereby exhibiting
stronger robustness. For example, in the sandwich-making task, once the base ingredient changes, the more
layers stacked before the change, the more steps are required to remove the top ingredients, amplifying the
adaptation cost.
Under restricted-area conditions, the performance gap among methods becomes particularly pronounced in
the IRZ task. The success rate of MCTS drops sharply to 12.4%, with a partial success rate of only 16.9%. By
contrast, CoELA maintains 77.2%, DynaHMRC-GPT4o achieves 84.9%, and DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B further
improves to 98.2%. This large discrepancy arises because temporary regional constraints are introduced only
through textual feedback, where navigation actions into restricted areas are passed into the simulator but
do not modify the environment state. As a result, search-based algorithms such as MCTS, when lacking
additional pruning strategies, repeatedly revisit the same nodes, thereby degrading performance. Conversely,
LLM-based methods, by leveraging their semantic understanding and reasoning capabilities, can exploit these
constraints to effectively reduce the exploration space and improve task performance.
When new robots are introduced during collaboration, MCTS achieves only a 23.2% success rate, reflecting
its limited dynamic scalability. CoELA remains stable at 75.9%, DynaHMRC-GPT4o improves to 77.2%,
and DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B reaches 87.4%. These findings highlight that the decentralized collaboration
framework offers clear advantages in scaling to heterogeneous teams, while fine-tuned models demonstrate
strong specialization for domain-specific tasks.

4.5.3        Centralized vs. Decentralized LLM-Based Comparative Experiments

To better demonstrate the advantages of our LLM-based decentralized framework over the centralized approach
in handling long-context and multi-objective tasks, Tab. 9 and Tab. 10 present a comparative evaluation
among COHERENT, DynaHMRC-GPT4o, and DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B across various robot configurations
and task types.
Overall, our decentralized method DynaHMRC exhibits consistently more competitive performance compared
to the centralized baseline COHERENT. As the number and configuration of robots increase, DynaHMRC
maintains robust adaptability and stable performance across different settings, whereas COHERENT suffers
from performance degradation with the growth of robot count and context length.
An interesting observation is that although COHERENT sometimes achieves the best results on the AS metric,
its overall task success rate remains low. Our analysis suggests that COHERENT ’s single-pass reasoning
must simultaneously attend to numerous task-related factors, which reduces both the efficiency and accuracy
of its outputs. This leads to repetitive wait actions, further lowering the AS metric.
In addition, we emphasize that the advantage of DynaHMRC is not merely due to shorter prompts. Although
centralized planners can be improved through decomposition, summarization, and staged calls, centralized
coordination still relies on a compressed global task state. In long-horizon heterogeneous tasks, robot-specific
information such as reachability constraints, local occlusions, failed grasps, blocked navigation targets, newly
observed objects, or embodiment-specific limitations may be compressed away, misinterpreted, or become
stale before the next global decision. This can lead to repeated infeasible actions, delayed reaction to changed
task goals, redundant assignments, or embodiment-mismatched action allocation.


                                                                                        26
In contrast, DynaHMRC preserves first-person planning at the robot level. Each robot reasons over its own
observation, memory, execution feedback, and embodiment description, while periodic discussion provides
lightweight global synchronization. Thus, the benefit of DynaHMRC comes from combining local embodiment-
grounded planning with low-frequency team-level coordination, rather than only reducing context length.
A representative COHERENT failure case is provided in App.18, where an incorrect centralized progress
estimate causes global idling and delayed recovery for a missing target object, illustrating how a centralized
belief can become a single point of failure, with one error affecting the behavior of all robots.
In addition, we conducted a simple token usage analysis using tiktoken. The average number of tokens
per inference for DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B is approximately 1,099, while COHERENT requires around 2,227
tokens—more than twice as many. This result indicates that decentralized planning can reduce the single-call
context burden, while the total episode-level token usage is further analyzed in App.19 and App.20.

4.5.4   Ablation Experiments

To comprehensively evaluate our framework and models, we design two types of ablation studies: (1) Inference
model ablation. We replace GPT-4o with alternative closed-source and open-source models to serve as the
reasoning core of the overall framework. (2) Framework component ablation. Building on the fine-tuned
Qwen3-4B-Instruct in our work, we conduct comparative experiments by ablating individual modules, including
history, feedback, leader, and reflection. These two sets of studies allow us to disentangle the contributions
of the central reasoning model and the auxiliary components, thereby clarifying their respective roles in
enhancing efficiency, adaptability, and robustness of heterogeneous multi-robot collaboration.
Tab. 11 presents the experimental results for these ablation studies. Across five task regimes, DynaHMRC-
Qwen3-4B achieves the best overall performance, with an average success rate of 86.1%. Compared with
DynaHMRC-GPT4o, it improves SUCC by 8.7% while reducing TS, AS, and CC by 20.1%, 28.2%, and
62.9%, respectively; relative to Claude, SUCC increases by 11.3% with 25.8%, 28.8%, and 54.8% reductions
in the same metrics. The advantages are most pronounced under IRZ, indicating strong adaptability to
textual constraints and re-planning. In the CTO, although DynaHMRC-GPT4o achieves slightly higher
SUCC, DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B compensates with fewer robotic steps, reflecting the fine-tuned model’s domain
expertise. For ANC and REC, DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B consistently achieves the highest success rates and the
fewest robotic steps, highlighting its robustness to team expansion and reduction.
Ablation studies confirm the necessity of each component. Removing feedback causes the largest drop in
task success rate and number of task steps, as robots lose the ability to assess execution outcomes, breaking
the closed-loop control. Removing history or reflection degrades performance particularly under CTO and
IRZ, underscoring their role in preserving contextual continuity and mid-horizon correction. Introducing a
lightweight leader election slightly increases communication but improves decision alignment and reduces
redundant actions, validating its effectiveness in decentralized collaboration. These results collectively validate
the effectiveness of DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B in achieving efficient, robust, and adaptive collaboration across
dynamic heterogeneous multi-robot scenarios.

4.5.5   Empirical Study

  Although the proposed system framework enables efficient and robust collaboration among heterogeneous
multi-robot teams, residual issues still emerge during execution due to the intrinsic hallucination tendencies
of LLMs and the limited domain expertise of proprietary models in specialized environments. These residual
problems can compromise task success and efficiency, ultimately degrading overall system performance. The
main failure modes are summarized as follows:
   i. State–Task Misalignment. Robots exhibit insufficient awareness of the current task state and may continue
      performing actions or communications toward subgoals that have already been completed. For example,
      even when the bowl has already been placed inside the tray, the drone continues to communicate with
      the manipulation robot, reminding it to execute the “place bowl” action.
  ii. Progress–Goal–Experience Fusion Gap. Robots fail to integrate prior observations and their own execution
      experiences into consistent reasoning about the current task progress and goal set, leading to off-topic
      behaviors and redundant queries. For instance, the mobile robot continues to navigate aimlessly after


                                                        27
        identifying and reporting the target object’s location; the mobile manipulation robot repeatedly queries
        teammates about task progress despite having completed its assigned transportation; and manipulators
        occasionally grasp objects not included in the designated target list.
  iii. Myopic Redundancy. During task execution, robots demonstrate repetitive and short-sighted behaviors.
       Some repeatedly send identical messages to collaborators; mobile robots oscillate between a limited set of
       locations, indicating restricted exploration ability; and manipulators execute consecutive pick-and-place
       actions on the same object multiple times.
  iv. Capability Grounding Error. Robots show incomplete understanding of their teammates’ action feasibility
      and capability boundaries, resulting in infeasible or inefficient delegation requests. For example, a
      drone may request a manipulation robot to open a refrigerator door even though the tabletop-fixed
      manipulator cannot reach the target or perform such an action.
  v. Over-Constrained Division of Labor. Robots tend to rigidly adhere to their initial task assignments and
     fail to act autonomously even when capable of contributing to other subtasks, leading to systemic idle
     waiting and reduced overall efficiency. For example, a mobile manipulation robot strictly follows its
     designated role of object transportation and passively waits for the mobile robot to complete exploration,
     instead of proactively assisting in the collaborative search process.
  vi. Spatial/Affordance Misbinding. Robots show limited understanding of spatial positioning, reachability,
      and container–support relations, leading to inefficient interactions and delayed responses even under
      graspable conditions. For example, a mobile manipulation robot may require several base adjustments
      before grasping, or attempt to open furniture prematurely, while a manipulation robot may hesitate
      despite the target being within reach.
 vii. Object-Level Contention. Multiple robots may simultaneously initiate actions on the same object, causing
      conflicts or behavioral reversals. For example, after a mobile manipulation robot places an object
      within the manipulation robot’s reachable range, it redundantly performs another pick action, directly
      conflicting with the manipulator’s grasping operation.
viii. Communication Inefficiency. Although prompts are designed to encourage LLMs to generate communicate
      actions only when necessary, inter-robot communication remains noisy. Robots frequently send redundant
      or repetitive messages with low information value, leading to inefficiencies in collaborative reasoning.
Through systematic observation of these experimental processes and detailed analysis of the identified failure
modes, we aim to inspire future research toward more adaptive, grounded, and communication-efficient LLM-
based heterogeneous multi-robot collaboration systems. Guided by these observations, we collect targeted
datasets and, building on the collected data, further develop data-driven methods to address or mitigate the
aforementioned issues.

5    conclusion and future work
In this paper, we presented DynaHMRC , a decentralized framework that leverages LLMs to enable robust
and adaptive collaboration among heterogeneous multi-robot systems under dynamic task conditions. By
formulating the problem within the DEC-POMDP paradigm and integrating reasoning-driven communication
and feedback-based replanning, our approach achieves continuous collaboration and resilience in the face
of evolving task goals, fluctuating team compositions, and environmental constraints. Through extensive
simulations and real-world experiments, DynaHMRC demonstrates superior performance in success rate,
efficiency, and generalization compared with baselines, validating the effectiveness of LLM-based reasoning
for decentralized robotic collaboration. Although the current benchmark evaluates teams with 2–4 robot
types, scaling DynaHMRC to substantially larger teams, such as tens of robots, remains an important future
direction. Such settings may require hierarchical coordination, message filtering, summary compression, and
more efficient communication protocols to prevent discussion overhead from growing with team size.
Looking forward, we plan to extend this framework toward broader real-world deployments and more diverse
modalities. Future work will focus on leveraging multimodal models to better perceive and interpret success
and failure during task execution, thereby generating more diverse and informative feedback. In addition, we
plan to adopt reinforcement learning paradigms to fine-tune LLMs, enhancing their long-horizon reasoning
and self-correction capabilities, enabling robots to autonomously refine their cooperative strategies from
interaction data. We believe that such advances will bridge the gap between high-level language reasoning and
low-level robotic control, paving the way for scalable embodied intelligence in complex, causally unstructured
environments.

.3                           Sensitivity of the Task Difficulty Metric
The coefficients in Eq. 9 are intended as an interpretable heuristic rather than an optimized fit. To examine
the sensitivity of this design choice, we re-evaluated the matched subset of 38 tasks (114 runs) by varying the
coefficients in Eq. 9. To isolate the effect of the object-count term, we scanned β while keeping α = γ = (1−β)/2.
We then measured the consistency between the resulting difficulty score D and the empirical task difficulty,
where the latter is summarized by both the original performance metrics and the composite empirical hardness
term Hemp = z(1 − PS) + z(TS). Here, z(·) denotes z-score normalization, which standardizes each metric
to have zero mean and unit variance across the evaluated tasks. This normalization makes 1 − PS and TS
comparable despite their different scales, allowing them to be combined into a composite empirical hardness
measure.
The results are summarized in Tab. 13. Across β ∈ [0.0, 0.8], the score remains consistently aligned with
empirical task hardness: ρ(D, Hemp ) stays within [0.785, 0.807], and ρ(D, TS) stays within [0.856, 0.921].
In addition, object count is more strongly associated with temporal steps than with partial success in the
same matched subset: ρ(object count, TS) = 0.847 versus ρ(object count, PS) = −0.571. This supports our
design choice of assigning a smaller coefficient to the object-count term than to the location-difficulty and
collaboration-intensity terms.

.5    Box Plot Comparison on Pack Objects Task
  Fig. 13 further shows the box-plot distributions of TS, AS, and CC for different methods on the Pack
Objects Task under Easy, Medium, and Hard settings. As task difficulty increases, all methods incur higher
temporal, action, and communication costs, but the degree of degradation differs substantially. MCTS-NoINS
and MCTS both deteriorate rapidly, with their TS medians reaching the time budget limit in harder settings,
indicating poor stability in complex tasks. CoELA and both DynaHMRC variants are generally more efficient,
but CoELA still exhibits strong concentration near the upper budget bound in Hard tasks. Among all
methods, DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B shows the most favorable efficiency–robustness trade-off, with consistently
lower medians in TS, AS, and CC than DynaHMRC-GPT4o and CoELA, especially in Medium and Hard
settings. This suggests that its advantage is not driven by a few lucky runs, but is reflected in a more stable
trial-level distribution with lower action and communication overhead.

.6         Failure Case Study
  Fig. 14 and Sec. .6.1 illustrate the reasoning and actions of the mobile manipulation robot Alice in a failed
episode. At step 10, Alice mistakenly identifies fork_0 as the target object and executes an incorrect pick
action. At step 11, Alice realizes that fork_0 is not the target and resumes searching for bowl_0. At step
23, due to the bounded memory window, Alice redundantly explores table_3. At step 29, since bowl_0 has
still not been placed into the tray and the time limit is reached, the task ultimately fails.

.6.1         Key Step Logs of the Failure Case



  ==== Reasoning Process&Action Decision of Alice ====
  ==== Step 10 ====
  Thoughts: Alice, the robot, has successfully identified the location of ‘fork_0’ inside the open drawer. The task
  requires Alice to retrieve items like ‘fork_0’ and ‘book_0’ and bring them closer to Bob’s range for placement
  in the tray. Since ‘fork_0’ is already located and accessible, Alice should prioritize picking it up to ensure efficient task
  progression. This action aligns with the collaboration plan, where Alice is responsible for retrieving items within
  her reach. Furthermore, picking ‘fork_0’ is a direct contribution to the task, as it will eventually need to be placed in
  the tray. Alice’s manipulator arm is capable of handling this task, and the gripper is currently empty, making it
  ready for object pickup. Therefore, the decision to execute the ‘pick(fork_0)’ action is logical and appropriate to advance
  the task at hand.

.7     Performance Comparison under Different Memory Queue Lengths
  To analyze how large memory storage affects LLM reasoning, we conduct a memory-queue-length sensitivity
study on the Pack Objects task under both Static and CTO settings. We compare the default memory length
M = 10 with a larger setting M = 15. For COHERENT, M denotes the length of global interaction history
retained in the centralized prompt; for CoELA and DynaHMRC , it denotes the per-agent memory queue
length.
As shown in Tab. 14, simply increasing the memory length does not consistently improve performance. In Static
tasks, longer memory can help preserve useful task-progress information. For example, DynaHMRC-GPT4o
improves from 0.6482 to 0.8889 in SUCC when M increases from 10 to 15, and DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B
improves from 0.8000 to 0.8519 in SUCC and from 0.8788 to 0.9568 in PS. This suggests that, when past
information remains relevant, a longer memory queue can provide the LLM with more complete execution
history and improve task grounding.
However, in dynamic CTO tasks, longer memory may also retain outdated target information after the task
objective changes, which can distract the LLM from the current goal. For COHERENT, increasing M from 10
to 15 decreases SUCC from 0.4074 to 0.2407 under CTO, and from 0.3148 to 0.0556 under Static. This indicates
that directly extending the global history in a centralized prompt can substantially increase the context
burden and dilute task-relevant information. Similarly, DynaHMRC-GPT4o and DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B also
show performance drops under CTO when M increases, suggesting that stale-context interference is a general
challenge for LLM-based long-horizon reasoning.
Comparing across methods, DynaHMRC remains more competitive than the baselines under both mem-
ory settings. Under the default M = 10, DynaHMRC-GPT4o and DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B outperform
COHERENT and CoELA in SUCC and PS under both Static and CTO settings. Even when M = 15,
DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B still maintains higher CTO SUCC than the baselines. This advantage suggests that
the performance gain of DynaHMRC does not simply come from using decentralized memory, but from a
more structured and efficient organization mechanism. Each robot maintains its own task-relevant memory
and reasons from its local execution context, while periodic reflection and leader-plan updates help identify
useful information for coordination instead of forcing all history into a single centralized prompt.
Overall, these results show that memory should be bounded and task-aware rather than simply enlarged.
Longer memory is beneficial when historical information remains relevant, but can harm dynamic adaptation
when obsolete goals or outdated plans remain in the prompt. Designing more structured, hierarchical, and
dynamically updated memory mechanisms is therefore an important direction for future work.

.10    Leader-Election Stability and Degraded Voting


Leader election in DynaHMRC is performed only once during task initialization. The elected leader remains
fixed throughout the episode, and later discussion stages only update the high-level division of labor without
re-electing a new leader. To analyze the stability of this one-time election process, we report the number of
voting rounds required to obtain a unique leader and the final leader distribution across team compositions.
As shown in Fig. 15, most elections converge quickly. For DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B and DynaHMRC-GPT4o,
80.1% and 79.5% of cases obtain a unique leader in the first round, respectively, and 92.8% and 93.6% are
resolved within two rounds. Only 3.8% and 3.3% require referee arbitration after three rounds. This indicates
that leader election is generally stable and rarely depends on the fallback arbitration mechanism.
We further test robustness under degraded voting by randomly replacing 50% of the votes during the leader
election. As shown in Tab. 17, DynaHMRC-Qwen3-4B remains robust under this perturbation. In Static
tasks, the success rate changes only slightly for 2- and 3-type teams and remains unchanged for 4-type
teams. In CTO tasks, the success rate remains unchanged across all team sizes. These results suggest that
the downstream closed-loop execution and periodic coordination can tolerate partial voting noise, although
high-quality votes remain important for selecting an appropriate leader.
Fig. 16 further shows that final leader selection follows interpretable capability-aware patterns. When no mobile
manipulator is available, the drone Lucy is frequently selected as the leader. When a mobile manipulator is
included, Alice becomes the dominant leader in most team compositions, reflecting its combined mobility and
manipulation capabilities. More ambiguous team compositions exhibit more distributed leadership, indicating
that multiple robots can plausibly serve as coordination centers.

To justify the training configuration used for fine-tuning, we conduct a brief hyperparameter sensitivity
analysis for Qwen3-4B. We mainly examine three factors: learning rate, training epochs, and batch size.
Specifically, we compare learning rates of 2 × 10−5 , 1 × 10−5 , and 5 × 10−6 , as well as batch sizes of 2, 4, and 8.
For each setting, we train the model with the same dataset split, prompt template, and other hyperparameters,
and report the validation loss curves over training epochs.
As shown in Fig. 18, the validation loss generally decreases in the early stage of training and then gradually
plateaus or increases, indicating mild overfitting after approximately 5.8 epochs. Therefore, we use 6 epochs
as the maximum training budget and select the checkpoint with the best validation loss rather than simply
using the final checkpoint. This strategy avoids overfitting caused by excessive training while keeping the
training procedure consistent across different settings. We observe that a batch size of 8 achieves the best
validation performance among the tested settings. Smaller batch sizes lead to less stable optimization and
slightly worse validation loss. Therefore, we adopt batch size 8 in the final configuration.
As shown in Fig. 19, 2 × 10−5 shows less stable convergence, while 5 × 10−6 achieves a slightly lower best
validation loss but converges more slowly. In comparison, 1 × 10−5 provides a better trade-off between
convergence speed and final performance. Based on these observations, we use batch size 8 and learning rate
1 × 10−5 in the main experiments.

Tab. 25 summarizes the failure process. The key issue is that the centralized progress state incorrectly accepts
toothpaste_0 as task progress, although it is not in the target object list. After soap is placed, the central
coordinator repeatedly treats the task as nearly or fully completed and issues wait commands to the team.
This prevents timely recovery for the missing fork_0. When the coordinator later attempts to recover, it asks
the fixed manipulator to pick fork_0; however, the robot reports that fork_0 is not available in its local scene
graph. Meanwhile, the drone repeatedly encounters obstacle-related failures, and the mobile manipulator
performs additional exploration without locating the missing object.
This example reveals that the failure is not caused by a single low-level action error. Instead, it is caused by
an incorrect centralized belief that propagates to the entire team. Since the central coordinator is responsible
for maintaining the global task state and assigning actions to all robots, an error in this state can lead to
coordinated but incorrect behavior, including unnecessary waiting, delayed search, and ineffective reallocation.
This is a typical limitation of centralized coordination in long-horizon heterogeneous tasks: the centralized
state acts as a single point of failure, and local robot feedback may not be incorporated quickly enough to
correct the global decision.
By contrast, the proposed decentralized coordination strategy reduces the dependency on a single global
progress estimate. Each robot maintains local observations and can share compact task-relevant information
with teammates. For example, if one robot observes that fork_0 is absent from its local scene graph or
that a non-target object has been placed, this information can directly influence local decisions and trigger
reallocation, rather than waiting for a central coordinator to reconstruct the correct global state. This
qualitative case therefore complements the aggregate results and helps explain why decentralized coordination
is more robust in scenarios involving partial observability, object search, and heterogeneous robot capabilities.

Communication and token cost As shown in Fig. 6, the average communication cost (CC) per robot remains
relatively stable across robot configurations. This suggests that increasing the number of heterogeneous
robots does not lead to a proportional increase in per-robot communication events within the evaluated team
sizes. Therefore, the total communication cost grows approximately linearly with the number of robots in the
studied regime, rather than super-linearly. This is because communication is selectively triggered through the
communicate action and periodic discussion, rather than being performed at every step. Tab. 27 compares the
token consumption of DynaHMRC-GPT4o and COHERENT across tasks and robot compositions using the
same GPT-4o backbone. DynaHMRC-GPT4o generally consumes more total tokens, approximately twice that
of COHERENT in the evaluated settings. This overhead comes from the structured decentralized workflow,
including self-description, task allocation & leadership bidding, leader election, decentralized execution, group
discussion, and leader-plan updating. In contrast, COHERENT uses fewer centralized calls, but each call must
aggregate multi-robot information into a single global context. Thus, the performance gain of DynaHMRC
should not be interpreted as lower total LLM usage; rather, DynaHMRC uses additional LLM computation in
a more structured way to preserve local execution context and maintain global coordination.

Coordination and alignment failures To examine whether more complex robot compositions introduce
coordination problems, we add diagnostic statistics on redundant and conflicting actions in Tab. 29. For
consistent comparison, redundant actions are counted only when a robot navigates to a previously visited
region, while conflicting actions exclude wait and communicate. Across all reported settings, DynaHMRC-
GPT4o consistently produces fewer redundant actions than COHERENT. On average, redundant actions
decrease from 7.67 for COHERENT to 3.64 for DynaHMRC-GPT4o, corresponding to an approximately 52.6%
reduction. Conflict counts are low for both methods, but DynaHMRC-GPT4o also reduces conflicts in several
dynamic settings, especially ANC. These statistics show that coordination failures tend to increase when robot
compositions become larger or more heterogeneous, since more robots introduce more local observations, action
histories, capability constraints, and object-level dependencies. However, this increase is more pronounced for
COHERENT, especially in Static, ANC, and REC settings. In contrast, DynaHMRC-GPT4o generally keeps
redundant and conflicting actions at a lower level, suggesting that local embodiment-grounded planning and
periodic synchronization help control the coordination overhead introduced by larger heterogeneous teams.

Latency analysis We further report an inference-latency breakdown on the NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin 32GB
under different team sizes in Tab. 28. All values are averaged over per-robot LLM calls. Preprocessing time
mainly consists of prefill and I/O overhead and is affected by the number of input tokens, while generation
time is mainly determined by model inference speed and output length. The results show that the average
total inference time remains at the seconds level and does not increase proportionally with team size. The
generation speed is stable at around 26 tokens/s, indicating that backend throughput is not strongly affected
by robot-team size in the evaluated regime. We also distinguish token cost from wall-clock latency. Total
token consumption measures overall LLM compute cost, but it does not directly determine real execution
time because multiple robots can invoke LLMs in parallel during decentralized planning and discussion. In a
parallel synchronization stage, the effective waiting time is dominated by the slowest parallel agent call rather

regime evaluated in this paper, i.e., teams with 2–4 robot types. Scaling to substantially larger teams may
introduce higher communication, token, latency, and coordination overhead, and will require message filtering,
summary compression, asynchronous inference, hierarchical coordination, and more structured discussion
protocols.
