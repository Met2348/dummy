                                                                                              Abstract
                                                       Neural operators approximate mappings between function spaces, but often generalize poorly
                                                   to other operators and usually require fine-tuning or retraining. In-Context Operator Networks
                                                   (Icon) addresses this issue by prompting the model with numerical context so that the model
                                                   learns specific operators from prompts and adapt to different operators without fine-tuning.
                                                   However, Icon may still fail to generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) operator tasks. Inpired
                                                   by the success of harness engineering of Large Language models (LLMs), we introduce Chain
                                                   of Operators (Chop), a framework that harness a frozen Icon to OOD operator tasks without
                                                   updating its parameters. Specifically, Chop constructs a chain of operators consisting of explicit
                                                   elementary transformations and the frozen Icon. Experiments on a scalar conservation law
                                                   and a mean-field control problem show that Chop reduces relative inference error over direct
                                                   Icon evaluation, while each operator in the chain remains interpretable and in closed form. A
                                                   chain constructed on one PDE family further generalizes to a different family, indicating shared
                                                   mechanisms across harness systems.


                                         1        Introduction
                                         The simulation and control of complex physical systems are often governed by families of solution
                                         operators,
                                                                              {Sφ : Xφ → Yφ }φ∈Φ ,
                                         where Xφ and Yφ denote the input and output function spaces for the instance φ, and φ encodes crit-
                                         ical physical information such as flux functions, coefficient fields, or boundary conditions. Varying
                                         φ may change the governing equation, the admissible function spaces and the structural constraints
                                         satisfied by the solution.
                                             Classical numerical methods approximate Sφ by discretizing the governing equations and solving
                                         the resulting finite-dimensional problem for each PDE instance. By contrast, operator learning
                                         methods seek to construct a surrogate for the solution operator Sφ itself, which, once trained, yields
                                         the solution for any new input through a single forward evaluation [1, 2, 3]. Deep operator networks
                                         (DeepONets [4]) and Fourier neural operators (FNOs [5]) are two representative architectures in
                                         operator learning, and have been shown to achieve high accuracy for operator families sufficiently
                                         close to the training distribution [6]. Subsequent developments have further incorporated physics-
                                         informed objectives, complex dynamics and irregular geometries, as well as alternative operator
                                         architectures [7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. For fixed trained weights, however, the model represents merely a
                                         single mapping between function spaces, as its parameters are specialized to one particular input–
                                         output relation. Consequently, when confronted with a new target operator or system, the model

may generalize poorly and often requires retraining. This out-of-distribution (OOD) sensitivity is
not unique to a particular architecture. Existing remedies for neural operators often incorporate
physics-informed constraints or sparse observations to improve reliable extrapolation [13], while
transfer learning has been used to adapt PDE operators under conditional shift [14].
     Such repeated retraining imposes a severe computational burden, particularly when the physical
domain or control parameters vary across tasks. To address this limitation, recent work has shifted
toward multi-operator learning [15, 16]. By jointly pretraining on large-scale datasets across PDE
systems, these approaches seek a shared latent space across governing equations, within which
common physical structure can be represented [17, 18, 19]. However, building a unified multiphysics
operator model remains challenging, since it requires multiscale training data and incurs a high
pretraining cost. When the target operator lies outside the training distribution, these models may
still require additional pretraining or fine-tuning to adapt to the new operator [19, 20, 21, 22].
To reduce the need for additional data collection and fine-tuning, some recent works incorporate
explicit PDE information to improve few-shot or zero-shot generalization across multiple physical
systems [23, 24, 25, 26]. Such approaches rely on prior knowledge of the underlying PDE, which is
often unavailable when a model is deployed in a new environment.
     To move toward zero-shot generalization across PDE systems without such prior knowledge,
recent work in scientific computing has turned to in-context learning. The In-Context Operator
Network (ICON [27, 28]) infers the target operator from a few prompted input–output function
pairs at inference time and can be retargeted across different PDE systems without updating its
weights. In-context learning has also been combined with data-efficient pretraining [17], generative
neural operators for parametric PDEs [29, 30], probabilistic generative formulations for uncertainty
quantification [31], and graph models for air quality prediction [32]. The paradigm has also been
extended to financial execution [33] and measure-valued optimal transport [34], while theoretical
work analyzes robustness [35] and task diversity [36]. Although in-context learning can identify
the target operator from prompted examples, distribution shift persists. A new PDE changes
the induced solution operator, often through shifts in coefficients, boundary data, smoothness,
oscillation scale or value range. Such shifts can degrade the frozen model’s predictions. Within the
Icon line of work, affine rescaling has been used to align shifted PDE instances with the training
scale [37]. The efficacy of this simple alignment indicates that OOD failure is not inherently a
limitation of the model itself. This phenomenon may instead arise from a mismatch between the
prompt and the regime in which the model has learned to identify operators. A natural alternative
to updating the parameters is therefore to reformulate the prompt and output variables, thereby
rendering the induced operator easier for the frozen model to infer.
     Most neural operator adaptation methods handle such shifts by fine-tuning the model or by
introducing additional neural modules. These strategies can be effective, but they add black-box
components and are often computationally expensive. To address this limitation, we propose Chain
of Operators (Chop), a framework for out-of-distribution operator adaptation without updating
the pretrained model. Instead of relying on direct prediction by the frozen model, Chop composes
the target operator within a chain of operators, thereby leveraging the multi-operator in-context
capability of Icon. This chain is a composition of elementary operators and calls to the frozen Icon
model, where each operator in the chain performs an explicit step, such as rescaling values, changing
coordinates, applying a symmetry, etc. By reformulating the input and output spaces for Icon
model through this chain, the originally challenging out-of-distribution problem is mapped back into
an in-distribution regime. Consequently, identifying and predicting the induced operator becomes a
task well within the inherent capabilities of the frozen Icon model. Because all operations outside
the frozen Icon are implemented as explicit, closed-form code, the entire adaptation process remains
fully interpretable. Furthermore, this mechanism conceptually mirrors the “Chain of Thought”

reasoning in Large Language Models: it systematically decomposes a complex, intractable problem
into a sequence of simpler, manageable sub-tasks.
    The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews Icon and formulates the
operator-adaptation problem. Section 3 presents Chop, including the prompt-side and prediction-
side operator construction and the agentic evolutionary search used to discover operator chains.
Section 4 evaluates Chop on a scalar conservation law and a mean-field control problem, including
cross-PDE transfer. Section 5 concludes.


2     Preliminaries
2.1   In-Context Operator Network
As described in Section 1, let
                                         {Sφ : Xφ → Yφ }φ∈Φ
denote the family of solution operators induced by a parametrized PDE class.
    In-Context Operator Networks were introduced for operator learning from data prompts of
paired input and output functions [27] and later extended to multimodal prompts with text de-
scriptions [38]. The input prompt contains D context pairs

                                 C = {(xi , yi )}D
                                                 i=1 ,   yi = Sφ (xi ),

and a query input x∗ ∈ Xφ . The context pairs are examples of the target mapping Sφ , and the
desired query output is Sφ (x∗ ). To build the prompt, each input xi , each output yi and the query x∗
are sampled on finite sets of spatial or spatiotemporal locations. The sampled locations and values
are encoded as tokens and passed to a transformer attention architecture. At inference time, the
context tokens represent examples of the target mapping, while the query tokens specify the input
whose output is unknown. With fixed parameters θ, Icon attends jointly to C and x∗ and defines
the inference map
                                   ŷ ∗ = Iconθ C, x∗ ≈ Sφ (x∗ ),
                                                      
                                                                                                   (1)
in which adaptation to Sφ is performed implicitly through attention over C, without any gradient
update. Because θ is fixed across operators, substituting different contexts can condition the same
parameters on different operators, provided that the prompt representation remains compatible.

2.2   Problem Setting
Given a pretrained Icon model with frozen parameters θ, let Dop denote its pretraining operator
distribution. At inference time, only the prompt (C, x∗ ) and Iconθ are available. Let X and Y
denote the input and output function spaces of the prompt. We denote by T : X → Y the target
operator expressed by the prompt. The context labels satisfy yi = T (xi ) for each context pair, while
the unknown query label is y ∗ = T (x∗ ). We consider the case where T may lie outside supp(Dop ).
    Raw Icon inference applies Iconθ (C, x∗ ) directly to this operator identification problem. For
out-of-distribution operators, this direct formulation may present the pretrained model with an op-
erator far from supp(Dop ), where its predictions can be unreliable even when the context examples
are correct. We therefore formulate inference as operator reformulation through prompts. The
objective is to apply explicit and interpretable mathematical operations to the prompt, thereby
obtaining an induced operator problem closer to the regime in which Iconθ is reliable. The refor-
mulation uses only the input data in the prompt (C, x∗ ): the query label y ∗ is unavailable and θ is
not updated. After Icon makes a prediction for the induced problem, the result is mapped back

not a single preprocessing or correction step, but an executable sequence of operators that rewrite
the prompt, run Icon inference on the induced problem, and then return or correct the prediction.

Here P ′ = (C ′ , x∗′ ) is the induced prompt, with C ′ = {(x′i , yi′ )}i=1 denoting its transformed con-
text data. Auxiliary variables used by an operator in the chain, such as normalization constants,

model, and prompt states or intermediate predictions produced by the chain. They do not access
the unknown target value y ∗ , the explicit parameter φ or a symbolic description of T . Transformed
prompt data are obtained only from the input data, structural symmetries or auxiliary Icon calls,
and are kept consistent with the induced operator passed to Icon.
    Designing an effective chain by hand requires combined mathematical and physical insight into
the target operator, and the choices of transformations and output corrections are usually settled
only through many trial experiments. We therefore discover chains through Evolutionary Ensemble
of Agents (EvE), an agent-based evolutionary framework [39], in which LLM-based agents iteratively
propose and improve candidate operator chains based on evaluation scores.


4      Experiments
We conduct numerical experiments on a scalar conservation law (Section 4.1) and a mean-field con-
trol problem (Section 4.2), evaluating each across a variety of out-of-distribution operator instances.
These two physical settings are designed to exercise the two primary inference modes of the Icon:
autoregressive multi-step rollout for tracking the long-term evolution of the conservation law, and
single-shot operator queries for the varied configurations of the MFC problem. In Section 4.3, we
test the robustness of the operator chains by applying them to a different PDE class without re-
running the evolutionary search. Crucially, the underlying Icon models are pretrained and remain
frozen throughout our study.

4.1    Scalar conservation law
We consider the scalar conservation law

                                   ∂t u + ∂x f (u) = 0,               x ∈ [0, 1],                   (12)

with periodic boundary conditions. For a flux f and time step ∆t > 0, we denote the time-∆t
solution operator by Sf,∆t : u(·, t) 7→ u(·, t + ∆t). In each prompt, the context pairs are sampled
from Sf,∆t , and Icon predicts the next-time function for the query input.
    Following [37], the frozen 1D Icon model is pretrained on (12) with the flux restricted to the
cubic family
                           f (u) = au3 + bu2 + cu,        (a, b, c) ∈ [−1, 1]3 ,               (13)
The training dataset contains trajectories with coefficients (a, b, c) sampled uniformly from [−1, 1]3 .
The time step is fixed at ∆t = 0.1, initial conditions are drawn from a periodic Gaussian random field
of length scale ℓ = 1, and ground-truth solutions are obtained by solving the PDE with a third-order
WENO scheme [40]. The in-distribution operator family is therefore {Sau3 +bu2 +cu, 0.1 }(a,b,c)∈[−1,1]3 .

Each prompt contains five context pairs and one query input. The five pairs record the one-step

each step.
    Evolution returns the following fixed chain,

               Bconsv :   Fshift → Fscale → Icon → Gunscale → Gunshift → Gmass .                        (14)

The chain combines reversible shift and scaling transformations with a final projection step. Fshift
aligns the prompt by an integer cyclic translation. The shift s is estimated from the most recent
context pair by minimizing the difference between mean-centered input and output. Fscale parame-
terizes the affine transformation u = σv +µ, where µ and σ denote the mean and standard deviation
respectively of the prompt values pooled across the context pairs and the query input. Gunscale and
Gunshift are the exact inverses of Fscale and Fshift applied in reverse to the Icon output. Gmass is the
orthogonal L2 projection onto the affine subspace of fields whose spatial mean matches that of the
query input, enforcing exact mass conservation under the periodic boundary conditions. The chain
has no learned parameters or tuned hyperparameters. Its shifts, value scales, and conserved mean
are computed directly from the prompt data. Algorithm 1 summarizes this deterministic inference
procedure.

Fvalue and Gvalue provide a reversible value normalization. For each query, the normalization com-
putes a center µ and scale σ from the prompt data, consisting of the context pairs and the query
input. For prompts containing non-negative density fields, the normalization sets µ = 0 and uses
the root-mean-square value of the prompt data as σ, preserving density positivity under rescaling.
For prompts containing cost values, the normalization instead uses the prompt mean and standard
deviation. The same (µ, σ) is then applied to the context inputs, context targets, and query input,
with Gvalue applying the exact inverse to the Icon output. The final block, Gres , builds residual
corrections by predicting each context pair after omitting that pair from the context, transfers these
residuals to the query using weights based on input similarity, and scales the transferred correc-
tion by a scalar α̂ fitted by cross-validation on the context pairs. Algorithms 2 and 3 give the
implementation details.
    The same in-context cross-validation step is used for MFC, comparing the full chain with Raw
Icon for each query. For each held-out context pair, both predictors are evaluated using the
remaining D − 1 context pairs, and the query x∗ is evaluated by the predictor with the smaller
cross-validation error as in equation (15).

5    Conclusion
This work introduced Chain of Operators (Chop), a framework for adapting frozen In-Context
Operator Network (Icon) models to out-of-distribution operator tasks without retraining. The
central idea is to surround the frozen Icon model with closed-form prompt-side and prediction-side
operators, forming the composition F → Icon → G. The agentic evolutionary search then selects
a chain of these transformations, making the adaptation explicit and interpretable.
    The experiments on conservation-law and mean-field control problems indicate that this for-
mulation can improve prediction outside the training operator distribution while retaining a clear
connection to the underlying PDE structure. They also show that the discovered transformations
play different roles, with some acting as reusable corrections across problem settings and others
encoding structure specific to a PDE class.
    By adapting a frozen Icon backbone through an explicit, interpretable chain of operators, Chop
provides a foundation for agentic scientific computing. A natural extension is to build an operator
agent around Icon that automates the many stages of this adaptation. We leave this to future
research.
