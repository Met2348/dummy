                                         1   Introduction

                                         Penetration testing simulates cyberattacks to identify vulnerabilities in com-
                                         puter systems. A common sub-task is privilege escalation, where an attacker
                                         manipulates a system to execute actions with higher privileges not intentionally
                                         granted to them. We focus on automated Linux privilege escalation, where a
                                         low-privilege user attempts to obtain root access.
                                             Recent work on hackingBuddyGPT shows that cloud-based Large Language
                                         Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 can achieve success rates of around 80% on
                                         standardized privilege-escalation benchmarks [10]. However, cloud-based LLMs
                                         introduce substantial security, privacy, and sovereignty concerns: all interaction
                                         data (including command histories, system output, and sensitive configuration
                                         details) must be transmitted to an external providers. This is particularly prob-
                                         lematic in the penetration-testing context, where often sensitive infrastructure
2         Benjamin Probst, Andreas Happe, and Jürgen Cito

details are processed. Cloud-based models also introduce supply-chain depen-
dencies and availability risks, motivating the use of locally hosted open-weight
alternatives.
    Small Language Models (SLMs), i.e., open-weight LLMs small enough to
run on local hardware, are a natural candidate. Unfortunately, prior work [10]
reports success rates of only 8–16% for SLMs such as Llama3 8B and Llama3
70B, compared to roughly 80% for cloud-based models, severely limiting their
practical applicability.
    In this work, we present a systematic empirical study whether this gap can
be reduced through targeted system-level and prompting interventions. We an-
alyze execution traces from autonomous privilege-escalation runs and identify
six recurring failure modes of SLMs: unstructured exploration, command repeti-
tion, hallucinated capabilities, ignored outputs, excessive command complexity,
and missing exploitation knowledge. We map these to established enhancement
techniques which are individually well-known, but whose effectiveness and inter-
actions in the privilege-escalation domain have not been studied. We select five
concrete treatments for implementation and evaluation.
    We implement these treatments as extensions to hackingBuddyGPT and eval-
uate their impact across multiple SLMs. With the full treatment stack, Llama3.1
70B achieves a success rate of 83%, while Llama3.1 8B and Qwen2.5 7B reach
67%, effectively matching or surpassing GPT-4o. A full-factorial ablation study
(25 combinations) quantifies individual and combined contributions, revealing
that reflection-oriented components (Analyze, Chain-of-Thought, Retrieval Aug-
mented Generation) are the primary drivers, enabling success rates of up to 67%
when combined.

Research Questions
Motivated by these observations, we investigate the following research questions:
    – RQ1 To what extent can established system-level and prompting treatments
      close the performance gap between small open-weight LLMs and cloud-based
      models for automated Linux privilege escalation?
    – RQ2 Which interventions, and which combinations, contribute most to per-
      formance gains, and what failure modes remain?

Contributions
This paper makes the following contributions:
    – A systematic failure-mode analysis of SLMs in autonomous Linux privilege
      escalation, identifying six domain-specific deficiencies and mapping them to
      candidate interventions (Section 4).
    – An open-source prototype extending hackingBuddyGPT [10] with five inter-
      ventions, enabling reproducible experimentation (Section 6).
    – Empirical evaluation across multiple models showing significant performance
      gains, including an ablation study quantifying individual component contri-
      butions (Sections 7 and 8).

2     Background & Related Work
2.1   Small Language Models (SLMs)
Open-weight LLMs provide public access to their pre-trained parameters, en-
abling deployment on user-controlled hardware. Small Language Model (SLM)
are open-weight LLMS with fewer than approximately 32B parameters that run
on local hardware. As SLMs share the same transformer-based architectures
as larger LLMs, most optimization and enhancement techniques apply to both
classes.
    Local deployment of SLMs offers advantages in security, privacy, and opera-
tional independence. In penetration-testing, cloud-based LLMs require transmit-
ting sensitive data (configuration files, credentials, command outputs) to external
providers. Locally hosted models avoid this issue entirely while also eliminating
supply-chain dependencies such as service outages, pricing changes, or model
deprecations.
    The primary limitation of local model deployment are hardware require-
ments: very large models require hundreds of gigabytes of GPU memory, but
SLMs can run on contemporary consumer GPUs [27]. While SLMs generally
under-perform larger LLMs, prior work shows that they can be competitive
when appropriately adapted [2, 36, 20].

2.2   LLM Enhancing Techniques
Despite their general capabilities, LLMs often exhibit deficiencies in structured
reasoning, long-horizon planning, and domain-specific knowledge. A variety of
enhancement techniques have been proposed to mitigate these limitations.
    Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting encourages models to generate in-
termediate reasoning steps before producing an answer, improving performance
in multi-step reasoning tasks [25, 30, 25, 17, 3]. CoT can be elicited via few-shot
exemplar traces [30], zero-shot cues such as "Let’s think step by step" [13], or
Plan-and-Solve prompting [28] which separates planning from execution.
    Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) augments LLMs retrieving
relevant documents from external knowledge sources and incorporating them
into the prompt [5, 14], extending the model’s knowledge without modifying its
parameters. RAG has been applied in domains requiring specialized or rapidly
evolving knowledge, including medicine [31], finance [35], legal reasoning [34],
and penetration testing [20, 12, 32].
    Fine-tuning adapts an LLM to a specific domain by further training it
on curated data. Parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA [11] or QLoRA [4]
4       Benjamin Probst, Andreas Happe, and Jürgen Cito

reduce cost by introducing few trainable parameters and operating on quan-
tized models. Fine-tuning has shown effectiveness across diverse tasks including
penetration-testing [36, 21, 2, 20]).
    Privilege escalation depends on reconnaissance within a vast search space
where exhaustive exploration is infeasible. Prior work shows that High-Level
Guidance can substantially improve LLM performance by directing attention
toward promising attack paths [10]. Guidance refers to task-specific hints that
indicate plausible exploration directions without revealing concrete exploitation
steps. In the employed benchmark, each task is associated with a single hint, e.g.,
“there might be some bad sudo binaries on the system” or “it looks like there is a
periodic backup script”, pointing to relevant subsystems or vulnerability classes.
Importantly, these hints do not constitute solution sketches or executable com-
mands. They model the kind of contextual information that is routinely available
in realistic penetration-testing workflows: output from automated enumeration
tools (e.g., LinPEAS, linux-exploit-suggester ), organizational threat intelligence,
or preliminary findings from earlier assessment phases. In this sense, guidance
represents a lightweight form of human-in-the-loop supervision rather than ora-
cle knowledge.

2.3   Related Work
LLMs have demonstrated strong performance across a range of cybersecurity
tasks, including source code vulnerability detection [24], security patch genera-
tion [1], and automated penetration testing [32, 12, 3, 10].
    Linux Privilege Escalation. Prior work shows that cloud-based LLMs
such as GPT-4-turbo can successfully perform automated Linux privilege esca-
lation [10]. However, the same evaluations demonstrate that small open-weight
models, including Llama3 8B and Llama3 70B, perform poorly even when pro-
vided with human guidance, indicating fundamental limitations in reasoning,
exploration, and exploitation.
    Automated LLM-based Penetration Testing Frameworks. Several
frameworks have been proposed for LLM-driven penetration testing. Pentest-
GPT [3] uses a modular parsing/reasoning/generation design but requires hu-
man intervention. AUTOATTACKER [32] is fully automated with a summa-
rizer, planner, navigator, and experience manager, but none of its evaluated
open-weight models successfully solve any task. PenHeal [12] adds remediation
advice but does not evaluate any open-weight models. To the best of our knowl-
edge, hackingBuddyGPT [10, 8] is the only framework explicitly designed for
automated Linux privilege escalation. It employs a single-loop architecture in
which the LLM iteratively generates commands based on the current system
state. Its results highlight the effectiveness of cloud-based models but expose
severe limitations of open-weight models. Overall, existing work provides lim-
ited evidence that open-weight models can be effective for autonomous privilege
escalation without substantial architectural support.
    Using SLMs for Privilege Escalation. Peres [29] follows the planner/-
commander/summarizer pattern [32, 12] to delegate priv-escalation tasks to mul-

3     Methodology

3.1   Treatment Selection Process

To identify why SLMs underperform cloud-based models [10, 32], we qualitatively
analyzed execution traces from GPT-4-turbo and two SLMs during autonomous
privilege escalation, identifying key behavioral differences and failure modes.
Based on these findings and established enhancement techniques (Section 2.2),
we selected treatments (Section 4) for investigation. The preliminary analysis is
detailed in the appendix (Section A).


3.2   Experiment Design

We integrate the treatments into an LLM-driven prototype (Section 3.3) con-
nected to vulnerable test VMs (Section 3.5). Each combination of model and
treatment configuration is run three times with a maximum of 40 rounds per
run. We additionally conduct an ablation study using Llama3.1 8B.
6      Benjamin Probst, Andreas Happe, and Jürgen Cito

3.3   Prototype

We implement our treatment ideas as extensions to the existing hackingBud-
dyGPT framework [10]. Its core architecture (Figure 1) consists of a single con-
trol loop: in each iteration, a query_next_command prompt, containing the goal,
scenario information, and action history, is used to generate a command which is
executed on the target over SSH. The loop repeats until root access is achieved
or a maximum iteration count is reached.


3.4   LLM Selection and Configuration

For our evaluation we chose a selection of common cloud-based models as baseline
to compare our selected open-weight models against.


Cloud-based Models For the cloud-based models, we use OpenAI GPT-4o
(gpt-4o-2024-11-20) and OpenAI GPT-4o mini (gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18). They
were released around the same time as the Meta Llama3.1 series, and are acces-
sible and cost-effective to run.


Open-Weight Models We select Llama3.1 8B, Llama3.1 70B, Qwen2.5 7B,
and WhiteRabbitNeo 7B. Llama3.1 8B and Qwen2.5 7B are SLMs runnable on a
single contemporary GPU. Llama3.1 70B can still run on consumer-grade GPUs.
WhiteRabbitNeo1 is fine-tuned on cybersecurity topics (misconfigurations, cre-
dentials, vulnerabilities) with Qwen2.5-Coder 7B as base model.
    The 7B/8B models are hosted locally via llama-cpp-python 2 ; Llama3.1 70B
uses the Microsoft Azure AI API; OpenAI models use the OpenAI API. Tem-
perature is set to 0.8 for all models, following the configuration used in prior
work [10], which balances diversity in command generation with output coher-
ence. Platform defaults are used for all other parameters.


Model Refusals The only LLM exhibiting model refusal was Llama3.1 8B,
which refused to generate exploitation commands in a substantial fraction of
iterations. While various jailbreaking methods have been researched [33], we use
Llama-3.1-8B-Lexi-Uncensored-V2 3 , a fine-tuned variant of Llama3.1 8B with
safety filters removed. This is representative of real-world offensive use, where
practitioners routinely deploy uncensored variants. We note that this fine-tune
preserves the base model’s architecture and general capabilities; the removal of
safety training does not introduce new failure modes (hallucinations, repetition)
beyond those already present in the base model.

Context size The size of a model context indicates how much information can
be processed by the model at once. Current models from OpenAI, Anthropic or
Meta can have a context size of up to 128k4 , or even 200k5 . Although such a
large context size may be easily accessible when using an API, it also increases
memory requirements, which is often the limiting factor when running a model
locally [15, 7]. We limit the context size for all tested models to roughly 8k, with
the goal of striking a balance between memory consumption and necessary space.


3.5    Testbed and Baselines

We use the benchmark-privesc-linux testbed [9], consisting of 12 test cases span-
ning five vulnerability classes: SUID/sudo misconfigurations (3 tests), password
hygiene issues such as reuse or weak passwords (3 tests), information disclosure
through config files or shell history (3 tests), Docker group membership (1 test),
and cron-based escalation (2 tests). Table 5 in the Appendix lists all scenarios.
Each test case runs in a dedicated Debian-based VM with a single intentionally
planted vulnerability, a low-privilege user account, and SSH access. This isola-
tion ensures that destructive commands do not affect other tests and that each
run starts from a clean state. A run succeeds if the agent obtains a root shell or
correctly identifies the root password within the iteration limit.
    As baselines, we use results from hackingBuddyGPT [10]: a human penetration-
tester, GPT-4-Turbo, and Llama3 8B. For the human baseline, each iteration
corresponds to one shell command executed on the target system, matching the
LLM agent’s per-iteration granularity. We also rerun all 12 test-cases with our
selected models (Section 3.4) using vanilla hackingBuddyGPT.

3.6     Metrics and Analysis

For each test, we collect timestamps, end state (root achieved or not), and per-
iteration data including all queries, prompts, answers, token counts, and timing.
Following [32, 10], we use success rate and number of iterations as evaluation
metrics.
    For qualitative analysis, we process the generated output and examine the
quality of the generated content, the behavior of the model (e.g. repetition,
missing low hanging fruits, running in circles, etc.) and the influence of the
different components. For quantitative analysis, we focus on the amount of tokens
generated by each component, the number of solved test-cases and the necessary
amount of iterations for each specific test-case.


4      Identified Problems

We analyze execution traces from two sources: existing hackingBuddyGPT ex-
periments [10]6 (Llama3 8B and GPT-4-turbo), and our own runs of Llama3.1
8B in the same environment (Figure 1). Following the process described in Sec-
tion 3.1, we identified six problems:

1. Complex Commands: Llama3 8B is prone to generate complex, inter-
   leaved commands that are either invalid or result in no output7 :
    1 exec_find / - type f - perm 4755 2 >/ dev / null | xargs ls - ld | grep suid |
           head -1 | cut -d ’ ’ - f11 - | xargs find / - name | xargs file | grep
           ELF | grep setuid | xargs cp / bin / bash .

2. Hallucinates Capabilities: Llama3 8B hallucinates non-existing capabili-
   ties (e.g., exec_which or exec_find instead of using the available exec_command
   and test_credential actions.
3. Zero Structure: GPT-4-turbo follows a structured approach, starting with
   consistent reconnaissance commands to gather system information. Llama3.1
   8B randomly tries commands without systematic exploration.
4. Command Repetition: Llama3.1 8B suffers from severe repetition, where
   a few unique commands cover the majority of all iterations in a test run.
5. Ignoring outputs: Llama3.1 8B frequently ignores previous command out-
   puts, repeating commands or slight variations even when the output contains
   exploitable information.
6. Missing Exploitation Knowledge: Llama3.1 8B lacks knowledge of spe-
   cific exploitation techniques for certain vulnerabilities indicating that its
   training data did not contain exploitation background. For example, Llama3.1
   8B has not shown any sign of knowledge about how to successfully exploit
   the tar binary, even when asked explicitly.

5   Treatments
We map the identified problems to potential treatments (Table 2), drawing on es-
tablished LLM enhancement techniques (Section 2.2). While these techniques are
individually well-known, their effectiveness for the specific task of autonomous
privilege escalation, and their interactions when combined, have not been stud-
ied.

Chain of Thought (CoT) CoT improves the reasoning capabilities of an LLM
and could help with multiple problems. For example, during the reasoning step of
CoT, the LLM may realize that it has already executed a specific command and
should therefore suggest an alternative approach. Similarly, additional reasoning
capabilities may reduce hallucinations as the model has to explain its steps.

Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) for Exploitation Knowledge
RAG aims to improve the quality of the generated answers by augmenting the
prompt with additional knowledge. This feature can enrich the model with do-
main specific knowledge such as specific commands, possible vulnerabilities, or
general exploits. This could solve one the problem of knowledge gaps.

Structure via Prompt (SvP) An essential part of automated penetration
testing is structured exploration. By default, we use the same approach as [10]
and [32], where the LLM is prompted with the current worldview and asked
to generate the next action. We augment this by including a curated checklist
of common privilege-escalation attack vectors and their enumeration commands
directly in the prompt. This checklist was generated using GPT-4o and manu-
ally validated against established references (Section 6). The checklist provides
structure and discovery coverage without revealing exploitation steps, so the
LLM still must reason about how to exploit any findings.

Context Management And History Compression Another crucial step for
automated penetration testing is keeping track of the attack history and stor-
10     Benjamin Probst, Andreas Happe, and Jürgen Cito

ing important extracted information and potential vulnerabilities. To minimize
storage while keeping relevant information, compressing the action history is of
special interest. Current LLM-based automated penetration testing systems [32,
12, 10] use an additional LLM prompt to summarize the most recent output and
update its current worldview. [10] compare this method to a baseline, where,
instead of summarizing the output, all previous commands and the respective
outputs are included in the prompt. Their evaluation shows that depending on
the model, summarizing can lead to a substantial boost or decrease in success
rate. We propose a modified approach that keeps all previously executed com-
mands but only the command output of the most recent executed one. This
reduces time and overhead, as [10] have shown that the additional LLM-callout
can significantly increase the runtime. The major downside of this approach is
that potential useful information is lost after the command output has been
pruned from history. To reduce information loss, we propose to include multiple
past outputs instead of just the last one.

No Duplicates An intuitive solution to reduce repetition is to not allow re-
peated commands. Each time an already executed command is generated, the
LLM is prompted again until a new command is returned. This is synergistic to
using SLMs as there are no direct costs associated with the LLM invocation. To
avoid infinite loops, after a configurable number of LLM-generations has been
reached, invocation of a previously used command is allowed.

Reflective State Analysis One important abilities for penetration-testing is
analyzing the last command output, and basing the next executed action on
this information. As shown in Chapter 4, Llama3.1’s capabilities for integrating
information are lacking as it issues random commands instead of grounding its
selection on the experienced command outputs. To combat this, we force the
LLM to perform an analysis by using the context compression method [32, 10],
in which the LLM updates its worldview after each iteration with the most recent
output. To further improve the quality of the analysis, we explicitly prompt the
LLM for an analysis instead of prompting it to update its current worldview.

State The last method we propose is called State. Its structure is identical
to the mentioned history compression method [10, 32], but instead of replacing
the action history the compressed history is included in addition to the history,
potentially combined with the History Compression method. Updating the state
after each iteration triggers an implicit analysis, which should help with the
problem of the LLM ignoring the outputs, reduce repetition, and provide missing
structure.

We conduct a preliminary analysis (Section A) to identify promising treatments.
Based on the results, we made two key adaptations: (1) RAG-derived information

    No Duplicates and State were excluded as they did not improve results during

the explicit deduplication mechanism redundant.

6   Prototype
We extend hackingBuddyGPT ’s architecture (Figure 2) with new components.
The core query_next_command prompt (Figure 7 in the Appendix) includes
scenario information, instructions, and the analysis, generating chain-of-thought
reasoning from which commands are extracted and executed. After execution,
the output is forwarded to rag_prompt (Figure 9, generating a vector store
search query) and analyze_cmd (Figure 8, producing an analysis incorporating
the command output, retrieved RAG documents, and optional guidance). The
analysis feeds back into the next query_next_command iteration.

CoT Standard zero-shot CoT [13] proved ineffective for our models. We instead
combine Plan-and-Solve [28] with zero-shot prompting into Extract-and-Think :
"Let’s first understand the problem and extract the most important facts from
the information above. Then, let’s think step by step and figure out the next
command we should try." Commands are extracted via <command></command>
tags.
12      Benjamin Probst, Andreas Happe, and Jürgen Cito

RAG We use langchain with two data sources: HackTricks [19] (43 pages on
Linux privilege-escalation attacks, split into chunks of 1000 tokens) and GT-
FOBins [18] (390 UNIX binaries exploitable in misconfigured systems). We use
text-embedding-3-small from OpenAI as embedding model. In each iteration,
the LLM generates a search query based on the last command output, relevant
documents are retrieved and trimmed to 1200 tokens, and the result is included
in the analysis prompt.

Analyze After each iteration, the LLM is provided with the executed command,
its output, and then tasked to analyze it. The analysis is included in the next
query_next_command prompt.

History Compression Instead of including all the commands and their respective
output in each query_next_command prompt, we remove all outputs except the
most recent one.

SvP The SvP document provides the LLM with a structured checklist of common
Linux privilege-escalation attack vectors and their associated enumeration com-
mands. We generated it by prompting gpt-4o-2024-05-13 for a comprehensive,
unbiased enumeration of Linux privilege-escalation techniques, organized by vul-
nerability class (SUID/sudo misconfigurations, cron jobs, writable files, kernel
exploits, etc.), with each entry listing the relevant reconnaissance commands.
We manually reviewed the output to remove duplicates and verify command
correctness against standard references (GTFOBins [18], HackTricks [19]). The
resulting document contains 47 commands across 12 vulnerability classes and
is included verbatim in the query_next_command prompt. Since SvP contains
only enumeration commands—not exploitation payloads—the risk of introduc-
ing hallucinated or harmful content is limited; the LLM uses these commands
for discovery and must still independently reason about exploitation. The full
SvP content and generation history are available in our artifact repository.


7     Evaluation

We evaluate treatments across four configurations: baseline, baseline+guidance,
treatments, and treatments+guidance, comparing open-weight models against
their enhanced variants and cloud-based baselines. WhiteRabbitNeo is excluded
from parts of the analysis due to frequent timeouts.


7.1   General Performance

Without guidance or treatments enabled, none of the open-weight models can
match the performance of GPT-4o. Qwen2.5 7B, LLama3.1 8B, GPT-4o mini
and WhiteRabbitNeo have success rates of 8%, while Llama3.1 70B reaches 17%.
GPT-4o, on the other hand, achieves 42%.

7.3   Ablation Study
To investigate the impact of the different treatments, we conduct an ablation
study using Llama3.1 8B with enabled guidance mechanism. With five different
treatments, we have 25 = 32 possible combinations. For our purposes, only 24 are
relevant: eight combinations are redundant due to the dependency between RAG
and Analyze. Since the information provided by RAG is only included through
the Analyze prompt, combinations where RAG is enabled without Analyze are
functionally equivalent to variations were both are disabled. The results are
shown in Figure 3 and Table 4.
    All configurations, with one exception, lead to a significant increase in suc-
cess rate and solve at least five test cases. The highest-scoring combination is
analyze+CoT+RAG, with 8 successfully solved test cases, matching the perfor-
mance of the cloud-based baseline.
    The most impactful treatment is Analyze, which appears in all top-ten config-
urations. Of the top 10 configurations, 6 use either History Compression or CoT,
                                 SLMs for Linux Privilege Escalation Attacks     17

5 use RAG, and 4 use SvP. While RAG appears only six times in the top ten,
its impact is disproportionately high given that only eight8 valid combinations
include RAG.
    In isolation, both Analyze and History Compression achieve a success rate of
50% (6 solved test-cases), CoT achieves 42% (5), while SvP matches the baseline
with 17% (2). The combination of RAG and Analyze achieves 58% (7 out of 12).
This confirms that reflection-oriented interventions (Analyze, CoT, RAG) are
the primary drivers, and their benefits are largely additive.


8     Discussion

We organize this discussion around our two research questions, then examine
emergent failure modes, implications, threats to validity, and limitations.


8.1    RQ1: Closing the Performance Gap

Without any treatments, SLMs achieve 8–17% success rates compared to GPT-
4o’s 42%. With the full treatment stack and guidance, Llama3.1 70B reaches 83%
and both Llama3.1 8B and Qwen2.5 7B reach 67%, matching or surpassing GPT-
4o’s guided baseline of 67%. Even GPT-4o mini improves from 8% to 75%. These
results confirm that the poor baseline performance of SLMs reflects inadequate
scaffolding rather than an inherent capacity limitation.
    However, the gap closes fully only when guidance is provided. Without guid-
ance, treatments alone yield modest SLM improvements (8% to 17%), while
GPT-4o mini benefits substantially (8% to 42%). This asymmetry points to a
deeper issue explored in Section 8.3: vulnerability discovery remains the primary
bottleneck for open-weight models.


8.2    RQ2: The Dominance of Reflection-Based Treatments

The ablation study reveals a clear hierarchy: reflection-oriented interventions
(Analyze, CoT) are the primary drivers of performance gains, while structural
interventions (SvP, History Compression) play supporting roles.
    Analyze appears in all top-ten configurations and achieves 50% in isolation,
making it the single most impactful treatment. Its effectiveness stems from ad-
dressing SLMs’ core deficiency: the failure to integrate command outputs into
subsequent decisions. Analyze forces the model to explicitly process each out-
put before selecting the next action, transforming the baseline’s “act-then-act”
pattern into a “reflect-then-act” loop. This addresses the root cause of multi-
ple failure modes (repetition, ignoring outputs, unstructured exploration) rather
than treating symptoms individually.

     CoT and RAG amplify this through complementary mechanisms. CoT struc-
tures reasoning within the command-generation step, reducing hallucinated ca-
pabilities and complex invalid commands. RAG fills domain-specific knowledge
gaps surfaced during analysis, e.g., providing exploitation techniques for spe-
cific SUID binaries that SLMs lack in their parametric knowledge. The best-
performing ablation configuration (Analyze+CoT+RAG, 67%) demonstrates that
these three components are largely additive, each addressing a distinct bottle-
neck in the observe-reason-act cycle. Notably, even the 8B-parameter Llama3.1
reaches 67% with the full stack, suggesting that the treatments externalize rea-
soning steps that larger models perform implicitly, compensating for capacity
limitations rather than merely amplifying existing capabilities [2].


8.3   The Discovery Bottleneck

The most significant remaining limitation is vulnerability discovery. Without
guidance, no model (including GPT-4o) searched for passwords in files (test-
7/8/10), despite this being a standard manual check. This explains the impact of
guidance: it primes the discovery phase, allowing models to focus on exploitation.
    Combining guidance with treatments substantially increased success rates
across all models. Without treatments, the improvements from guidance alone
were significantly smaller, with Qwen2.5 7B showing no improvement at all. Log
analysis showed that both Llama3.1 8B and Qwen2.5 7B located the respective
vulnerability in 7 out of 12 test-cases but either ignored the information or failed
to exploit it, highlighting that the intervention stack is what enables models to
act on guidance.
    Discovery involves two distinct sub-challenges. The first is finding poten-
tially vulnerable configurations: SvP addresses this by providing enumeration
directions, and models consistently locate relevant binaries when SvP covers
the vulnerability class. However, SvP’s coverage is limited by its curated con-
tent; for example, the checklist omitted credential searches in user files, leav-
ing information-disclosure vulnerabilities unaddressed. The second is recogniz-
ing findings as exploitable: in test-1, all models found a python binary with a
set SUID bit but focused on common SUID binaries instead. Analyze improves
recognition but remains bounded by the model’s parametric knowledge.
    These findings converge with those of Peres [29], which similarly reports that
guidance remains essential for open-weight models. Both approaches—multi-
model delegation and single-model scaffolding—arrive at the same conclusion:
current open-weight models lack the broad reconnaissance intuition needed for
autonomous discovery.

Realism of Guidance. While our benchmark uses manually authored hints, com-
parable information is routinely produced by automated enumeration tools such
as LinPEAS or linux-exploit-suggester, which highlight misconfigured SUID bi-
naries, writable cron jobs, or credential files at a similar abstraction level. Inte-
grating such tools as a pre-processing step is a natural extension of our pipeline.
                               SLMs for Linux Privilege Escalation Attacks     19

Crucially, guidance alone is insufficient (baseline+guidance reaches only 8–33%
for SLMs), confirming that the interventions are the enabling factor.


8.4    Emergent Failure Modes

While the treatments resolve the six original failure modes (Section 4), they
introduce new ones characteristic of scaffolding-dependent systems.
    Guidance-Induced Errors. Guidance can both mislead and be misinter-
preted. In test-11, the hint “there is a periodic backup script” caused all models
to search for a file literally named backup. More subtly, in test-3, Llama3.1 8B
correctly identifies an exploitable sudo/tar misconfiguration but refuses to ex-
ecute the exploit: it interprets “there might be some bad sudo binaries” as a
warning not to trust sudo, preventing it from running the correct exploitation
command.9
    RAG-Induced Hallucinations. RAG can overwhelm models with infor-
mation about tools absent from the target system, leading to hallucinated com-
mands referencing searchsploit, exploit.sh, or backdoor.sh. This suggests that
RAG retrieval should be filtered against the target’s actual tool inventory.
    Multi-Command Dilution. SLMs frequently return multiple commands
per iteration (see Figure 5 in the Appendix), unlike GPT-4o mini which con-
sistently returns one. This dilutes the subsequent RAG search query, which is
generated from the combined output of all commands rather than a single fo-
cused result.


8.5    Implications

Our findings carry implications for both sides of the security equation. From
a defensive perspective, discovery, not exploitation, is the primary bottleneck
for automated attackers. Minimizing attack and thus reconnaissance surfaces
directly limits automated exploitation, even against capable scaffolding. From
an offensive perspective, locally hosted models are now viable for autonomous
privilege escalation, eliminating the need to transmit sensitive data to external
providers. The token overhead of SLMs (up to 281% more output tokens) rep-
resents a compute cost borne entirely locally, a favorable trade-off when data
sovereignty is a concern.


8.6    Threats to Validity

Selection bias. Both the model selection and the benchmark could be subject
to bias. An exhaustive privilege-escalation benchmark is infeasible, and frequent
SOTA releases preclude evaluating all models. To mitigate this, we selected three
well-known LLM families (Llama3.1, Qwen2.5, GPT) covering both open-weight
and closed-source models.

    Randomness. We use a non-zero temperature (0.8) to reflect real-world con-
ditions and run each configuration three times. More repetitions would improve

    Data contamination. The benchmark testbed [9] was released in 2023,

appear in training data. This applies equally to all models, preserving relative
comparisons. The low SLM baselines (0–17%) further suggest that exposure
alone does not confer exploitation capability.

8.7   Ethical Considerations
All experiments were conducted on isolated virtual machines under the full con-
trol of the research team; no real-world systems were targeted or affected. The
benchmark vulnerabilities are synthetic and intentionally introduced, and no
unauthorized access to third-party infrastructure occurred at any point. We ac-
knowledge the dual-use nature of this research: improving the autonomous attack
capabilities of locally hosted models could lower the barrier for malicious use.
However, we believe that understanding these capabilities is essential for the de-
fensive community, as it enables the development of more effective detection and
hardening strategies against automated privilege-escalation attacks. To support
reproducibility and responsible use, all artifacts will be made publicly available
upon acceptance.

9     Conclusion
We presented a systematic empirical study of whether established system-level
and prompting interventions can close the performance gap between small open-
weight models and cloud-based LLMs for autonomous Linux privilege escala-
tion. Our results demonstrate that a combination of five interventions (chain-of-
thought prompting, retrieval-augmented generation, structured prompts, history
compression, and reflective analysis) enables models with fewer than 10B param-
eters to match or surpass cloud-based models like GPT-4o, while reducing con-
text usage. A full-factorial ablation study identifies reflection-based components
as the primary drivers of these gains.
    However, vulnerability discovery remains a bottleneck for open-weight models
without guidance, suggesting that defenders should prioritize minimizing infor-
mation leakage and reconnaissance surfaces as the most effective countermeasure
against automated local attackers.

A.2      Evaluation

The runs can be found on github10 and the results can be seen in Table 6. We
discuss benchmark & metrics, as well as experiment design in Chapter 7.
    LLama3.1 8B performs similarly to its predecessor Llama3 [10], solving only
a single test. Guidance slightly improves the performance from 8% to 17%. Our
treatment ideas barely change the success rate from 8% to 17%, but allow the
LLM to solve different tests. Combining our treatments and guidance increases
the score from 17% to 50%.
    GPT-4o mini has a similar success rate to LLama3.1, reaching 8% without
and 25% with guidance. Compared to LLama, our treatments alone already
lead to a significant improvement, increasing the score from 8% to 42%. With
guidance and treatments enabled, GPT-4o mini has a success rate of 67%.

     RAG is effective at filling knowledge gaps. In most runs, when the vector store

ing capabilities. In most iterations, both LLama3.1 8B and ChatGPT-4o mini

4o mini tends to ignore this part of the CoT template and instead states its
reasoning and suggested command.
     For history compression, we also found, that it fulfills its goal of removing
noise. The major disadvantage of History Compression is that if the LLM iden-
tifies essential information but does not use it immediately in the next iteration,
that information is lost because the output is purged and the next one is in-
cluded. The test runs for our initial prototype show that this problem is not
severe. In most cases, the vulnerability is found multiple times. Furthermore,

likely that the LLM would successfully extract the correct output, among all the
irrelevant outputs and the analyze component, and then correctly exploit it.


New Problems Although the treatments are successful in resolving identified
problems and substantially increasing the performance of the tested models, it
also introduces new issues. The inclusion of RAG and Analyze led to a significant
increase in the information in the query_next_command prompt, which overloads
it. This results in a variety of problems. For example, models are far more likely
to extract useless facts and miss important information during the CoT process.
This happens frequently to LLama3.1 8B, where it ignores the guidance or essen-
tial information from the analysis. An additional consequence of an overflowing
prompt is the "Lost in the Middle" Problem [16], which states, that performance
can significantly decrease, depending on the position of relevant information in
the prompt.
    Another issue, that we identified is, that the analysis is often irrelevant and
acts as noise in the query_next_command prompt, possibly misleading and con-
fusing the LLM. This can occur, if the LLM is missing knowledge to make a
proper analysis or if the most recent output does not contain any relevant infor-
mation and the LLM overanalyzes the situation.
    Finally, we also found, that in some scenarios RAG provides the correct com-
mand/way to exploit a vulnerability, but the LLM changes it to an invalid/useless
one and executes this new version.
