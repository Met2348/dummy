1   Introduction
More than 18 million cancer survivors live in the United States in 2026, and almost a third experience clinically
significant depression, anxiety, or emotional distress in the years following treatment [39, 59]. Clinical guidelines
now recommend routine screening and management of these symptoms [6], yet timely and scalable mental health
interventions remain underdeveloped. Just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAIs) aim to use mobile technology
to offer support to survivors at precisely the moments when support is most needed and most likely to help [42].
Effective JITAIs could reduce the mental health burden of cancer survivors at scale. However, an effective JITAI
must do more than detect distress—it must infer whether support is currently desired and whether the survivor
is available to engage with different types of support were it to be offered. In JITAI terminology, this process
is determined by tailoring variables, intervention options, decision points, and decision rules, or components that
determine when, whether, and how to intervene from real-time signals [43].
   The dominant approach to estimating if an intervention should be delivered relies on ecological momentary
assessment (EMA), which are brief self-report surveys delivered multiple times daily to a survivor through their
mobile phone [57]. While EMA provides invaluable ground truth for research, it introduces a fundamental paradox
when used as the input to a proactive support system: the very moments when intervention is most needed are
precisely when self-report data are most likely to be absent [20, 41]. We call this the diary paradox. For example, one
cancer survivor experiencing acute distress may stop responding to surveys because they are too overwhelmed;
another survivor caught in a depressive episode may lack the motivation to complete a diary entry. As a result,
the data that would be most clinically informative for these hypothetical survivors—those that capture periods of
high distress and low functioning—would be missing not at random. In CALLM [71], the authors highlighted
that diary text is highly predictive of emotional states when available, but it cannot be used to predict emotional
states when the diary itself is absent. A truly proactive system must be able to reason about the user’s state from
data that requires no active participation.
   Passive smartphone sensing provides exactly this: continuous, unobtrusive streams of behavioral data—
movement patterns, location traces, screen usage, sleep timing, and social communication—that accumulate
regardless of the user’s engagement with diaries or other self-report prompts. A decade of mobile sensing
research, from StudentLife [68] and early digital phenotyping [48, 55] to GLOBEM [76], has established that
these behavioral signals correlate with mental health outcomes. Yet, the translation from correlation to clinically
useful prediction has been frustratingly slow. Prior work reveals a persistent prediction ceiling: traditional
machine learning models achieve approximately 0.52-0.60 balanced accuracy on passive sensing-based affect

prediction, barely above chance for many targets, and this ceiling persists across datasets and feature engineering
approaches [2, 34, 76].
   Why does this ceiling exist? While there are several possibilities, we argue that the problem is not in the data
itself but in the reasoning that traditional models apply to these data. Fixed feature pipelines extract the same
summary statistics from every user on every day, regardless of context. They cannot notice that a particular
user’s 3:00 AM screen session is anomalous relative to their personal baseline, or that a sudden drop in GPS
mobility following a week of high activity might signal a withdrawal pattern. They cannot synthesize sleep
disruption, reduced social communication, and increased late-night phone use into a coherent clinical narrative.
The bottleneck lies not in feature extraction but contextual behavioral signal interpretation [44]—the kind of
integrative reasoning that a clinician performs when interpreting a patient’s behavioral record.
   Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) suggest a different path forward. LLMs can synthesize
behavioral evidence in natural language, and prior work has shown that they can reason about both mental
health text and wearable sensing summaries [26, 74, 77]. But most systems still hand the model a pre-formatted
summary and ask it to reason using whatever the fixed pipeline has already chosen to expose. The model may be
eloquent, yet it still cannot decide what to inspect, which anomaly to pursue, or how to compare today’s behavior
against the right personal baseline.
   In this paper, we ask two questions: (1) can LLM agents predict intervention opportunities from passive sensing
data, and (2) does giving the LLM autonomy to investigate sensor data—dynamically deciding which modalities to
query, how far back to look, and which anomalies to pursue—improve prediction over fixed analytical pipelines?
   We introduce Pulse, a system that shifts the paradigm from feature engineering to agentic investigation of
passive sensing. Rather than feeding pre-extracted features into a prediction model, Pulse equips an LLM agent
with eight purpose-built sensing query tools—implemented via the Model Context Protocol (MCP) [7]—and
allows the agent to investigate each user’s behavioral data over multiple turns. The agent decides what sensing
data modalities to examine, what temporal windows to query, what baseline comparisons to make, and how to
synthesize evidence into a prediction. This investigation strategy is not fully pre-specified by the system designer;
it emerges from the interaction between the agent’s self-emerged behavioral reasoning capabilities and the eight
tools it is offered, following the ReAct paradigm of interleaving reasoning with action [79]. In doing so, Pulse
operationalizes the idea of contextual behavioral signal interpretation as an interactive reasoning process rather
than a one-shot feature transform.
   To cleanly isolate the contribution of agentic investigation from data modality, we evaluate Pulse through a
2×2 factorial design crossing two dimensions: (1) reasoning architecture—structured (fixed pipeline, single LLM
call) versus agentic (autonomous multi-turn tool use); and (2) data modality—sensing-only (passive sensing data
alone) versus multimodal (sensing plus diary text). This yields four primary conditions—Struct-Sense, Auto-Sense,
Struct-Multi, and Auto-Multi— all evaluated on 50 cancer survivors from the BUCS (Better Understanding Cancer
Survivors) observational study, with each system producing predictions for all 4,112 EMA entries (50 users, 74–96
entries per user, mean 82.2).
   We focus on intervention opportunity as the conjunction of two complementary constructs. Emotion regulation
desire asks whether the user currently wants support. Intervention availability asks whether the user is behaviorally
able to engage with support right now. A useful JITAI must estimate both: a willing but unavailable user cannot
act on an intervention, while an available but uninterested user is unlikely to benefit from one.

       pipelines with adaptive, context-sensitive behavioral reasoning. We provide eight reusable MCP tools and
       an open architecture that the community can extend to other sensing domains.

2     Related Work
Pulse sits at the intersection of four research threads: passive mobile sensing for mental health, large language
models for health and affective computing, agentic AI with tool use, and just-in-time adaptive interventions
(JITAIs).

2.1     Passive Sensing for Mental Health
The foundational insight that smartphone sensor data correlates with mental health outcomes dates to Stu-
dentLife [68], which demonstrated associations between passively sensed behavioral patterns (sleep, sociability,
physical activity, phone usage) and depression, stress, and academic performance in college students. Saeb et
al. [55] further showed that GPS features—location variance, circadian movement, and time spent at home—
correlated with depressive symptom severity, while Canzian and Musolesi [10] demonstrated that mobility traces
alone could track depressive states. Onnela and Rauch [48] formalized the concept of “digital phenotyping,” and
Torous et al. [60] connected it to the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) framework for psychiatry. These early
studies launched a productive decade of mobile sensing for mental health research, supported by instrumenta-
tion frameworks such as AWARE [19], with systems extending to detecting relapse and symptom changes in
schizophrenia [8, 67], tracking depression dynamics [69], identifying digital biomarkers of mood disorders [25],
detecting behavioral changes during the COVID-19 pandemic [24, 47], and detecting depression onset from
longitudinal passive data [11]. Recent large-scale efforts include a four-year mobile sensing study of college
students [45] and systematic reviews of digital phenotyping for mental health monitoring [12, 35].
   Alongside these advances, however, a persistent challenge has emerged: predictive models trained in one context
rarely generalized to another. The GLOBEM benchmark [76], which systematically evaluated 18 algorithms across
four longitudinal sensing datasets, revealed that most models achieve balanced accuracy in the range of 0.50–0.55
on depression and affect prediction, with cross-dataset transfer often performing at chance. Meegahapola et
al. [34] replicated this ceiling across eight countries for mood inference, and Adler et al. [2] documented the
same pattern across longitudinal sensing datasets, showing that models trained on one study population failed to
generalize to another. Feng et al. [18] recently provided a comprehensive comparison of traditional ML, deep
                       PULSE: Agentic Investigation with Passive Sensing for Proactive Intervention in Cancer Survivorship   •   0:5


learning, and LLM approaches on smartphone sensing data, confirming that standard supervised approaches
plateau around the same accuracy levels.
   As Adler et al. [3] argued in “Beyond Detection,” the field must move from passive detection of correlates
to actionable clinical support. The problem is not that smartphone sensors lack informative signals—they do
contain them—but that fixed feature pipelines cannot perform the contextual interpretation needed to translate
raw signals into clinically meaningful predictions. This explanation is intuitive: The same 500 steps of walking
means different things for a user who typically walks 5,000 steps versus one who averages 200, and its emotional
significance likely depends on time of day, concurrent phone usage, location context, and the user’s longitudinal
trajectory.
   Pulse addresses this limitation directly: rather than engineering better features, it gives an LLM agent the
tools to investigate behavioral data in context, performing the kind of personalized, integrative reasoning that
fixed pipelines cannot.



2.2   LLMs for Affective Computing and Health
The application of LLMs to health prediction has evolved rapidly, building on the few-shot reasoning capabilities
first demonstrated in GPT-3 [9] and the instruction-following capabilities of RLHF-aligned models [49]. Singhal
et al. [58] showed that LLMs encode substantial clinical knowledge, while Cosentino et al. [15] demonstrated that
LLMs can reason about personal health data including sleep and fitness metrics. Health-LLM [26] established
that LLMs can reason about wearable sensor data across multiple health tasks, evaluating 12 models on 10
prediction targets with various prompting strategies. Mental-LLM [77] demonstrated LLM-based mental health
prediction from online text data, achieving state-of-the-art results on depression and stress detection benchmarks.
Zhang et al. [81] were among the first to combine LLMs with smartphone sensing features for affect prediction,
showing that LLMs with structured sensor summaries could outperform traditional ML in some configurations.
SensorLLM [32] further explored aligning LLMs with raw motion sensor data for activity recognition, and a
recent survey [53] documents the rapid growth of LLM applications for wearable health data. Zhang et al. [82]
provide a reality check on LLM sentiment analysis, highlighting both capabilities and limitations relevant to
affect prediction.
   The system most directly related to our work is CALLM [71], which demonstrated that combining diary text
with TF-IDF-based cross-user retrieval and LLM reasoning achieves strong performance on emotional state
prediction for cancer survivors (the same population and prediction targets we address here). However, CALLM
is fundamentally reactive: it requires diary text as input and cannot make predictions when diary entries are
absent. Pulse extends CALLM’s approach from reactive, diary-dependent prediction to proactive, sensing-based
investigation, replacing diary dependence with agentic investigation of passive sensing data.
   Several systems have explored related uses of LLMs. MindScape [46] integrates LLMs with behavioral sensing
to personalize and contextualize LLM-generated journal prompts. GLOSS [13] employs multiple LLMs for open-
ended sense-making of passive sensing data, using code generation to analyze behavioral patterns. LENS [75]
aligns multimodal sensing data with language models for mental health narrative synthesis. Vital Insight [31]
combines visualization with human-in-the-loop LLM analysis for expert-driven sensemaking of personal tracking
data. PHIA [36] represents the closest conceptual comparison: it is an LLM agent that generates and executes code
to analyze wearable data, answering retrospective health questions. IoT-LLM [5] provides a general framework
for LLM reasoning over IoT sensor data. These systems collectively demonstrate that LLMs can reason about
health-relevant behavioral data across a range of modalities and populations. However, they differ substantially
in the degree of autonomy granted to the model—a distinction we examine next through the lens of agentic AI.

2.3   Agentic AI and Tool Use
The agentic AI paradigm, in which LLMs autonomously plan, reason, and take actions through tool use, has
emerged as a powerful framework for complex problem-solving. A foundational enabler is chain-of-thought
prompting [73], which unlocks multi-step reasoning in LLMs and underlies the deliberative processes of agentic
systems. ReAct [79] demonstrated that interleaving reasoning traces with tool-use actions produces more reliable
and interpretable outcomes than either pure reasoning or pure action. Toolformer [56] showed that LLMs can
learn to use tools in a self-supervised manner, and Gorilla [51] extended this to massive API function calling.
Voyager [65] introduced the concept of embodied LLM agents with growing skill libraries that explore open-ended
environments—an analogy that resonates with Pulse’s approach of equipping agents with investigation tools for
an open-ended behavioral data space. Lewis et al. [30] introduced retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), which
provides the foundation for Pulse’s cross-user calibration mechanism, and Gao et al. [4] recently surveyed the
growing role of RAG in healthcare applications.
   More recently, Wang et al. [66] provided a comprehensive survey of LLM-based autonomous agents, categoriz-
ing approaches by their planning, memory, and tool-use capabilities. Huang et al. [23] offered a complementary
analysis focused specifically on the planning capabilities of LLM agents. The Model Context Protocol (MCP) [7]
has emerged as an open standard for connecting LLMs to external tools and data sources, providing a repro-
ducible interface specification. ContextAgent [78] demonstrated proactive LLM agents with wearable sensing
perceptions in an egocentric modality space. In the clinical domain, ReflecTool [33] introduced reflection-aware
tool-augmented agents that build long-term memory from successful problem-solving episodes and verify tool
usage through iterative refinement, demonstrating that structured reflection over tool outcomes can improve
clinical task performance.
   An important caveat for health sensing is that LLMs are unreliable numerical reasoners when asked to directly
manipulate tabular data; they can produce fluent but incorrect interpretations. Pulse addresses this by assigning
counting, aggregation, thresholding, and temporal retrieval to deterministic tool code, while reserving the LLM
for contextual interpretation of those results. This division of labor is central to our design and to our claim that
agentic value comes from investigation strategy rather than from letting the model “do the math” itself.

2.4    Just-in-Time Adaptive Interventions
The JITAI framework [42, 43] identifies six key components for adaptive intervention systems: decision points,
intervention options, tailoring variables, decision rules, proximal outcomes, and distal outcomes. Tailoring
variables—the real-time signals used to adapt intervention delivery—are recognized as the lynchpin of effective
JITAI design, yet estimating them in real time from passive data remains the central challenge [42]. Even still, a
recent meta-analysis [17] found that JITAIs produce small but significant improvements in mental health outcomes
across diverse populations, despite finding that JITAIs in mental health are mostly stuck in the conceptual stage
and have rarely moved beyond initial piloting. This emphasizes their value while showing that there is room for
improvement.
   Kunzler et al. [29] introduced the concept of “state-of-receptivity” for mHealth interventions, decomposing
receptivity into separate components including physical availability and psychological openness. Mishra et al. [38]
advanced this with in-the-wild receptivity detection using smartphone and wearable sensors, demonstrating that
contextual factors (time, activity, phone usage) predict intervention engagement. Klasnja et al. [27] developed the
microrandomized trial (MRT) methodology for evaluating JITAI components, later applied in HeartSteps [28] for
physical activity promotion. Mohr et al. [40] introduced the IntelliCare suite of skills-based apps for treating
depression and anxiety, illustrating that digital interventions can adapt to user needs. Notification timing in
behavior change contexts has also been studied through MRT designs [21].
   Pulse’s prediction targets—emotion regulation desire (ERdesire ) and intervention availability (INTavail )—map
directly to the various JITAI components. ERdesire , grounded in the emotion regulation framework of Gross [22],
captures the psychological dimension of receptivity (does the user want support?), while INTavail captures the
behavioral dimension (is the user in a context where they can engage?). Our finding that these two constructs
are best predicted by different data modalities has direct implications for JITAI design, as we discuss in Section 6.

3     System Design
Figure 2 presents the Pulse system architecture. The system follows a three-stage pipeline: Sense (passive
smartphone data and optional diary text), Think (an LLM agent that investigates behavioral data through a
ReAct-style reasoning loop with eight MCP sensing tools and cross-user RAG for calibration), and Inference
(predicted affect, emotion regulation desire, and intervention availability, accompanied by natural-language
reasoning and a confidence score). After each prediction, the real outcome—defined as the conjunction of elevated
ground-truth desire to emotionally regulate and self-reported intervention availability—feeds an agent reflection
step whose summary is stored in persistent per-user memory for future episodes. Figure 3 illustrates a concrete
investigation trace from the evaluation.

3.3   The BUCS Dataset
Pulse is evaluated on the BUCS dataset [16], a longitudinal study of 407 adult cancer survivors who completed
5 weeks of data collection. The dataset was obtained with the original study team and used with institutional
review board approval. Data collection included:
      • Ecological Momentary Assessment [57]: Three daily surveys (randomly delivered each day in the
        morning, afternoon, evening) capturing positive and negative affect ratings, discrete emotions, emotion
        regulation desire, intervention availability, and brief diary entries about emotional drivers.
      • Passive Smartphone Sensing: Continuously collected streams including accelerometer-based motion
        (stationary, walking, running, automotive activity), GPS location (distance traveled, time at home, location
        variance), screen usage (session counts, durations, lock/unlock patterns), app usage (category-level
        durations; Android only), and sleep detection (accelerometer-derived sleep periods). Keyboard activity
        (typing sessions, character counts) is sparsely available given participants’ willingness and agreement to
        share this modality.
      • User Profiles: Baseline demographic information, cancer type and treatment history, and validated
        psychological trait measures.
  Platform differences between iOS and Android affect data availability: app-level usage is available only on
Android (approximately 36% of the test sample). The sensing tools are designed to gracefully handle missing
modalities, returning explicit “data not available” responses that the agent incorporates into its reasoning. The

3.4   Structured Agents
The structured agents (Struct-Sense, Struct-Multi) implement a fixed pipeline in which all available data is
pre-assembled and the LLM produces a prediction in a single call. This design mirrors the predominant approach
in prior LLM-for-sensing work [26, 81].
   Input Assembly. For each prediction, the system pre-computes a sensing summary covering all available
modalities for the current day (sleep metrics, mobility statistics, screen usage patterns, activity breakdowns).
This summary, along with the user’s trait profile, a longitudinal memory document, and cross-user calibration
examples, is assembled into a structured prompt.
   Cross-User Calibration. To ground the LLM’s predictions in empirical distributions, both structured and
agentic agents have access to cross-user calibration data. For structured agents, this takes the form of TF-IDF-based
text retrieval (in multimodal mode) or cosine-similarity-based sensing fingerprint matching (in sensing-only mode)
against a peer database constructed from training-fold participants. The top-𝑘 most similar entries, including
their ground truth EMA outcomes, are included in the prompt as calibration anchors.
   Prompting Strategy. Within a single LLM call, the prompt asks the model to consider the available behavioral
modalities (sleep, mobility and activity, and social signals), reference the peer calibration anchors, and produce a
final prediction. The natural-language reasoning the model generates inside the prompt is open-ended, but the
input context and the elicited output schema are held fixed across all predictions to keep this baseline a faithful
representative of the predominant “pre-assemble and prompt” approach in prior LLM-for-sensing work.

3.5   Agentic Agents
The agentic agents (Auto-Sense, Auto-Multi) implement autonomous behavioral investigation through a multi-
turn tool-use loop, leveraging chain-of-thought reasoning [73] interleaved with tool actions following the ReAct
paradigm [79]. Rather than receiving a pre-formatted data summary, the agent receives a system prompt describing
its clinical task, a user profile, an optional diary entry, and access to eight MCP sensing query tools. For example,
the sensing-only system prompt begins:
       You are an expert behavioral data scientist specializing in affective computing and just-in-time adaptive in-
       terventions for cancer survivorship. Your task: predict the emotional state of a cancer survivor at the moment
       they are about to complete an EMA survey, using ONLY their passive smartphone sensing data. You have access
       to MCP sensing tools that let you query their behavioral data. Use these tools like a detective—investigate the
       signals that matter, compare to their personal baseline, look for behavioral anomalies.
   The multimodal variant additionally instructs the agent to analyze the current diary entry and use sensing
tools to validate and calibrate diary-derived hypotheses. The agent’s investigation strategy emerges from the
interaction between its clinical reasoning and the available tools.
3.5.1 MCP Sensing Tools. A core design principle is the separation of computation from interpretation. All
counting, aggregation, thresholding, and temporal retrieval is handled by deterministic tool code; the LLM
receives only natural-language results and is responsible solely for contextual interpretation. This division
of labor directly addresses the known unreliability of LLMs as numerical reasoners [18, 26] while preserving
their strength in integrative reasoning. Our tool design is further grounded in two additional principles. First,
clinical reasoning in behavioral assessment is hypothesis-driven: practitioners begin with an overview, form
provisional explanations, and then seek confirming and disconfirming evidence through targeted investigation.
Second, affect prediction from behavioral data is strongly idiographic: what matters is often not an absolute
value but whether today’s pattern deviates from this person’s own norm. We therefore provide tools that sup-
port broad orientation (get_daily_summary, get_behavioral_timeline), targeted follow-up (query_sensing,
query_raw_events), within-person contextualization (compare_to_baseline, find_similar_days), and cross-
user calibration (find_peer_cases).
0:12 • Zhiyuan Wang, Ariful Islam, Indrajeet Ghosh, Xinyu Chen, Katharine E. Daniel, Subigya Nepal, Philip Chow, and Laura E. Barnes


   We implement eight domain-specific tools via the Model Context Protocol [7], each designed to mirror a step
in clinical behavioral chart review:
    (1) get_daily_summary: Returns a natural-language overview of a day’s behavioral patterns across all
        sensing modalities. Supports multi-day lookback for trend analysis. Serves as the typical starting point
        for investigation.
    (2) get_behavioral_timeline: Reconstructs the day as a chronological timeline of behavioral segments
        (e.g., “6:00–9:00 AM: stationary, low screen use, possible sleep continuation; 9:00–12:00 PM: walking
        detected, moderate screen activity”). Configurable segment width (1–6 hours).
    (3) query_sensing: Targeted query for a specific modality within a configurable time window (1–48 hours
        before the EMA, with 1–24 hour durations). Returns hourly or daily aggregates. Allows the agent to zoom
        into specific signals of interest.
    (4) query_raw_events: Accesses the raw event stream for a modality (individual app sessions, screen
        lock/unlock events, activity transitions, typing sessions). Provides fine-grained temporal detail when the
        agent needs to resolve specific questions (e.g., “When did she first unlock her phone this morning?”).
    (5) compare_to_baseline: Computes z-scores comparing a current metric to the user’s personal historical
        distribution. Enables anomaly detection—the agent can identify when today’s behavior deviates from this
        individual’s norm, which is more informative than absolute values.
    (6) get_receptivity_history: Retrieves the user’s past intervention availability patterns and aggregate
        mood trends over a configurable lookback window (up to 14 days). Provides temporal context for receptivity
        prediction.
    (7) find_similar_days: Identifies past days with the most similar behavioral fingerprint (cosine similarity
        over normalized sensing features) and returns the user’s actual emotional states on those days. Enables
        analogical reasoning: “On past days with similar behavior, this user felt. . . ”
    (8) find_peer_cases: Searches across other participants for similar cases via text (TF-IDF similarity of diary
        entries) or sensing (behavioral fingerprint similarity) and returns their ground truth outcomes. Provides
        population-level calibration anchors.
  All tools enforce a strict temporal boundary: only data collected before the current EMA timestamp is accessible.
This prevents information leakage and mirrors the prospective deployment scenario where the system must
predict before knowing the outcome.
3.5.2 Investigation Loop. The PULSE agent operates through a multi-turn conversation with tool-use capabilities.
A typical investigation proceeds as follows:
    (1) The agent reads the user profile and (if available) diary entry, forming initial hypotheses about the user’s
        likely emotional state.
    (2) It calls get_daily_summary to orient itself on today’s behavioral patterns.
    (3) It calls get_behavioral_timeline to understand how the day unfolded chronologically.
    (4) Based on these overviews, it formulates targeted queries—for example, calling query_sensing on screen
        usage if the summary suggests unusual phone behavior, or query_raw_events on app usage to inspect
        which applications were active.
    (5) It calls compare_to_baseline to contextualize observed metrics against the user’s personal history.
    (6) It uses find_similar_days and/or find_peer_cases to retrieve empirical calibration anchors.
    (7) It synthesizes all evidence into a prediction, outputting a structured JSON object with all 16 binary targets,
        3 continuous scores, a reasoning trace, and a confidence estimate.
  Critically, the agent is free to deviate from this typical order. It may skip steps it deems uninformative, revisit
earlier queries after discovering new information, or pursue investigation paths not anticipated by the system

4 Evaluation Methodology
4.1 Evaluation Setup
We evaluate Pulse on a test sample of 50 users randomly selected from the 399 BUCS participants with sufficient
data (mean 82.2 entries per user, range 74–96). We chose 50 users as a balance between inference cost and
statistical robustness. Each agentic user costs approximately $2–5 in LLM inference (across ∼80 multi-turn
predictions), making full-cohort evaluation of all 399 users across five systems prohibitively expensive. At the
same time, 50 users with ∼80 entries each provide sufficient power for within-subject factorial comparisons while
spanning diverse behavioral profiles. The main comparison evaluates the four factorial systems plus CALLM,
each on all 4,112 EMA entries for the 50 users.
4.1.1 Representativeness Analysis. To verify that the 50 randomly selected test users are representative, we
conducted a systematic representativeness analysis comparing them to the remaining 349 BUCS participants.
Table 3 presents the results.
   For three of the four focus prediction targets (PA_State, ER_desire, INT_availability), base rates do not differ
significantly between the test and full samples (𝑝 > .05). NA_State shows a modest difference (𝑝 = .028, 𝑟 = 0.19)—
the test sample has slightly lower negative affect base rates, a small effect that may slightly reduce the difficulty
of NA prediction but does not compromise the internal validity of the factorial comparisons, which are conducted

within the same 50 users across all conditions. The higher Android representation (36% vs. 25%, 𝑝 = .027) means

same 50 users, making representativeness relevant only for contextualizing absolute performance levels, not for

4.2   Baselines
4.2.1 Reference Baselines. We report three families of reference baselines to contextualize the aggregate score-
board. First, we include traditional sensing baselines (Random Forest, XGBoost, and Logistic Regression) trained on
hourly sensing features across six modalities: motion (stationary, walking, running, automotive minutes), screen
(total minutes, session count), GPS (travel distance, time at home, location variance), keyboard (session count,
character count), app usage (total and per-category minutes), and light (ambient lux). For each EMA timestamp,
features are extracted from hourly windows preceding the prediction point, yielding a flat feature vector (e.g.,
h0_screen_on_min, h1_motion_walking_min, etc.). The lookback horizon (1, 2, 4, 6, 12, or 24 hours), number of
selected features, and model hyperparameters are jointly optimized via grid search within 5-fold across-subject
cross-validation on the full 399-user dataset. Second, we include text-only baselines: TF-IDF+Logistic Regression,
Bag-of-Words+Logistic Regression, and MiniLM sentence embeddings+Logistic Regression. Third, we include
combined baselines (Random Forest and Logistic Regression) over the concatenation of hourly sensing features
and diary embeddings. All reference predictions are filtered to the same 50-user evaluation subset.
   These baselines are informative but not the paper’s cleanest fairness comparison. They differ from Pulse in
training population, feature representation, and data access. The primary comparative claim therefore remains the
within-same-50-users factorial contrast between structured and agentic agents operating on the same modality
conditions.

4.2.2 LLM Baseline. CALLM [71] serves as the LLM baseline, using diary text with TF-IDF cross-user retrieval
and longitudinal memory. CALLM represents the strongest existing LLM-based approach for affect prediction in
cancer survivor populations.


4.3   Metrics
Balanced Accuracy (BA) is the primary metric for binary targets, defined as the arithmetic mean of sensitivity
and specificity. BA is preferred over standard accuracy for health data with class imbalance, as it gives equal
weight to both classes regardless of base rates. Macro F1 is reported as a secondary metric.
  For all analyses, we report entries-level balanced accuracy computed across all predictions pooled from all 50
users, with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals (10,000 resamples). Statistical significance is assessed via bootstrap
resampling of the BA difference between conditions, with 𝑝-values computed as the proportion of resamples
where the observed advantage disappears.


4.4   Implementation
All LLM inference uses Claude Sonnet (Anthropic), a transformer-based [64] LLM, via the Claude CLI, with a
maximum of 5 concurrent processes and 16 tool-use turns per prediction. Sensing tools are served via an MCP
server launched as a subprocess for each prediction, ensuring per-user data isolation. Inference time ranges from
30 to 90 seconds per prediction depending on the number of tool calls. Each of the five systems (four factorial
plus CALLM) produces 4,112 predictions on the 50-user sample.

5.7   Calibration and Failure Modes
While Pulse’s binary predictions are strong, the underlying continuous predictions show systematic biases.
For Negative Affect, Auto-Multi over-predicts (predicted mean ∼6.2 vs. ground truth mean ∼3.2, MAE = 4.21),
reflecting a negativity bias in which the agent overestimates distress from diary cues. For Positive Affect, the
agent slightly under-predicts (predicted mean ∼16.7 vs. ground truth ∼18.0, MAE = 5.42), exhibiting variance
compression (predicted SD 4.7 vs. ground truth SD 8.1). ER desire shows the best continuous calibration (MAE =
1.74, 𝑟 = 0.61).
   Importantly, these continuous biases do not substantially impair binary classification performance because
the individualized state thresholds absorb much of the systematic offset. Whether a user’s predicted Negative
Affect of 9 is classified as “unusually high” depends on that user’s personal baseline, not on the population
mean. The structured agents (CALLM, Struct-Multi) show better-calibrated continuous predictions but worse
binary performance, suggesting that the agentic agents trade absolute calibration for more discriminative binary
decisions. At the same time, the negativity bias is a genuine failure mode: it indicates that stronger binary
performance should not be misread as perfect affect calibration.

5.8   Agentic Investigation Patterns
5.8.1 Tool Usage Patterns. Across all predictions (31,686 total tool calls), agentic agents invoke an average
of 7.5 tools per prediction. Three tools form a near-universal core: get_daily_summary (99.7% of predictions),
get_behavioral_timeline (99.7%), and find_peer_cases (99.7%). The anomaly detection tool compare_to_baseline
is used selectively (60.7%), while the remaining tools—find_similar_days (14.2%), query_sensing (4.9%),
query_raw_events (4.4%), and get_ receptivity_history (4.3%)—are deployed only when the agent’s initial
overview suggests they are needed. This emergent three-tool core strategy—orient with overview tools, then
calibrate with peers—was not prescribed; the agent converged on it autonomously.

5.8.2 Confidence Calibration. Each prediction includes a self-reported confidence score between 0 and 1 (mean
0.76, SD = 0.10). This confidence is well-calibrated with actual accuracy: predictions with confidence below 0.65
achieve 66.9% average accuracy across the four focus targets, predictions with confidence 0.65–0.80 achieve
74.1%, and predictions above 0.80 achieve 82.5%. We examine the relationship between confidence and ER_desire
accuracy in more detail in Section 5.10.

5.8.4 Example: Structured vs. Agentic on the Same Entry. Consider a multimodal prediction for a user whose diary
entry reads: “Feeling tired but trying to stay positive after my appointment.” The structured agent (Struct-Multi)
receives a pre-formatted summary showing moderate screen usage, low walking, and moderate GPS mobility. It
follows the fixed pipeline: notes adequate sleep, observes reduced mobility, notes the appointment mention in
the diary, and predicts slightly elevated positive affect based on the “stay positive” language.
   The agentic agent (Auto-Multi), given the same underlying data, pursues a different strategy:
    (1) Reads the diary and notes the tension between tiredness and positive coping.
    (2) Calls get_daily_summary and observes the day’s overall patterns.
    (3) Notices low walking minutes and calls compare_to_baseline on walking, discovering it is 1.8 standard
        deviations below this user’s personal mean.
    (4) Calls get_behavioral_timeline and observes a gap in activity during the morning hours, consistent
        with a medical appointment.
    (5) Calls query_sensing on screen usage and finds a prolonged phone session in the waiting room.
    (6) Calls find_similar_days and identifies three past days with similar behavioral patterns (low mobility
        + medical context), finding that the user’s affect on those days was lower than their diary language
        suggested.
    (7) Calls find_peer_cases with the diary text and finds that peers expressing similar “trying to stay positive”
        language had moderate-to-low positive affect.
    (8) Synthesizes: the diary expresses coping effort, not genuine positive affect; behavioral evidence confirms
        reduced activity; past analogs suggest the user’s positive affect is likely below their personal mean.
  The agentic agent’s prediction is more calibrated because it was able to (a) detect the anomalous mobility
through personal baseline comparison, (b) contextualize the diary language through peer calibration, and (c) use
analogical reasoning from the user’s own history. None of these reasoning steps are available to the structured
agent, which processes all data in a single pass without the ability to form and test hypotheses.

5.8.5 Case Study: Personalization Overrides Population Priors. The previous example illustrated how the agentic
agent uses tools to build evidence. The following real case from our evaluation (also depicted in Figure 3)
demonstrates a more subtle capability: the agent’s ability to override population-level evidence with accumulated
personal knowledge.
   Participant 089 is a cancer survivor with a stoic coping style—across 39 prior EMA entries, this person was
receptive to intervention in only 6% of cases. On December 22, their diary reads: “Funeral of close friend today.
Worried about mother’s declining health.” The agent’s investigation proceeds through five steps:
    (1) Diary analysis. The agent identifies two compounding stressors—grief and caregiving worry—and forms
        an initial hypothesis: elevated negative affect and likely elevated ER desire.
    (2) Behavioral overview. Calling get_behavioral_timeline and get_daily_summary for both the current
        day and the previous day reveals sustained withdrawal: fully stationary, only 2 minutes of screen use by
        mid-morning. This multi-day pattern is consistent with deep grief, not a transient bad morning.
    (3) Baseline anomaly detection. compare_to_baseline on screen usage confirms extreme disengagement:
        2 minutes versus this person’s typical 14 minutes per hour—far below their personal norm.

    (4) Peer calibration. find_peer_cases with “funeral friend” returns other cancer survivors who wrote
        about grief. Most reported elevated ER desire—they wanted emotional support. This creates a conflict: the
        population evidence suggests this person should want help.
    (5) Session memory resolves the conflict. The agent’s accumulated session memory contains reflections
        from 39 prior entries, including three prior grief episodes (December 15, 16, and 21). In every case, this
        person was not receptive. The agent’s memory records a consistent pattern: stoic, self-directed coping
        even under acute emotional distress. The agent overrides the peer prior and predicts low ER desire.
   The prediction is correct on all four binary targets: PA_State = low, NA_State = elevated, ER_desire_State = not
elevated, INT_availability = yes. The key reasoning step is the last one: the agent uses accumulated personal
experience from binary receptivity feedback—never having seen this person’s actual emotion scores—to override
a reasonable population-level prior. This is the core advantage of in-context personalization through session
memory.
5.8.6 Case Study: Two Learning Signals Enable Calibration. A natural question is how the agent produces accurate
predictions without access to the current user’s ground truth emotion labels. The following case illustrates the
two complementary information sources that make this possible.
   Participant 258 is a cancer survivor with high trait negative affect and moderate depression. On an evening
EMA, their diary reads: “Tired and a bit frazzled not sure why.” The agent draws on two types of evidence:
   Signal 1: Personal history from receptivity feedback. The agent has observed three prior entries for this
person. From these, it learned a pattern: when the diary is internally focused (describing feelings, e.g., “frazzled”),
the person tends to be receptive; when the diary is externally focused (describing activities, e.g., “nice weather”),
the person is not receptive. The current entry is internally focused, suggesting receptivity. Additionally, passive
sensing reveals 35.7 km of travel (z = +1.79 above baseline), indicating that the tiredness is physically driven by
an unusually active day rather than depressive withdrawal.
   Signal 2: Peer outcomes from cross-user retrieval. The agent calls find_peer_cases and retrieves 15 other
cancer survivors who wrote similar “tired” or “frazzled” diary entries. Among these peers, the majority had typical
positive affect, elevated negative affect, and elevated ER desire. This empirical distribution provides calibration
anchors that the agent cannot derive from reasoning alone. The agent anchors its prediction to the peer base
rates, then adjusts for this person’s high trait negative affect.
   The resulting prediction is correct on all four binary targets: PA_State = typical, NA_State = elevated,
ER_desire_State = elevated, INT_availability = yes. The two signals are complementary: receptivity feedback
from this person’s own history teaches the agent when this individual is likely to need intervention, while peer
outcomes teach what predictions are typical for people in similar situations. Together, they enable accurate
personalized prediction without direct access to the current user’s emotion labels.

5.9   Investigation Strategy Evolves Over Time
To understand how the agent’s investigation strategy changes with experience, we tracked tool usage across the
first 70 EMA entries per user. Total tool usage decreases from 8.8 calls per prediction in entries 1–5 to 7.4 calls in
entries 66–70, a reduction of 16%. Simultaneously, self-reported confidence increases from 0.69 to 0.76 (+10%).
   This efficiency gain is not uniform across tools. Two tools decrease substantially: compare_to_baseline drops
from 2.4 to 1.6 calls per entry (−33%), and get_receptivity_history drops from 0.4 to 0.1 calls per entry (−75%).
Both provide information that the agent internalizes into session memory over time—personal baselines and
receptivity patterns become part of the agent’s accumulated context, making explicit tool queries redundant. In
contrast, the three core tools (get_daily_summary, get_behavioral_timeline, find_peer_cases) remain per-
fectly stable, each at approximately 1.0–2.0 calls per entry throughout. These tools provide real-time information
that changes with each new day and cannot be memorized.

6 Discussion
6.1 Why Agentic Investigation Works
The 2×2 factorial results suggest that the agentic architecture—not merely the presence of an LLM or the addition
of more data—is the primary driver of prediction performance. Why might autonomous investigation outperform
a structured pipeline that receives the same underlying information?
   We hypothesize three contributing mechanisms, while noting that we do not yet have per-tool ablations to
establish them causally. First, selective attention: the agentic agent can focus its reasoning on the most informative
signals for a specific user and time point, rather than applying the same analytical pipeline to every example. A
user with disrupted sleep may trigger deeper sleep analysis; a user with unusual social behavior may trigger
communication-oriented investigation. The agent appears to allocate its limited reasoning budget adaptively.
   Second, anomaly detection through personal baselines: the compare_to_baseline tool transforms absolute
sensor values into deviations relative to the individual’s history, enabling the agent to detect what is unusual for
this person. The structured agent receives the same raw values, but it does not have an equally explicit mechanism
for iteratively testing those values against personal norms within a single-pass prompt.
   Third, analogical reasoning: through find_similar_days and find_peer_cases, the agent grounds its pre-
dictions in retrieved examples rather than relying only on parametric prior knowledge. When the agent observes
a behavioral pattern, it can look for what actually happened on matching days—both for the same user and for
similar users. This may function as a practical calibration mechanism that a fixed pipeline cannot easily emulate.
   These mechanisms connect to the Behavioral Signal Processing (BSP) framework proposed by Narayanan and
Georgiou [44] and to Xu et al.’s Penetrative AI framing [74]: the agentic agent appears to perform contextual
behavioral signal interpretation, extracting meaning from sensor streams through dynamic, context-sensitive
reasoning rather than through fixed feature engineering alone.
   A reasonable concern is whether the agentic advantage simply reflects making more LLM calls rather than
reflecting the investigation paradigm itself. We cannot fully disentangle those factors here. What we can say
is that the structured agents already receive the same underlying sensing content in summarized form, so the
observed gains are not explained by a simple information asymmetry. The difference lies in how the information
is processed: selectively, iteratively, and in response to emerging hypotheses rather than in a single forward pass
over a preformatted summary.


6.2   The Diary Paradox and Graceful Degradation
The diary paradox—the systematic absence of self-report data during periods of greatest clinical need, a well-
documented challenge in EMA research [20, 41]—motivates a fundamental design requirement for proactive
mental health systems: they must function, at least partially, without active user participation. Pulse’s sensing-
only capabilities directly address this requirement.
   The degradation from multimodal to sensing-only is real: mean balanced accuracy across the four focus targets
drops from 0.725 (Auto-Multi) to 0.637 (Auto-Sense), a decline of 0.088. Yet the drop is strongly target-dependent.
INT_availability changes by only 0.010 (0.722 to 0.713), whereas ER_desire falls by 0.092 (0.743 to 0.651). This
pattern suggests that passive sensing may already be sufficient for behavioral availability estimation, while
emotional desire still benefits materially from diary input.
   At the same time, we are cautious about calling this graceful degradation in a deployment sense. Our evidence is
retrospective, and the 9-point mean gap may still matter clinically. Moreover, the same crisis states that suppress
diary completion may also degrade passive sensing coverage if the phone is turned off, left behind, or used
differently. A practical system may therefore be able to fall back to sensing-only reasoning, but whether that
fallback is adequate in real-time care settings remains unvalidated.

6.3   INT_availability as a Behavioral Construct
Perhaps our most conceptually important finding is the clean dissociation between ER_desire and INT_availability.
ER_desire benefits substantially from diary text (Δ = +0.092), while INT_availability shows negligible benefit
(Δ = +0.010). This dissociation is not an artifact of prediction difficulty—both constructs achieve high balanced
accuracy with the right data source—but reflects a fundamental difference in their nature.
   ER_desire captures a psychological state: the user’s subjective desire for emotion regulation support. This state
is directly expressed in diary text (“I feel overwhelmed,” “I need to talk to someone”) and only indirectly reflected
in behavioral signals. INT_availability captures a behavioral state: whether the user is in a context where they
can engage with an intervention (not driving, not in a meeting, phone accessible). This state is directly captured
by sensing streams (motion activity, screen usage, location) and only tangentially related to diary content.
   This dissociation has direct design implications for JITAI systems [29, 38, 42]. Rather than treating receptivity
as a unitary construct, intervention delivery may benefit from modeling desire and availability as separate
tailoring variables estimated from different data sources: passive sensing for behavioral availability and brief
check-ins or diary text for emotional desire when available.
   Two caveats matter here. First, our availability target is a self-reported binary item; it reflects stated availability,
not necessarily true receptivity or eventual intervention engagement. Second, some of the availability signal may
be recoverable from simpler heuristics such as time of day or motion state. We therefore interpret INT_availability
as a promising behavioral construct, not as a solved deployment variable.

6.4   Cross-User RAG as Calibration
The cross-user RAG mechanism in Pulse serves a different purpose than typical RAG in question-answering
systems [4, 30]. Rather than retrieving factual knowledge, it provides empirical calibration: grounding predictions
in the outcomes observed for similar cases. When the agent retrieves peer cases with similar behavior or diary
content, it receives a small empirical reference set for interpreting what it sees.
   We hypothesize that this mechanism may help with the cross-dataset generalization challenge documented
in GLOBEM [76]: instead of learning only a fixed population-specific decision surface, the system can combine
general reasoning with local empirical anchors. Whether this truly improves transfer remains an open question,
but the architecture is at least compatible with population-adaptive calibration.

6.5   Limitations
We acknowledge several limitations that contextualize our findings.
   Retrospective evaluation. Pulse was evaluated in a retrospective replay of existing BUCS data, not in a
prospective deployment. While the temporal boundary enforcement ensures the agent cannot access future
data, real-time deployment introduces additional challenges: latency constraints (30–90 seconds per prediction),
network reliability, model availability, and user interface design. Our results demonstrate proof of concept, not
deployment readiness.
   Sample size. The evaluation includes 50 randomly selected users. While the representativeness analysis
(Section 4.1.1) shows that these users are broadly similar to the full BUCS sample on most measures, 50 users
may not capture the full diversity of behavioral patterns in the cancer survivor population. The within-subject
design and 4,112 predictions per system provide substantial statistical power for the comparative analyses, but
absolute performance levels should be interpreted in the context of this sample.
   Baseline comparability. The broader baseline scoreboard is useful for reader orientation, but it is not a
perfectly fair head-to-head comparison. Several baselines differ from Pulse in training population, feature access,
temporal resolution, or modality richness. The stronger claim is the controlled within-family comparison between
structured and agentic systems on the same 50 users.
                         PULSE: Agentic Investigation with Passive Sensing for Proactive Intervention in Cancer Survivorship   •   0:25


   Model dependency. All experiments used a single LLM (Claude Sonnet). While the agentic investigation
paradigm is model-agnostic in principle—any LLM capable of multi-turn tool use could instantiate it—the specific
performance levels may vary across models. Open-source alternatives such as LLaMA [61] could improve
reproducibility and enable on-device deployment. The MCP tool interface is an open standard, facilitating
cross-model evaluation in future work.
   Inference cost. Each prediction requires 30–90 seconds and approximately $2–5 per user in LLM inference,
making full-cohort evaluation expensive and real-time deployment cost-sensitive. This cost constrained our
evaluation to 50 of 399 available users. For deployment, model distillation, cheaper frontier models, or on-device
inference could reduce costs while preserving the agentic reasoning advantage.
   No memory ablation. We did not ablate the per-user session memory component. While memory provides
longitudinal context that plausibly improves predictions, we cannot quantify its contribution without a controlled
ablation study.
   Continuous calibration and negativity bias. The agentic agents show systematic biases in continuous
predictions (over-predicting negative affect, under-predicting positive affect) despite strong binary classification.
One plausible concern is that the investigation process itself may amplify negativity bias by rewarding anomaly-
seeking in a clinical context. We have not yet tested post-hoc calibration or alternative prompting strategies to
mitigate this.

6.6     Implications for JITAI Design
Our findings suggest four tentative design principles for next-generation JITAI systems, informed by the growing
evidence base for JITAI efficacy [17] and the specific needs of cancer survivor populations [6, 59]:
      (1) Separate desire from availability. Intervention receptivity is not a unitary construct. Systems should
          independently estimate emotional desire for support (diary-aided, when available) and behavioral avail-
          ability for engagement (sensing-based, continuous). Intervention delivery may benefit from including
          evidence for both.
      (2) Use agentic reasoning for state detection. Fixed rule-based triggers and traditional ML models appear
          to operate near the generalization ceiling documented by GLOBEM. Agentic investigation offers a plausible
          path beyond that ceiling by enabling dynamic, personalized, context-sensitive analysis.
      (3) Design for fallback rather than assuming graceful degradation. A JITAI system should provide its
          best estimates when all data sources are available, but it should also retain a sensing-only pathway when
          diary entries are absent. Whether that fallback is clinically sufficient still requires prospective validation.
      (4) Leverage population data for calibration. Cross-user retrieval provides empirical grounding that pure
          within-user models lack. Future JITAI systems should maintain growing databases of behavioral patterns
          linked to outcomes, enabling agents to calibrate their predictions against empirical evidence from similar
          cases.
   Traditional approaches to constructing JITAIs typically involve a multi-step process: conducting observational
studies to identify candidate tailoring variables, decision points, and decision rules, followed by separate micro-
randomized trials to evaluate and refine these components. While rigorous, this fixed-rule framework may limit
the scalability and broader adoption of JITAIs in mental health, as it places a substantial burden on researchers
to predefine intervention components that may not generalize well across populations or contexts. In contrast,
PULSE introduces a more flexible paradigm by enabling agents to query, dynamically interpret data, and make
context-sensitive decisions in real time. Rather than relying solely on pre-specified rules, this approach supports
a more adaptive interplay between the system and incoming data, potentially improving responsiveness and
personalization across diverse users and settings.

6.7    Ethical Considerations
This study was conducted under IRB oversight, and all participants provided informed consent for passive
smartphone sensing and EMA data collection. Data was de-identified before analysis, with participant IDs
replaced by numeric study identifiers.
   The combination of passive sensing data and LLM-based inference raises privacy considerations. Sensing
data, while not containing explicit personal content, can reveal sensitive behavioral patterns (location history,
communication patterns, sleep disruption). As Coravos et al. [14] emphasize, digital biomarkers must be developed
with safety and effectiveness frameworks from the outset. In this study, data was processed in a research context
with appropriate safeguards. Future deployment would require careful attention to data handling practices [50],
user control over data sharing, transparency about how predictions are generated [37], and compliance with
applicable privacy regulations.
   We emphasize that Pulse is a prediction layer, not a clinical intervention or diagnostic system [1, 63]. Its
predictions are intended to inform intervention delivery decisions within a broader JITAI framework, not to replace
clinical assessment. The system should not be the sole determinant of clinical action. Given the asymmetric cost
structure of intervention delivery—an unnecessary check-in has lower cost than missing acute distress—a modest
bias toward caution may be acceptable, but the negativity bias we observe also creates a risk of over-triggering
and should be treated as a safety concern rather than as an automatic virtue.
   The use of LLM APIs for processing behavioral health data raises questions about data transmission and storage.
In the current implementation, data sent to the LLM API is de-identified. Future on-device LLM deployment [54,
70, 80] would eliminate the need for external data transmission entirely.


7     Future Work
Several directions extend this work. A first priority is prospective validation: embedding Pulse within a live
JITAI or microrandomized trial [27] to test whether these predictions improve intervention timing and outcomes
outside retrospective replay. A second is cross-model and on-device evaluation: testing whether the agentic
advantage persists across other LLMs and whether lighter-weight or edge models [54, 70, 80] can preserve enough
of the benefit to support privacy-preserving deployment. A third is better calibration and ablation: isolating
the contributions of session memory, peer retrieval, and individual tools while explicitly addressing negativity
bias in continuous affect prediction. A fourth is generalization beyond cancer survivors: evaluating whether
the same agentic investigation pattern transfers to other sensing domains, populations, and intervention settings.


8     Conclusion
This paper introduces Pulse, a system that shifts passive sensing-based affect prediction from fixed feature
pipelines toward agentic investigation. By equipping LLM agents with eight purpose-built sensing query tools,
Pulse enables hypothesis-driven investigation of behavioral data rather than one-shot reasoning over precom-
puted summaries. The evaluation provides evidence that reasoning architecture matters: agentic investigation
outperforms structured prompting in both sensing-only and multimodal settings, with all main contrasts highly
significant (𝑝 < 0.0001, bootstrap test). The most clinically consequential result is the dissociation between two
intervention-opportunity constructs. Emotion regulation desire benefits substantially from diary text, whereas
intervention availability is already predicted strongly from passive sensing alone, suggesting that desire and
availability should not be treated as a single tailoring variable. The results suggest a useful direction for the field.
Mobile sensing has long established that behavioral signals correlate with mental health. Pulse begins to show
how an agent can investigate those signals in context.
